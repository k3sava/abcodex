Josh Braun's approach inverts the standard sales copy playbook. Most conversion copywriting teaches you to position yourself as the expert who has the answer. Braun teaches the opposite: position yourself as a peer who has a question. The moment you try to convince someone, you activate their resistance. The moment you ask a genuine question, you activate their curiosity.

His equal-positioning framework: communication should feel peer-to-peer, not expert-to-prospect. On landing pages, this translates to copy that explores a problem alongside the reader rather than declaring solutions from above. "We noticed something interesting about teams that switched from manual dialing" is equal-positioning. "Our industry-leading solution will transform your outreach" is expert-positioning. The first invites. The second sells. Invitation converts better.

His cold email framework is a masterclass in brevity and respect: greeting (one line, personal), context (one line, relevant observation about the prospect), question (one genuine question that opens a conversation). No pitch. No feature list. No "I'd love to get 15 minutes on your calendar." The email earns a reply by being genuinely interesting, not by being persuasive.

Applied to landing pages and demos, Braun's key insight is illuminating the cost of inaction through questions, not statements. Instead of "You're losing $340K in pipeline," the Braun approach would be: "What happens to leads that don't get a callback within 5 minutes?" The reader does the math themselves. Their own conclusion is more persuasive than your claim.

Connection to demo methodology: Braun's "stop persuading" philosophy aligns with Orlob's "catalyze a decision, don't educate." Both argue that the buyer should feel like they arrived at their own conclusion. The best demos (and the best landing pages) create the conditions for self-persuasion.
