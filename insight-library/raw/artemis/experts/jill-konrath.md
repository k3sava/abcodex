Jill Konrath is the buyer psychologist of modern sales methodology. Where most sales frameworks are built from the seller's perspective (what should I say, what questions should I ask, how should I present), Konrath builds from the buyer's cognitive and emotional reality. Her central insight is that today's B2B buyer is not just busy but "frazzled": overwhelmed with information, drowning in vendor outreach, defaulting to no-decision under cognitive overload, and requiring sellers to fundamentally reduce the mental effort required to engage, evaluate, and decide. This framing shifts the seller's primary job from persuasion to cognitive burden reduction.

SNAP Selling operationalizes this through four principles that function as design constraints for every sales interaction. Simple (can a busy executive understand this in under 60 seconds?), iNvaluable (would the buyer take my call even if they had no intention of buying?), Aligned (does every message connect to what this person is trying to achieve this quarter?), and Priority (have I shown why this matters now, not eventually?). These principles apply differently across three sequential buyer decisions that Konrath identifies as the actual decision-making architecture: Decision 1: Allow Access (will I grant this seller my time?), Decision 2: Initiate Change (is the problem bad enough to justify the disruption of changing?), Decision 3: Select Resources (which solution and vendor best reduce my risk?). Each decision requires fundamentally different seller behavior. A demo-centric approach fails at Decision 1 (no one wants a demo from someone they have not decided to engage with). A discovery-heavy approach may fail at Decision 1 if it imposes cognitive load before earning the right. A feature-focused presentation may fail at Decision 2 because it does not address whether change itself is worth the effort.

Konrath's Buyer's Matrix is a pre-engagement research tool that maps each persona's role, objectives, problems, trigger events, status quo, and decision-making process, enabling sellers to enter conversations with hypotheses rather than blank-slate questions. Her Agile Selling methodology provides a rapid learning framework for sellers entering new markets or companies: identify minimum viable knowledge, chunk and sequence learning, apply immediately, and reflect iteratively, with a 30-day sprint structure for onboarding. Konrath's framework addresses the same "no decision" problem as Matt Dixon's JOLT Effect but from the prevention side rather than the treatment side. Dixon provides techniques for overcoming indecision once it has formed (JOLT: Judge, Offer recommendation, Limit exploration, Take risk off table). Konrath provides principles for preventing indecision from forming in the first place by reducing cognitive burden throughout the engagement. Together, they form a complete system for addressing what is now the largest source of lost deals in B2B sales.
