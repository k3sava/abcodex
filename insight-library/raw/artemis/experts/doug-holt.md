Douglas Holt is the originator of Cultural Branding Theory and the Cultural Strategy model, the most rigorous academic-turned-practitioner framework for understanding how the world's strongest brands are built. His central argument, developed through detailed historical analysis of dozens of iconic brands (Nike, Harley-Davidson, Apple, Jack Daniel's, Volkswagen, Coca-Cola), is that conventional branding models are fundamentally wrong about how iconic brands work. The conventional model assumes brands are built through distinctive benefits, brand personalities, and emotional relationships. Holt demonstrates that the strongest brands instead succeed by forging deep connections with culture. They compete not for market share but for "culture share," creating identity myths that soothe collective anxieties arising from acute social change. His framework rests on seven axioms: (1) Iconic brands address acute contradictions in society; (2) They develop identity myths that address collective desires and anxieties; (3) These myths reside in the brand and are experienced via ritual action; (4) The myths are set in populist worlds, drawing from the margins rather than the elite; (5) Iconic brands perform as activists, leading culture; (6) They rely on breakthrough performances rather than consistent communications; (7) They enjoy a cultural halo effect where identity value enhances perceived quality, benefits, and status.

The operational version of this theory is the Cultural Strategy model, which Holt developed through hundreds of consulting projects after leaving academia. The model works by identifying "cultural orthodoxies" (the stale conventions that dominate a category), detecting "ideological opportunities" created by historical social changes that render the orthodoxy inadequate, and then crafting a new cultural expression (ideology + myth + cultural codes) that taps into subcultural source materials to address the emerging opportunity. The model reverses the standard innovation logic: instead of starting with technology or product features, it starts with cultural transformation. Holt's case studies demonstrate this consistently. Patagonia had become a victim of its own success as every outdoor brand copied its subcultural aesthetic; his cultural analysis unearthed a new approach that drove 30% sales growth in year one. REI was losing under-40s and bleeding to Amazon; his strategy repositioned the co-op around John Muir's vision of public land activism, leading to the award-winning #OptOutside campaign and 30%+ growth in membership and revenue. For Coca-Cola, he showed the company how to revitalize the ideology that made the brand iconic in the 1970s.

In his influential 2016 HBR article "Branding in the Age of Social Media," Holt extended the framework to explain why branded content strategies have failed. He argues that social media has given rise to "crowdcultures," densely networked communities that have become prolific cultural innovators whose content quality surpasses what brands can produce. Rather than trying to compete with crowdcultures through branded content (which consumers dismiss as clutter), brands should use cultural branding to champion ideologies that spring from these crowdcultures. Chipotle's anti-industrial-food films are the exemplar: the brand tapped into a crowdculture that began in the organic-farming subculture, championed its ideology at scale, and catapulted brand equity. His 2020 HBR article on "Cultural Innovation" extends the framework further, arguing that breakthrough businesses are built not just through technological disruption but through cultural disruption: challenging everyday notions of value by introducing innovative ideologies rather than just innovative features.
