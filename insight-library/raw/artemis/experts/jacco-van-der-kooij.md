Jacco van der Kooij is the architect of the most comprehensive systems-level framework for recurring revenue businesses. His central thesis is that SaaS and subscription companies have fundamentally outgrown the traditional sales funnel. In a perpetual license model, 60% of total revenue is captured at the initial sale; in a recurring model, only 18% is captured at the initial commitment, with 82% coming from renewals and expansion over subsequent years. This single observation drives his entire body of work: if most value is created after the initial sale, the entire go-to-market system must be redesigned to optimize for the full customer lifecycle, not just acquisition.

His two flagship frameworks operationalize this insight. The Bowtie Model replaces the linear sales funnel with a symmetrical shape where the "knot" represents the initial commitment, the left side covers Awareness, Education, and Selection (acquisition), and the right side covers Onboarding, Impact, and Expansion (retention and growth). The right side creates a compound growth loop, where customers achieving recurring impact drive expansion and advocacy. The model originated in the travel industry (2009) for modeling ancillary revenue streams and was adapted to SaaS, where post-sale stages like achieving first impact, recurring impact, and expanding impact are explicitly modeled. The Bowtie serves as both a customer journey map and a standardized data model that aligns Marketing, Sales, and Customer Success around shared metrics and a common language.

The SPICED framework is van der Kooij's diagnostic qualification methodology, designed specifically for recurring revenue businesses. SPICED stands for Situation, Pain, Impact, Critical Event, and Decision criteria. It replaces older qualification frameworks like BANT (designed for IBM mainframe sales in the 1960s) and even MEDDIC, which van der Kooij acknowledges as the closest precursor due to its inclusion of impact as a qualifier. The key differentiator of SPICED is that it applies across the entire Bowtie, not just at the point of sale. Customer Success uses SPICED during onboarding and renewal conversations, not just sales during qualification. The framework's central qualifier is impact: can the seller help the client achieve the impact in the timeframe they need, and will that impact result in profit for the seller? Van der Kooij distinguishes between rational impact (measurable, benefits the corporation) and emotional impact (experiential, benefits the individual), arguing that sellers must identify which type matters most to each stakeholder because emotional decisions are validated with rational data, not the other way around.

Beyond these two frameworks, van der Kooij introduces six interconnected models that form the foundation of Revenue Architecture: the Revenue Model (subscription vs. consumption), the Data Model (Bowtie), the Mathematical Model (exponential and compound math replacing linear funnel math), the Go-To-Market Model (matching motion to ACV and deal volume), the Growth Model (achieving GTM Fit before scaling), and the Operating Model (one unified methodology across all customer-facing roles). His "Revenue Factory" metaphor reframes the GTM organization as a production system with distinct production lines (GTM motions), each with its own cost and revenue profile. He explicitly warns against "scaling failure," where companies scale headcount before achieving GTM Fit, leading to unsustainable cost structures. His work represents the most rigorous attempt to bring engineering-level systems thinking to revenue operations, treating growth as a design problem rather than a hiring problem.
