Emily Kramer's positioning philosophy is built on a single, sharp critique: most positioning exercises take companies too deep into the weeds and never produce a clear, usable answer. She uses the analogy of long division — the research is the work, but positioning is the answer. Companies focus too much on showing the work (detailed research decks, 30-slide positioning documents) instead of producing the right answer (a crisp statement of who the product is for, what it is, and why it is better). This leads to the paradoxical outcome where companies that run thorough positioning exercises still lack clarity on their differentiation.

Kramer's MKT1 positioning process is structured into four distinct phases that must be kept separate: (1) Product marketing research (audience, market, and product research), (2) Product type and comparator selection, (3) Positioning statement (filling in three blanks: who is it for, what is it, why is it better), and (4) Messaging (adapting positioning to audiences, funnel stages, and channels). The most distinctive contribution is Step 2 — the product type and comparator framework. Kramer identifies four product types (10x better, vertical solution, new way, buy vs. build), each with a specific comparator you position against. She argues this is the most skipped step in positioning and the one that causes the most downstream damage: if you position against the wrong comparator to the wrong audience, nothing else works.

Kramer also provides critical guidance on "new way" positioning, arguing it is the hardest of all four types. Her key insight is that early competitors in an emerging category help you grow rather than hurt you, and the real challenge is making your audience problem-aware and solution-aware — not fighting other new entrants. She recommends positioning "new way" products against using "nothing" or the "old way" rather than against emerging competitors. She also draws a firm hierarchy: product positioning is a subset of brand story, not the same thing, and messaging is the downstream application of positioning to specific audiences and channels.
