Karl Sakas's system of thought centers on a single observation drawn from advising 600+ agencies: most agency problems are leadership problems disguised as operational ones. The owner is too deep in client work to think strategically, the team lacks clear roles and authority, and every decision flows through a single bottleneck. Sakas calls this "agency chaos" and argues it is not a symptom of growth but a failure to build an operating system before growth happens. His framework, codified in "Calm the Chaos," provides a 10-part system for agency leaders to extract themselves from daily operations without the business collapsing.

Sakas's core diagnostic framework evaluates agencies across three dimensions: desire (does the owner actually want to lead and manage?), competence (do they have the skills to delegate and develop people?), and capacity (do they have the bandwidth to step back from delivery?). When all three are present, the owner can build a leadership team and scale. When any one is missing, a different intervention is required -- coaching for competence gaps, restructuring for capacity constraints, or an honest conversation about exit if the desire is absent. His delegation framework borrows from Michael Hyatt's five levels: at the lowest level, a team member researches and reports back; at the highest, they act autonomously and the owner does not even need to know. Sakas's insight is that most agency owners operate at levels one and two when they need to be at four and five, and the reason they do not delegate higher is not trust but the absence of documented processes, clear decision-making authority, and accountability structures.

On agency growth, Sakas is notably not an advocate for growth at all costs. He distinguishes between scaling (adding revenue and team in a sustainable way) and just getting bigger (adding headcount and complexity without corresponding profitability). His recommendation is that many agencies would be better off staying at their current size but running more efficiently -- a view that connects directly to Paul Jarvis's Company of One philosophy and David C. Baker's observation that some of the most profitable agencies are 5-16 people. Sakas also works extensively on exit planning and succession, providing a practical bridge between the "build to run" mentality of most agency owners and the "build to sell" mentality that Baker advocates. The cross-disciplinary connection to Marcel Petitpas is operational: while Petitpas focuses on the financial metrics of agency profitability (delivery margin, ABR, utilization), Sakas focuses on the organizational design and leadership systems that make those metrics achievable. Together, they cover the full stack of agency operations -- Petitpas as the financial architect and Sakas as the organizational architect.
