Andrew Faris's approach to ecommerce growth is defined by a single, contrarian operating principle: nearly 100% of Meta ad spend should use cost controls (cost caps, bid caps, or minimum ROAS), not the lowest-cost delivery that most advertisers default to. His argument is both philosophical and mechanical. Philosophically, cost controls solve the most expensive problem in DTC advertising: creative testing. Without cost controls, media buyers evaluate new creative by setting up test campaigns and analyzing results over days or weeks, which Faris calls "extraordinarily flawed" because you are paying full price to learn that most ads do not work. With cost controls, you launch every new piece of creative in the same campaigns as evergreen content using bid caps, and Meta automatically suppresses spend on likely losers while scaling spend on likely winners. The result is that you can launch limitless new creative into an account and trust the algorithm to self-select for quality, transforming creative testing from a manual, expensive process into an automated, capital-efficient one.

Faris frames the scaling journey in two stages, using a volleyball analogy: media buying is the setter (necessary infrastructure) and creative is the hitter (the spike that scores the point). Stage 1 is finding the creative messaging that resonates with customers, which is a creative problem, not a media buying problem. Stage 2 is scaling that messaging into a never-ending stream of highly diverse creative assets, which is an operational challenge. Most brands stall between these stages because they conflate them: they try to scale media spend before they have found winning creative, or they find winning creative but cannot produce enough variations to sustain scale. His diagnostic for Stage 1 is whether you have found at least two to three creative concepts that can profitably spend $1K+/day each. If not, the bottleneck is creative, not budget. His diagnostic for Stage 2 is whether you can produce five or more net-new creative assets per week without quality degradation. If not, the bottleneck is creative operations.

Faris's preferred client profile is the entrepreneur-founder running an ecommerce brand between $1M and $20M, a stage he considers the most exciting because you can take giant bites fast and see huge impact. His coaching extends beyond media buying into financial infrastructure: cohort forecasting, P&L modeling, cashflow management, and understanding the unit economics that determine whether growth is profitable. He advocates directness in advertising as a first principle: say what your product does, who it is for, and why someone should buy it, rather than being clever, abstract, or brand-atmospheric. This connects to Peep Laja's clarity-first messaging layer and Tas Bober's anti-jargon stance: across B2B and DTC, the practitioners who actually drive conversion converge on the principle that clear, direct communication outperforms sophisticated messaging.
