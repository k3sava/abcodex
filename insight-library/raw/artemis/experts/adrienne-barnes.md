Adrienne Barnes's work is built on a damning statistic she uncovered through her own research: 85% of companies create buyer personas, but 77% never use them. Her diagnosis is that traditional buyer personas have become a "check the box" marketing practice because they are built on demographic scaffolding (Mary Marketer, 28, lives in this city, has a cat, wants to be Wonder Woman, loves ramen noodles) that gives a content marketer zero actionable information for creating content, developing products, or supporting customers. Her prescription is to rebuild persona methodology from the ground up using the Jobs to Be Done framework, which segments buyers by what they are trying to accomplish rather than who they are demographically. Using JTBD segmentation, Barnes has never seen a company require more than three to five personas, compared to the ten or more that demographic approaches typically produce.

Barnes's operational methodology centers on qualitative customer interviews conducted with rigorous technique. She invested years studying interview methodology from social workers, journalists, and product researchers (citing Michelle Hansen's "Deploy Empathy" as a key influence). Her interview technique has several distinctive elements: warm introductions (she always has the client introduce her so she is not coming in cold), empathetic probing ("wow, that sounds like that was hard, would you mind sharing that experience more?"), the "tell me a little bit more about that" question which she considers the most powerful in any interviewer's toolkit, and always ending with "is there anything else I should know?" which consistently produces the greatest insights and most concise language. She extracts four types of output from interviews: frustrations (which become how-to content), aspirations and delight (which become case studies and testimonials), relational keywords (the exact words customers use for your product, which should replace your internal terminology in all copy and SEO), and content consumption patterns (how customers actually use your content, which often reveals that they do not know your blog exists).

Her analytical process for converting interview data into actionable personas uses word clouds as the primary synthesis tool. She groups interview responses under the topic each question was designed to probe, feeds the responses into word clouds, and the resulting frequency patterns reveal the language customers use most and the words that most define their problems. The persona output is structured so that every line of information informs something specific: product development decisions, customer success call scripts, content strategy topics, or sales messaging. If a piece of persona information cannot be traced to a business action, it does not belong in the persona. This "every line must inform something" principle is what separates Barnes's methodology from traditional persona development and connects directly to Emily Kramer's fuel and engine framework: the persona is fuel, but it must be designed to power a specific engine (content, product, sales, support).
