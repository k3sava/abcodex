Chris Do's system of thought is built on a single reframe that he delivers to creative professionals: stop selling your skills and start serving your clients' business outcomes. His formative experience running Blind, a motion design studio in Los Angeles, taught him that the creative industry's default model -- trading hours for dollars, competing on portfolio quality, and undercharging for strategy -- is a trap that keeps talented designers poor. Do's central thesis is that value is not determined by the creator but by the client, and the only way to discover value is through disciplined questioning. His mantra "amateurs give advice, experts diagnose" encapsulates the workflow shift he teaches: before proposing any creative solution, the practitioner must conduct a diagnostic conversation that uncovers the client's business goals, success metrics, and the economic value at stake.

Do's pricing framework identifies three tiers that creative professionals move through: time-based pricing (hourly rates, the default for beginners), project-based pricing (flat fees scoped to deliverables, a step up but still cost-anchored), and value-based pricing (fees tied to the business outcome the work creates, the expert tier). The transition between tiers is not primarily tactical but psychological -- it requires the practitioner to stop identifying as a maker of artifacts (logos, websites, videos) and start identifying as a solver of business problems. Do's practical workflow follows a sequence: (1) ask what the client is trying to achieve, (2) investigate the gap between their current state and desired state, (3) determine the economic value of closing that gap, (4) propose a solution at a price that represents a fraction of the value created. He insists that whoever asks the most questions controls the conversation, and that the entire sales interaction should feel like a consultation, not a pitch. His signature phrase "serve, don't sell" captures this: when the client feels served, the sale happens naturally.

The Futur's educational model itself demonstrates Do's cross-disciplinary thinking. He took a successful but non-scalable agency model (Blind) and layered an education business on top of it, effectively productizing his hard-won expertise into courses, communities, and content. This connects directly to David C. Baker's "55 Topics" technique and Jonathan Stark's productized consulting framework -- Do built a media company around the expertise he accumulated as a practitioner. His pricing philosophy also intersects with Blair Enns's work: both advocate pricing the client, not the job, and both use diagnostic conversations to surface value. The key distinction is audience: Enns speaks primarily to agency principals managing teams and multi-million-dollar pipelines, while Do speaks to solo creatives and small studio owners who may be charging $50/hour and need to hear the value-pricing message in a more accessible, visual, and emotionally resonant format.
