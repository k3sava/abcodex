Peep Laja's career represents a unique arc from conversion optimization practitioner to B2B messaging strategist, and the connection between these two domains defines his distinctive contribution. At CXL, he built the definitive body of work on evidence-based conversion optimization: running experiments, measuring results, and codifying what actually moves conversion rates. This empirical orientation carried directly into Wynter, where he applied the same test-and-measure discipline to B2B messaging. His central argument is that most B2B companies suffer from "sameness," the combined effect of being too similar in their offers, poorly differentiated in their branding, and indistinct in their communication. He identifies three root causes: benchmarking (constant comparison leads to conformity), customer research (which produces category-level insights that every competitor also acts on), and best practices (which by definition are what everyone else is already doing). His prescription is not to be "better" but to be "different," because being objectively better than the competition is nearly impossible in crowded markets.

Laja's most actionable framework is the Messaging Layers model, developed from thousands of message tests at Wynter. Unlike the traditional messaging hierarchy pyramid (brand promise at the top, proof points at the base), Laja's model is structured as concentric layers, each of which must succeed before the next one matters. The layers, from outermost to innermost, are: Clarity (what is it?), Relevance (is it aligned with customers' priorities and pains?), Value (how bad do your customers want it?), Differentiation (why you?), and Conversion/Consideration (yes, you!). The critical insight is the ordering: clarity is the pinnacle, not differentiation. It does not matter how well you differentiate from competitors if you cannot clearly communicate what you do. This directly challenges the positioning-first orthodoxy of practitioners like April Dunford, who emphasize competitive differentiation as the starting point. Laja argues that differentiation without clarity is wasted effort, and his data from message testing shows that most B2B websites fail at the clarity layer before they even get a chance to differentiate.

His operational process for building a messaging framework starts with understanding the strategic narrative (the big-picture story of where the market is going and why your company exists), then moves to positioning (your place in the competitive landscape), then differentiation (what makes you distinct), and finally messaging (the specific words and phrases used across channels). He insists these are four separate but interwoven elements, not synonyms, and that "the story is the strategy," citing Ben Horowitz's observation that companies without a clearly articulated story do not have a clear strategy. For testing, he advocates running message tests with panels of your target buyers (Wynter's core product), comparing your messaging head-to-head against competitors', and iterating based on comprehension and preference data rather than internal opinion. This empirical approach to messaging connects to Adrienne Barnes's customer research methodology and Emily Kramer's emphasis on audience understanding as the foundation of positioning.
