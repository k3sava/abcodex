Tim Soulo is the strategic mind behind Ahrefs' content-led growth engine, which has become a case study in how a SaaS company can use SEO and content marketing to dominate its category. His core philosophy is built on a deceptively simple principle: the only goal of your content should be to promote your product, but this works only when your product genuinely solves problems and your promotion carries integrity. He operationalized this through a methodology he calls the "90/10 Content Strategy," where 90% of content is driven by keyword research targeting queries with organic search traffic potential and clear business relevance, while 10% is dedicated to thought leadership that builds authority and credibility on topics nobody is searching for yet but that offer unique insights.

Soulo's keyword research methodology evaluates content opportunities on three dimensions: search volume and traffic potential (measured not by individual keyword volume but by total traffic to top-ranking pages across all keywords they rank for), ranking difficulty (assessed through the number of referring domains needed to reach the top 10, not a generic difficulty score), and business potential (how naturally the product can be showcased while addressing the topic). He explicitly rejects vanity traffic metrics and insists that every piece of content must allow the product to be demonstrated as a solution. His framework for content creation centers on what he calls the "content playground" concept, derived from the principle that "the first time people use your product is in their heads." Ahrefs content is packed with interface screenshots, step-by-step walkthroughs, and enough specificity that readers can mentally simulate using the tool before they sign up.

What sets Soulo apart from most content marketing leaders is his insistence that ideas matter more than word count, and expertise cannot be outsourced. At Ahrefs, he requires content creators to be product experts who can independently discover use cases and strategies, not writers who execute briefs from strategists. He himself continues to write articles and create videos as CMO, modeling the principle that senior team members produce the most valuable content. His view on link building aligns with this philosophy: rather than pursuing tactical link-building campaigns, he argues that credible experts with a body of work naturally attract links because other creators reference authoritative sources. He acknowledges that building this level of authority takes years, noting that "sometimes you have to spend years becoming worthy of ranking for a certain phrase," which makes SEO fundamentally a long-term investment in genuine expertise rather than a short-term optimization exercise.
