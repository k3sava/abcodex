Marcus Andrews is the practitioner who formalized narrative design as a product marketing discipline, adapting principles from video game storytelling into a structured framework for SaaS companies. His intellectual contribution is the argument that product positioning -- while necessary -- is insufficient for market differentiation in crowded categories. Positioning tells customers where your product fits; a narrative tells them why the world is changing and why that change demands a new approach. His defining case study is HubSpot's transition from "inbound marketing platform" to the Flywheel model, where he helped craft a narrative that repositioned the entire CRM category around the idea that funnels were broken and customer-centric growth required a fundamentally different operating model. This narrative did not describe HubSpot's features; it described a worldview shift that made HubSpot's features inevitable.

Andrews' narrative design process follows a specific workflow. First, identify the "big shift" -- the macro-level change in the market or customer behavior that creates urgency. This is not a product trend but a structural change (e.g., buyers now do 70% of research before talking to sales; therefore, the funnel is broken). Second, articulate the "old way vs. new way" -- what the world looked like before the shift and what it must look like after. This creates tension that demands resolution. Third, define the "promised land" -- the specific, tangible outcome that the new way delivers, stated in customer terms rather than product terms. Fourth, connect your product as the bridge from old way to promised land, but only after the narrative has established the urgency and the vision. The sequencing matters: if you lead with the product, you are pitching features; if you lead with the narrative, you are creating demand. Andrews predicts that narrative design could potentially replace positioning as the primary strategic framework for product marketers, arguing that in mature categories where products are functionally similar, the narrative -- not the feature set -- is the primary differentiator.

His enterprise PMM philosophy is built on launch cadence discipline. At HubSpot, he established that launches must have substance and that cadence should be dictated by product readiness, not marketing calendar pressure. He argues that companies attempting more than one major launch per month dilute impact and exhaust sales enablement capacity. For enterprise launches, he emphasizes that the narrative must work at multiple levels: the executive sponsor needs the strategic vision (the big shift), the champion needs the use-case story (the promised land for their team), and the end user needs the feature walkthrough (how it actually works). This multi-layered deployment connects to Lauchengco's Storyteller fundamental (narrative as the core PMM capability), to Dunford's positioning (narrative extends positioning with a temporal dimension -- the before/after arc), and to Pierri's homepage methodology (the homepage should lead with narrative, not feature lists).
