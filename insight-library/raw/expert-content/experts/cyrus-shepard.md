Cyrus Shepard occupies a unique position in the SEO world: he is one of the few prominent SEO practitioners who has actually worked as a Google Search Quality Rater, evaluating search results according to Google's 170-page Quality Rater Guidelines. This insider experience, combined with his background as Moz's Chief SEO Strategist, gives him a distinctive perspective that bridges the gap between how SEOs theorize about Google's quality signals and how Google actually instructs humans to evaluate them. As a quality rater, he personally scored thousands of websites, learning that E-E-A-T (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness) is not a direct ranking factor but rather a framework that helps raters distinguish reliable content from misleading content. Google uses indirect signals like domain reputation, author clarity, and source quality to approximate what raters would assess manually.

His research methodology is data-driven at scale. His title tag study analyzed 81,000 title tags across 2,000+ sites and found that Google rewrites 61% of page title tags, meaning the title you write is often not the title Google shows in search results. The practical implication is that SEOs need to understand Google's rewriting patterns (it tends to shorten long titles, add brand names, and replace keyword-stuffed titles with more natural alternatives) and write titles that are less likely to be rewritten. His internal linking study examined 23 million internal links across 1,800 websites and 520,000 URLs, correlating link patterns with Google Search Console click data. The key finding was that anchor text variety, not raw link count, was the primary driver of traffic improvement. Sites with greater diversity of anchor text pointing to a page consistently outperformed sites with many identical-anchor internal links. He also found that pages with more than 40-50 internal links typically had sitewide navigation links, which provide diminishing SEO value.

Shepard's Google Page Quality Rating Scorecard translates Google's complex quality rater guidelines into an actionable assessment tool that SEOs can use to evaluate their own pages. The scorecard is organized into categories that mirror the rater guidelines: page purpose and content quality, E-E-A-T signals, website reputation, and potential for harm (YMYL considerations). His cross-disciplinary contribution lies in connecting three usually separate SEO disciplines: technical optimization (how pages are structured and linked), content quality assessment (how Google evaluates whether content is trustworthy and useful), and measurement (what signals actually correlate with ranking improvements). For content strategists, his work provides the empirical evidence to justify investments in author attribution, source citation, and content comprehensiveness rather than keyword density and link quantity.
