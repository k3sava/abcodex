Alex Hormozi's system of thought is built on a hierarchy of business levers: Starving Crowd (market selection) matters more than Offer Strength, which matters more than Persuasion Skills. Most entrepreneurs obsess over the bottom of this hierarchy (better ads, better sales scripts, better funnels) when the highest-ROI intervention is almost always improving the offer itself or selecting a better market. A "Grand Slam Offer" in Hormozi's framework is one so compelling that the customer feels stupid saying no -- not because the price is low, but because the perceived value is so disproportionately high that the price becomes irrelevant. This is the same principle that Blair Enns describes as "pricing the client, not the service," but Hormozi operationalizes it with a specific equation and a step-by-step construction process.

The Value Equation is the diagnostic core of Hormozi's framework: Value = (Dream Outcome x Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) / (Time Delay x Effort & Sacrifice). Each variable maps to a specific lever the entrepreneur can pull. To increase value, increase the numerator (make the dream outcome bigger, make the likelihood of achieving it more credible through proof and guarantees) and decrease the denominator (reduce the time to results, reduce the effort required from the customer). The Grand Slam Offer creation workflow follows seven steps: (1) identify the customer's dream outcome in their own words, (2) list every obstacle between their current state and that outcome, (3) convert each obstacle into a named solution, (4) choose delivery vehicles for each solution (done-for-you, done-with-you, do-it-yourself), (5) trim low-value solutions and stack high-value ones to achieve a minimum 10:1 value-to-price ratio, (6) add enhancers -- scarcity (limited quantity), urgency (limited time), bonuses (high-perceived-value additions), and guarantees (risk reversal), (7) name the offer using a result-timeframe-container formula.

The cross-disciplinary connections from Hormozi's work to the agency and consulting world are direct. His delivery vehicle selection framework (DFY/DWY/DIY) maps precisely to David C. Baker's "thinking vs. doing" distinction and Pia Silva's intensives model: the highest-value component should be done-for-you (strategy, which is the thinking), while supporting components can be done-with-you (coaching, reviews) or do-it-yourself (templates, frameworks, recordings). This allows agencies to productize their offer without commoditizing their expertise. Hormozi's Value Equation also provides a practical framework for the "value conversation" that Blair Enns advocates: instead of asking the client an abstract question about the value at stake, the consultant can systematically work through each quadrant of the equation to surface what matters most. His premium pricing philosophy -- higher prices create better clients who get better results, which creates better case studies, which attracts better clients at higher prices -- is the virtuous cycle version of what Baker and Enns describe as the expertise flywheel. The key distinction is scale: Hormozi's frameworks are designed for businesses that want to grow aggressively (his portfolio does $200M+ annually), while Baker, Enns, and Stark optimize for the smaller, more profitable, more selective firm. Both paths use the same underlying principles; the difference is in the appetite for growth.
