Naval Ravikant's intellectual contribution is a unified framework for individual wealth creation built on four interlocking variables: specific knowledge, accountability, leverage, and judgment. Unlike most business thinkers who focus on tactics, Naval operates at the level of principles and personal operating systems. His central formula is: Outcome = Specific Knowledge x Leverage x Judgment x Accountability, compounded over time. Each variable is necessary; none is sufficient alone.

Specific knowledge is the cornerstone of the framework. Naval defines it as knowledge that cannot be mass-trained: if society can train someone to replace you, it will. Specific knowledge is discovered by pursuing genuine curiosity, not by chasing whatever field is currently hot. It often lives at the intersection of multiple disciplines, feels like play to the person who has it but looks like work to everyone else, and is typically observed by others before the person recognizes it in themselves. The critical implication for agency founders is that positioning your services around specific knowledge (not generalist skills) creates pricing power and defensibility.

Leverage is the force multiplier. Naval identifies three forms: labor (oldest, worst: managing people is messy), capital (powerful but permissioned: someone must give you money), and products with no marginal cost of replication, which means code and media (newest, most democratic, permissionless). His key insight is that the new leverage, code and media, does not require anyone's permission to deploy. A solo operator with a podcast, a codebase, or a content library can reach millions without hiring or fundraising. This makes the division "leveraged vs. unleveraged" more important than "rich vs. poor" or "white-collar vs. blue-collar."

Judgment is the decisive skill that makes leverage valuable. Naval defines it as knowing the long-term consequences of your actions. Judgment requires real-world experience with skin in the game; intellect without accountability is "often worse than useless." The best judges are among the least emotional, because emotions prevent you from seeing what is actually happening. Judgment compounds: a person who is right 90% of the time operating with 1,000x leverage will earn hundreds of times more than someone right 80% of the time. This is why Naval advises spending the first part of your career acquiring leverage and the second part slowing down to apply judgment carefully, since at scale, the cost of a wrong decision dwarfs the cost of a slow one.
