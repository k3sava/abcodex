Dave Harland (The Word Man) builds his practice on a single conviction: the brands that stand out are the ones that sound like nobody else. In a market where most B2B copy is interchangeable, distinctive voice is not a luxury. It is the primary conversion mechanism. AI cannot replicate a specific person's voice, which makes authentic voice the ultimate anti-AI signal.

His first principle is radical specificity about voice: "Write like YOU speak. Not how people generally speak, but how you specifically speak." This goes beyond the standard advice to "write conversationally." Harland means that your specific verbal tics, rhythms, analogies, and odd references are assets, not liabilities. Dollar Shave Club's founding copy worked because it sounded like one specific guy who found the razor industry absurd. Wistia's product copy worked because it sounded like one specific team of nerds who loved video. The voice was inseparable from the brand.

His benefits-over-pride rule cuts to the heart of most B2B copy failures: "People don't want to know how proud you are of your company. They want to know how you'll change their life." Every sentence that begins with "We are proud to..." or "Our award-winning..." is a sentence about the company, not the reader. Delete and rewrite from the reader's perspective.

Harland uses humor as a weapon, not decoration. The humor must serve the argument. Dollar Shave Club's Roger Federer joke was not just funny. It reframed the entire economics of razors in one sentence. The test for whether humor earns its place: remove the joke and check if the argument weakens. If the argument is unchanged, the humor is decoration. If the argument loses force, the humor is doing persuasive work.

His "make your claim scream a little" principle addresses the rhythm of persuasive pages. Not every sentence should scream. Most should be clear and direct. But once per section, one sentence should make the reader stop. The scream is what gets remembered. The quiet sentences are what make the scream possible.

Practical process: write the draft, walk away, come back. Harland insists that critical thinking cannot be rushed. Ideas need to simmer. The second look always finds what the first missed.
