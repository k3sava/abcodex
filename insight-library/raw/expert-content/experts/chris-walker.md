Chris Walker's central thesis is that most B2B marketing organizations are fundamentally misallocating their resources by over-indexing on demand capture (reaching the ~5% of buyers actively in market) while neglecting demand creation (educating and building preference with the ~95% who are not yet buying). He argues this misallocation stems from a structural incentive problem: attribution software and MQL-based KPIs reward marketers for capturing existing demand through trackable channels like Google Ads, gated content, and content syndication, while punishing investment in the untrackable channels where buyers actually discover and evaluate products. His prescription is to separate demand creation and demand capture into distinct strategies with different measurement mechanisms, rather than forcing both through a single attribution model.

Walker's most distinctive framework is the Split the Funnel analysis, which separates pipeline into two sources: Declared Intent (demo requests, contact sales forms) and Low Intent (ebook downloads, webinar attendees, trade show badge scans, intent data signals). By calculating sales velocity, win rates, and revenue per dollar spent across these two sources independently, companies can identify exactly how much budget is being wasted on low-intent lead gen that never converts. He pairs this with the HIRO (High Intent Revenue Opportunity) Pipeline metric, which standardizes pipeline measurement across companies by requiring that leads come through a high-intent source with win rates above 3% and reach a deal stage converting at 25% or higher. Across a cohort of 20 B2B SaaS clients, Refine Labs achieved a median 76% increase in HIRO Pipeline.

Walker champions a hybrid attribution model that combines software-based tracking with customer-reported attribution (a simple open text field asking "How did you hear about us?"). He cites that Passetto attributed 97% of their revenue to dark social using customer-reported attribution, while their software-based attribution showed 0% from those channels. His five critical marketing metrics are: total HIRO Pipeline creation, marketing spend to HIRO Pipeline ratio, sales velocity, closed-won new business, and closed-won to total marketing spend ratio. He is also a proponent of combining marketing sub-disciplines (brand, demand, account-based, content, advertising) into one integrated motion rather than running them as separate silos, and he advocates using social platforms like Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn, and podcasts for demand creation through native educational content rather than lead gen forms.
