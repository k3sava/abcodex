Blair Enns has spent over two decades teaching creative professionals to win business at higher prices without giving away their thinking in pitches. His body of work centers on a fundamental reframe: the sale of expertise should be a series of conversations between two discerning professionals trying to determine mutual fit, not a performance where the agency convinces the buyer. His "Four Conversations" model structures the entire sales arc into the Probative Conversation (initial outreach), the Qualifying Conversation (mutual fit assessment), the Value Conversation (uncovering what the engagement is worth), and the Closing Conversation (presenting options and reaching agreement). Each conversation has a specific objective and framework, and nowhere in the arc does the firm go into "presentation mode." Enns explicitly trains firms to replace presentations with conversations, arguing that you can present to people or be present to them, but you cannot do both.

Enns's pricing framework represents a direct challenge to the agency industry's default of billing hours. He identifies three levels of financial success: Level One is the Efficient Firm, which maximizes billable utilization and might reach $200k in AGI per FTE; Level Two is the Value-Curious Firm, which prices based on perceived value and can push past $250k AGI/FTE; Level Three is the Entrepreneur, who thinks not about what to sell but about how much value to create, with no theoretical ceiling on earnings. The transition between levels requires not just tactical changes but a profound mindset shift. His "Anchor High" principle, drawn from Kahneman and Tversky's anchoring effect research, is a core pricing tactic: always start with the most expensive option in a multi-option proposal, because the first price sets the context for the decision. His concrete advice is to throw out big numbers even in the Value Conversation, before any proposal is written.

Central to Enns's methodology is the concept of pricing the client, not the service. A logo designed for one company may be worth $10k while the same work for another is worth $100k, because the difference is $90k worth of reassurance that business goals will be met. He frames premium pricing through an insurance lens: a premium in marketing is the same as a premium in insurance, and the amount above the benchmark price represents the client's willingness to pay for reduced risk. His practical framework for the value conversation follows four steps: (1) confirm the client's desired future state, (2) agree on metrics of success, (3) uncover the value that would be created, and (4) offer pricing guidance as a range. He also shares a "hack" for those who struggle with the value conversation: put an early one-page, three-option proposal on the table not as the final proposal but as a catalyst for discussion, with each option tagged "Choose this option if X is important to you" to reveal what the client values most. Enns advocates publishing minimum levels of engagement on your website as a positioning signal (demonstrating exclusivity, not affordability) while avoiding fixed package pricing, which removes the ability to practice price discrimination.
