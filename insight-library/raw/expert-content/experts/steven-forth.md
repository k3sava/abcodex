Steven Forth brings the most rigorous and systematic approach to value-based pricing in SaaS. Where Patrick Campbell starts with willingness-to-pay surveys and Madhavan Ramanujam starts with direct customer price conversations, Forth starts with value modeling, building a system of equations that quantifies the economic impact a solution has on a customer's business. His work extends Tom Nagle's Economic Value Estimation (EVE) framework into a practical methodology for SaaS companies, and his central argument is that most companies confuse willingness-to-pay research with value-based pricing when the two are fundamentally different disciplines.

Forth's value model methodology works as follows. Begin with the customer's next-best competitive alternative and its price (the reference value). Then systematically identify value drivers, the specific ways your solution impacts the customer's business differently from the alternative. Forth categorizes value drivers into three types: economic (measurable P&L impact such as revenue increase, cost reduction, or risk mitigation), emotional (trust, confidence, reduced anxiety, brand association), and community (network effects, ecosystem access, peer validation). For each economic value driver, build an equation: if your solution reduces customer support costs by 20% and the customer currently spends $500K on support, that value driver is worth $100K annually. Sum all positive and negative differentiation values against the reference price to arrive at the Economic Value to Customer (EVC). The pricing decision then becomes: what share of this value do we capture?

His three approaches to implementing value-based pricing are a critical decision framework. Approximate Value-Based Pricing uses competitive benchmarks and rules of thumb to set prices in the right range without building a full value model; appropriate for early-stage companies with limited data. Derived Value-Based Pricing works backward from observable market signals (willingness to pay, win rates at different price points, competitive pricing) to infer value; more rigorous but still indirect. Direct Value-Based Pricing builds an explicit value model with customer-specific inputs and prices each deal based on calculated economic value; this approach best aligns buyer and seller but requires the deepest customer knowledge and sales sophistication. Forth strongly advocates moving toward Direct over time because it maximizes long-term revenue and creates defensibility through deep customer understanding.

His framework for choosing a pricing metric introduces a critical three-part system: Value Driver (the way you impact the customer's business), Value Metric (the unit of consumption by which a user gets value), and Pricing Metric (the unit of consumption for which a user pays). These three must be aligned. When they are misaligned, pricing feels arbitrary and churn increases. The pricing metric must pass three tests: does it correlate with the value the customer receives, can the customer predict and control their costs, and does it scale with the customer's success? Forth explicitly warns against defaulting to per-seat pricing when the value driver is not headcount-related, a mistake he sees in the majority of B2B SaaS companies. His segmentation principle is also distinctive: a good market segment is one where customers get value in the same way and buy in the same way, and the value model should generate different outputs for different segments based on their specific value driver weights.
