Claire Suellentrop is the leading practitioner of applying Jobs-to-be-Done theory specifically to SaaS marketing and growth strategy. Her intellectual origin story is instructive: as Director of Marketing at Calendly, she joined expecting to run a typical top-of-funnel acquisition playbook, only to discover the product was already growing organically. This forced her to pivot toward understanding why customers were self-selecting into the product -- which led her to JTBD methodology, originally encountered through Intercom's writing and Bob Moesta's work. Her journalism and radio background gave her an unfair advantage in customer interviews, and she combined this interview skill with JTBD's theoretical framework to develop a practical, repeatable process for SaaS companies.

Suellentrop's JTBD methodology for SaaS follows a specific workflow. First, identify 100-200 "high-value" customers -- people who are actively paying and recently converted, so they still remember life before the product. Send a survey (minimum 25-50 responses needed for pattern recognition) built around the "switch interview" structure: What struggle pushed you to look for a solution? How did you find us? What was the moment you knew this product was right? What can you do now that you could not do before? From survey responses, identify patterns to fill in a job story template: "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome]." Then select 10-12 customers for deep 1:1 interviews, keeping questions open-ended (her litmus test: "Would you ask this at a bar or coffee shop?"). The cardinal sin is asking about specific features rather than letting customers describe their own journey. From these interviews, distinct customer segments emerge organically -- not from demographic data but from shared jobs-to-be-done.

The output of this research flows directly into positioning, messaging, onboarding, and retention strategy. When Suellentrop discovered that Calendly's users included both salespeople and professors, she could see that different features mattered to each segment, enabling segment-specific onboarding emails that drove users to the features most relevant to their job. This is the operational bridge between JTBD theory and growth: you use voice-of-customer language in marketing copy (what customers actually say, not what the company wishes they said), build onboarding sequences tailored to each job, and prioritize features based on what drives value for your best customers. Her framework connects to Dunford's competitive alternatives analysis (what customers used before hiring your product is the real competitive set), to Laudi's experience mapping (the JTBD research feeds directly into the customer experience map), and to Kramer's messaging architecture (VoC mining produces the raw material for the messaging house).
