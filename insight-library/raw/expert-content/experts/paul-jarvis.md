Paul Jarvis's system of thought begins with a question that most business advice assumes is already answered: should this business grow? His Company of One philosophy does not argue that growth is bad, but that it should be questioned rather than assumed. The default setting in entrepreneurship is "more" -- more revenue, more clients, more employees, more products. Jarvis argues that this default creates businesses that own their founders rather than the reverse: the machine must be fed, and every growth milestone creates new obligations (payroll, office space, management overhead, complexity) that require even more growth to sustain. His alternative is to define "enough" -- a specific, personal threshold for revenue, clients, and work hours beyond which additional growth creates diminishing returns on happiness, autonomy, and creative satisfaction.

The Company of One is defined by four traits: resilience (the ability to weather economic volatility by staying lean), autonomy (retaining full control over business decisions without investor or board obligations), speed (the ability to move quickly because there are no committees, approvals, or coordination costs), and simplicity (minimizing the operational surface area so the business can be understood and managed by one person or a very small team). Jarvis's workflow advice follows a deliberate sequence. First, start by solving a real problem for a specific audience (not by building a team or raising capital). Second, build systems and processes that allow you to serve clients without proportionally increasing your time investment -- this means productizing services, creating digital products, building recurring revenue through subscriptions, or designing self-service models. Third, set upper limits: Jarvis profiles entrepreneurs like Sean D'Souza, whose consultancy Psychotactics has a hard cap of $500K in annual profit; when revenue exceeds the cap, D'Souza takes time off rather than scaling up. Fourth, reinvest in quality over quantity: instead of adding more clients, invest in serving existing clients better, building stronger relationships, and increasing lifetime value per customer.

The cross-disciplinary connections from Jarvis's work weave through the entire agency advisory ecosystem. Jason Fried's calm company principles are the organizational-design version of what Jarvis describes at the individual level -- Fried builds a 70-person company that operates with the discipline and intentionality Jarvis advocates for a one-person business. David C. Baker's observation that the most profitable agencies are often 5-16 people is the empirical validation of Jarvis's thesis: growth past a certain point often destroys per-person profitability. Karl Sakas's work on helping agency owners "work less and earn more" is the consulting implementation of Jarvis's philosophy for owners who have already grown past the one-person stage and need to restructure. Pia Silva's intensives model is arguably a Company of One in practice: a two-person team doing $30K-$50K per month in productized brand intensives with no employees, no proposals, and no accounts receivable. Jarvis's most provocative contribution is philosophical rather than tactical: he gives permission to entrepreneurs who have been socialized to believe that not growing is failure. In the agency context, this means it is legitimate to build a profitable solo practice or boutique firm and stop there, rather than constantly pursuing the next hire, the next retainer, the next growth milestone.
