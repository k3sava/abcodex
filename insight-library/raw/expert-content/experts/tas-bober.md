Tas Bober's approach to B2B landing pages is built on a foundational reframe that most B2B marketers get wrong: the goal of a landing page is consumption, not conversion. Because the B2B buying journey involves extensive information gathering, stakeholder alignment, and internal justification before a buyer ever contacts a vendor, the landing page's job is to provide enough information for the buyer to self-qualify and self-educate. Bober's cardinal rule is that the goal of each section is to send the user to the next section: the headline should get users to read the description, the description should urge readers to watch the video, the video helps understand the gaps and leads to the value propositions, which lead into social proof, and finally to the ask. This sequential consumption model directly challenges the CRO orthodoxy of minimizing page length and putting the CTA above the fold.

Bober identifies five cardinal sins of B2B landing page messaging: unclear or vague language (jargon like "revolutionize," "streamline," "seamless," "all-in-one"), being company-centric instead of buyer-centric (talking about your solution, awards, and badges without consideration for the user), hiding information (gating product demos or pricing so users get that information elsewhere where you cannot control the narrative), lacking empathy (not stating clearly that you understand your prospective customers' problems), and not talking to your ICP directly (failing to call out your customers so they know the page was created specifically for them). Her eight-element landing page framework specifies: summarized value proposition in clear words, product showcased at every opportunity with video and imagery, direct ICP callout, demonstration that you understand their problems and the market gap, top three capabilities of your solution, relevant social proof matched to the specific product, FAQs that answer real objections (not cutesy questions), and finally the ask with clear expectations of what happens next.

Her tactical craft extends into specific above-the-fold rules: page load speed must be under three seconds (if the page does not load fast enough, content quality is irrelevant), remove distractions like chatbots, pop-ups, and multiple CTAs before the user has had a chance to understand the landscape, ensure messaging clarity in the hero that covers exactly what you do for your ICP, and provide immediate proof via a logo garden under the hero that matches the company type you are targeting (if you target SMBs, do not show enterprise logos). She also applies the POUR accessibility framework (Perceivable, Operable, Understandable, Robust) to landing page design, and recommends writing at a third to fifth grade reading level because even a Harvard professor prefers to get information without strain. This connects to Julian Shapiro's Purchase Rate = Desire - (Labor + Confusion) formula and Peep Laja's clarity-first messaging layer model: all three practitioners converge on the principle that comprehension beats persuasion.
