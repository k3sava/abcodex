Madhavan Ramanujam is the leading voice on monetization strategy and the integration of pricing into product development. His central thesis, captured in the phrase "price before product, period," argues that the root cause of innovation failure is postponing pricing decisions until after product development. Based on Simon-Kucher's global study, 72% of innovations fail to meet their financial targets or fail entirely, and Ramanujam has built a complete diagnostic and prescriptive framework around preventing those failures. His signature case study is the Porsche Cayenne: before any product design began, Porsche surveyed customers on every possible feature and their willingness to pay for each. Features customers valued went in; features they did not, no matter how beloved by engineers, were excluded. The result was half of Porsche's total profit.

Ramanujam's most distinctive contribution is his taxonomy of four monetization failure modes. Feature Shock occurs when too many features are crammed into a product, making it hard to explain, costly to build, and overpriced (Amazon Fire Phone being the canonical example). Minivation describes a correctly designed product priced too low to capture its full revenue potential (Asus's 2008 mini-notebook that sold out instantly and left massive revenue on the table). Hidden Gems are potential blockbuster products never brought to market because they fall outside the company's core business (Kodak's digital photography technology, shelved for 21 years). Undead products are innovations nobody asked for, brought to market anyway (the Segway). This diagnostic framework gives companies a precise vocabulary for post-mortem analysis and pre-launch risk assessment.

His prescriptive methodology comprises nine rules organized around three principles. First, have the willingness-to-pay talk early using three direct questions: acceptable price, expensive price, and prohibitively expensive price, followed always by "Why?" This reveals psychological price thresholds and demand cliffs. Second, investigate how you charge as much as what you charge, identifying the right monetization model from five archetypes (subscription, dynamic pricing, auction, pay-as-you-go, freemium). The Michelin case study illustrates this: switching from selling tires to charging per mile driven transformed the company's economics. Third, never settle for one-size-fits-all; segment customers by needs, values, and willingness to pay, then configure product bundles for each segment using the Leaders-Fillers-Killers framework. Ramanujam categorizes pricing strategies into three types: maximization (for homogeneous WTP), penetration (low price for market share), and skimming (high price dropping over time). His more recent work extends these principles to AI products, arguing that AI companies should price against labor budgets (10x larger than IT budgets) and can capture 25-50% of value created, far exceeding the traditional SaaS 10% capture rate, because AI offers higher autonomy and clearer attribution.
