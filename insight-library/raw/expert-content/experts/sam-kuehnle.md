Sam Kuehnle brings a practitioner's perspective to B2B demand generation, blending hands-on paid media execution with systems-level thinking about how GTM motions actually work. His most distinctive contribution is the Brand Coefficient framework, which reframes the traditional "brand vs. demand" debate as a multiplicative relationship rather than a budget allocation trade-off. He argues that brand is what makes demand gen actually work: demand gen gets people in the door, but brand makes them want to walk through it. The practical implication is that a company with a strong brand and mediocre demand gen tactics will outperform a company with sophisticated demand gen but weak brand, because brand affects every funnel variable simultaneously (demo-to-SQO rate, cost per SQO, win rate, and ACV).

Kuehnle is particularly sharp on paid media efficiency. His LinkedIn manual bidding strategy demonstrates how marketers unknowingly outsource their thinking to platform algorithms: LinkedIn's "Maximum Delivery" default can charge CPMs of $115+ when manual bidding could achieve the same results at a fraction of the cost, potentially recovering 50-450% of wasted ad budget. He advocates using win/loss analysis from CRM data as a core demand gen activity, slicing opportunities by loss reason, win reason, opportunity source, company demographics, prospect seniority, sales rep, and incumbent solution to surface actionable patterns for channel and messaging optimization.

Kuehnle's systems-thinking approach draws heavily from Eliyahu Goldratt's Theory of Constraints, applying manufacturing optimization principles to GTM. His core insight is that GTM should be treated as an ecosystem rather than a collection of independent channels: optimizing individual channels in isolation (local performance) while ignoring how they interact degrades overall system output (global performance). He uses the Socratic method to help leadership teams arrive at this conclusion themselves, asking questions like "Would you rather hit your lead goal but miss revenue, or miss your lead goal but hit revenue?" and "Tell me about the last purchase you made for the business" to expose the non-linear, partially untrackable buying journey that linear attribution models deny. He also makes a critical distinction between goals and metrics: a department should rarely have more than 1-2 goals (typically revenue), with everything else treated as metrics that indicate progress toward those goals, not targets to optimize in isolation.
