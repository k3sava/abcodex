Jay Acunzo's central intellectual project is dismantling the best practices paradigm in marketing and replacing it with a decision-making framework grounded in contextual intuition. His book Break the Wheel uses a Game of Thrones metaphor: best practices are like the ever-spinning wheel where first one ruling approach is on top, then another, and breaking the wheel means asking six fundamental questions in any situation to make better decisions for your specific context. The core question is deceptively simple: "What works best for us?" This question replaces the default marketing question ("What's the best practice?") with a context-dependent inquiry that forces teams to examine their specific audience, goals, constraints, and competitive environment rather than copying what worked for someone else. Acunzo argues that best practices are averages derived from broad populations, and averages describe no one; the details of your own context hold vital clues, and intuition is the pattern-recognition skill that surfaces them.

Acunzo's signature concept is resonance over reach. He defines resonance as the urge to act that people feel when a message or moment aligns with them, like two frequencies aligning where the first imparts energy to the second and amplifies it. Resonance is how much people care; reach is how many people see it. His argument is that most B2B marketing optimizes for reach (impressions, clicks, followers) while ignoring resonance (the depth of connection that actually drives conversion). This distinction has operational implications: content that resonates with 1,000 people who are genuinely in your market produces more business results than content that reaches 100,000 people who are not. He identifies four elements of effective storytelling that creates resonance: Align (show you understand the audience's hopes, challenges, and goals), Agitate (highlight the consequences of the status quo), Assert (present your distinct perspective as the solution), and Invite (call to action). Most marketers skip to Invite without doing the work of Align, Agitate, and Assert, which is why their calls to action fall flat.

Acunzo's cross-disciplinary contribution is connecting the craft of storytelling to the business of marketing in a way that is operationally actionable rather than inspirational. His distinction between great storytelling (which entertains) and effective storytelling (which moves people to act) is critical for B2B teams that confuse thought leadership with entertaining content. He argues that most marketing fails because teams do not take the time to be honest with themselves about their vision, their point of view, and what they are actually trying to communicate. The foundational work is internal (understanding your own perspective) before it is external (communicating to audiences). This philosophy directly challenges the common B2B approach of leading with features and benefits, which Acunzo sees as a relic of an era when businesses were the only player in town and did not need to earn attention through resonant storytelling.
