Jonathan Stark's system of thought begins with a single provocation: hourly billing is not a pricing method, it is a billing method borrowed from industrial-age factory management and legal cost accounting, and it has no business in professional services. His reasoning proceeds from a structural observation: when you bill by the hour, your financial incentive is to work slowly and your client's incentive is to rush you. The better you get at your craft, the fewer hours you need, so expertise literally reduces your income. This misalignment is not a bug in the hourly model -- it is the core mechanic. Stark's alternative is value-based pricing, where fees are set based on the economic outcome the client receives, not the time the consultant spends. The shift requires a complete reorientation of the sales conversation: instead of scoping deliverables and estimating hours, the consultant must first understand what the client is trying to achieve and why it matters financially.

Stark's core workflow is the Why Conversation, a structured discovery process that happens before any proposal is written. The consultant asks the client a series of questions designed to surface the business problem, the desired outcome, the metrics of success, and the economic value at stake. The sequence matters: you diagnose before you prescribe, which is the distinction between an expert and a pair of hands. Only after the Why Conversation does the consultant design a solution and propose pricing. Stark recommends starting the transition to value pricing on small projects with trusted clients, adding an 85% premium to the hourly-based estimate as a buffer, and gradually building confidence with the model. His LFPS (Laser-Focused Positioning Statement) formula -- "I help [target market] with [expensive problem]" -- is the prerequisite for value pricing, because you cannot price on value if you do not know whose problems are expensive enough to justify premium fees.

The cross-disciplinary connection between Stark, Blair Enns, and David C. Baker is tight but each occupies a distinct lane. Enns provides the sales methodology (Four Conversations, multi-option proposals, anchoring), Baker provides the positioning and expertise-building framework (10-200 competitors, 2,000-10,000 prospects, the 55 Topics technique), and Stark provides the solo-consultant translation layer: how to apply value pricing when you are a one-person operation without a sales team or account managers. His concept of "crystallizing your specialization" is the solo practitioner's version of Baker's positioning framework -- pick the intersection of what you are good at, what you enjoy, and what the market will pay a premium for, then go narrow. Stark also connects to Alex Hormozi's offer creation framework: while Hormozi works at the business scaling end, Stark's daily emails and bootcamp curriculum address the same Value Equation variables (dream outcome, perceived likelihood, time delay, effort) but for the solo consultant context, where the "offer" is a productized consulting engagement rather than an info-product or service business at scale.
