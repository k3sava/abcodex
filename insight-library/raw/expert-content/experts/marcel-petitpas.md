Marcel Petitpas has built his expertise around a single, high-stakes problem: why agencies with growing revenue still see shrinking profit. His central framework, the Agency Profitability Flywheel, provides a systematic model for identifying the highest-ROI investments an agency can make to protect time and profits. At its core, the framework revolves around one master metric, Delivery Margin, which measures how much of each dollar collected from clients is left over after the direct cost of completing deliverables. Petitpas insists this is distinct from gross margin as most accountants define it, because agencies must first strip out pass-through expenses (ad spend, subcontractors, white-label partners, materials) to calculate Agency Gross Income (AGI), then isolate delivery costs (delivery payroll plus shared delivery software and equipment) to arrive at the true Delivery Margin. His benchmark target is at least 50% delivery margin, which leaves 30% for overhead and 20% for EBITDA.

Beneath Delivery Margin sit three operational levers that Petitpas considers the complete toolkit for improving profitability. Average Billable Rate (ABR) measures revenue earned per hour spent on client work, regardless of billing model; across North American agencies he has audited, the average sits around $150-160/hour. ABR can be improved either by charging more for the same scope or by reducing the hours spent on a given deliverable without reducing the price (efficiency gains). Utilization measures the percentage of team time dedicated to revenue-generating work; Petitpas recommends targeting 85-90% (32-36 hours of a 40-hour week), but emphasizes this is a separate problem from earning efficiency. A team can have high utilization and still lose money if ABR is too low, or high ABR and lose money if utilization is too low, and each requires a different response. Average Cost Per Hour (ACPH) focuses on reducing the per-hour cost of the delivery team, typically by shifting certain tasks to less expensive resources through better processes, documentation, and technology. A 5% increase in utilization alone can produce a 50-80% increase in bottom-line profitability because of the leveraged nature of these metrics.

Petitpas challenges two common misconceptions in the agency world. First, that profitability must come at the expense of team happiness. He argues that infinite combinations of the three levers can achieve high profitability, including configurations with relatively low utilization rates, as long as the agency commands above-average rates or uses lower-cost talent. Second, he challenges the "growth solves everything" belief with a signature observation: "Most agencies suffer from indigestion, not starvation; they just happen to feel the same." He has seen agencies double revenue while founders take home less profit, because they kept adding unprofitable retainer clients instead of replacing underperformers. His recommended tactic is to measure ABR by client, identify the least profitable ones, and replace them with higher-quality revenue rather than simply adding volume. On the future of agency operations, Petitpas advocates for functional specialization: the most profitable agencies he has audited specialize not just in their target market and services but in which business functions they handle internally versus outsourcing. He cites a case of an SEO agency that outsourced all delivery to white-label vendors while retaining strategy, account management, and sales in-house, achieving 40%+ EBITDA at eight-figure revenue.
