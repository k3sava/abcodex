Steph Smith's contribution to content strategy is the application of systems thinking and engineering discipline to what is traditionally treated as a creative endeavor. Her book Doing Content Right is less a manifesto than an operating manual: it covers every aspect of building a content business from topic selection through distribution, SEO, monetization, and scaling, with the systematic rigor of a product engineering playbook. Her central thesis is that content creation is a compounding asset that rewards consistency, distribution, and strategic channel selection over creative inspiration alone. A blog is like a startup: you test hypotheses until you find product-market fit, and the quality bar is binary: your content must be genuinely new (information that does not exist elsewhere) or measurably better (at least 1% improvement) than the competition.

Smith's distribution framework uses the explore-and-exploit method borrowed from machine learning and decision theory. In the explore phase (which she recommends conducting monthly), you test one to three new distribution channels with minimal investment. In the exploit phase, you cut channels showing no progress and double down on those that do. Her channel taxonomy divides distribution into six categories: Bedrock Channels (SEO, email, which are dependable and owned), Viral Platforms (Product Hunt, Hacker News, Reddit, which offer explosive but unpredictable growth), Social Media (Twitter/X for scale, LinkedIn for professional audiences), Syndication (republishing on Medium, Dev.to, or industry sites), Targeted (niche forums, communities, direct outreach), and Paid Growth (ads, sponsorships). She recommends Twitter for scale, newsletter for ownership, and SEO for dependability as the three-channel foundation, then expanding only after proving at least one channel works.

Smith's approach to content at scale reflects her experience at The Hustle (where she led the Trends product to millions in ARR) and HubSpot. She treats SEO as a systematic discipline: understanding search intent, listening to what Google is telling you through its SERP features, optimizing keywords methodically, and building content clusters around topics where you can establish authority. Her programmatic content thinking applies software engineering concepts to content production: identifying repeatable patterns, building templates and frameworks that can be applied across hundreds of pages, and using data to prioritize content investments. In the early stages, she advocates doing things that do not scale (one-on-one outreach, personal networking, manual community engagement) to build the initial audience before systematizing. This tension between unscalable relationship building and scalable systems is a distinctive feature of her methodology and connects to the broader broader operator themes of sustainable growth over growth hacking.
