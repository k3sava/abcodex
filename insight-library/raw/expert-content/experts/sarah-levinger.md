Sarah Levinger occupies a unique position at the intersection of consumer psychology and performance advertising. Her central thesis is that post-iOS 14, creative has replaced targeting as the most important variable in paid ad performance, and the brands that win are the ones that design ads based on emotional data rather than transactional data alone. While most marketers rely on clicks, conversions, and AOV to guide creative strategy, Levinger argues these metrics only reveal what happened, not why it happened. Her work through Tether Insights focuses on uncovering the emotional context behind purchase decisions: how subconscious behavior, identity construction, and personal narratives influence what people buy. This is not abstract theory but a practical research methodology that produces directly actionable creative briefs.

Levinger's operational process has three components. First, identity-based audience research: instead of defining audiences by demographics (women, 25-34), she maps the psychological identity markers that drive purchase behavior. What do they value? What cultural shifts have shaped their beliefs? What makes them feel seen, understood, and validated? She distinguishes between generational psychology (millennials were raised with high validation and prioritize brands that reinforce identity; Gen Z values fluidity and expects brands to evolve with them) and individual psychology (the specific emotional needs that a product fulfills). Second, emotion-based surveys: rather than asking "what is your favorite product?" or "how did you hear about us?", she uses pictorial surveys that gather subconscious reactions by asking customers to choose images that resonate with them. This eliminates self-reporting bias and produces deeper emotional insights than traditional survey methods. Third, emotional data analysis of existing creative: she examines why specific ads perform by mapping their emotional triggers (security, nostalgia, empowerment, identity validation) rather than their surface features (UGC vs. studio, video vs. static).

Her most illustrative case study involves a bamboo sheets brand where the data showed the primary buyer was a 35-55-year-old woman, but the best-performing ads featured masculine-colored sheets (navy, slate gray) even though the most-purchased colors were softer pastels. Traditional logic would push pastel ads, but Levinger's emotional analysis revealed that women were clicking masculine colors because they were considering what their partner would like, then ultimately buying their own preferred colors. The actionable insight was to double down on masculine-colored ads with messaging about couples shopping together, producing a major conversion lift. This exemplifies the gap between transactional analysis (which would optimize for pastel ads matching purchase data) and emotional analysis (which optimizes for the psychological state that initiates the purchase journey). Her work connects to Adrienne Barnes's customer research methodology: both practitioners argue that surface-level data misses the deeper behavioral patterns that actually drive decisions, but Levinger applies this specifically to paid creative while Barnes applies it to persona development and content strategy.
