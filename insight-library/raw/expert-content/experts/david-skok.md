David Skok is the architect of the modern SaaS metrics framework. His contributions are not just analytical but definitional: the LTV:CAC ratio, months to recover CAC, and the SaaS cash flow trough are concepts that Skok either originated or popularized to the point where they became industry-standard vocabulary. His reasoning process starts from a deceptively simple question: "Can I make more profit from my customers than it costs me to acquire them?" Everything else in SaaS finance flows from how rigorously you answer that question.

Skok's SaaS Metrics 2.0 framework identifies three keys to SaaS success: acquiring customers efficiently, retaining them, and monetizing them over time. For acquisition, the critical metrics are CAC (fully loaded cost to acquire one customer, including all sales and marketing spend) and months to recover CAC (the time it takes for a customer's gross margin contribution to pay back their acquisition cost). His guideline is that LTV should be at least 3x CAC (the "golden ratio" that became an industry standard), and CAC should be recovered in under 12 months. For retention, he demonstrates that halving the churn rate doubles LTV, making churn reduction far more impactful than acquisition optimization. He introduced the concept of negative churn (net revenue retention above 100%) as the defining characteristic of the best SaaS businesses, where expansion revenue from existing customers exceeds revenue lost to churn.

His SaaS Economics series provides the most detailed analysis available of why SaaS businesses are cash-intensive to build. In Part 1, he models the "SaaS cash flow trough": because customers pay monthly but acquisition costs are incurred upfront, every new customer creates a temporary cash deficit. When a company hires two new salespeople per month to drive growth, his model shows a worst monthly loss of $190K and first profit only after 21 months. The faster you hire, the deeper the trough but the steeper the eventual profitability curve. In Part 2 (Scaling), he introduces the unit economics of a sales person: how long it takes each sales rep to ramp to full productivity, what the expected payback period is on each sales hire, and what percentage of sales hires fail to reach quota (which he estimates at 30-40%). This salesperson-level unit economics model is distinct from customer-level unit economics and is essential for modeling hiring velocity. Skok's contribution to cross-disciplinary thinking is his insistence that SaaS metrics are not finance department tools but operational decision frameworks: they should drive hiring speed, marketing budget allocation, product prioritization, and pricing strategy. A declining LTV:CAC ratio is not just a financial signal but a diagnostic that something is wrong in product, sales, or market fit.
