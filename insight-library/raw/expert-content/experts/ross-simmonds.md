Ross Simmonds is the most systematic thinker on content distribution in B2B marketing. His central thesis, codified in his book Create Once, Distribute Forever, is that the content marketing industry has a structural imbalance: teams invest disproportionately in creation while treating distribution as an afterthought, which means good content routinely dies in obscurity. His framework inverts the conventional ratio, arguing that distribution should receive more time, budget, and strategic attention than creation, because distribution is the multiplier that determines whether content investment compounds or evaporates. At Foundation Marketing, distribution is a full line item with its own strategy, budget, and execution playbook, not a checkbox at the end of a content brief.

Simmonds's distribution framework has three core techniques applied to every content asset after publication. Resharing means posting the link across owned channels (LinkedIn, Twitter, email, Slack communities, Quora) on a recurring schedule, not just once at publication. Remixing means transforming the original asset into new formats for new channels: a blog post becomes a LinkedIn carousel, a YouTube video, a podcast segment, a Twitter thread, an infographic, or a Reddit discussion starter. Redistributing means revisiting high-performing content months or years after publication and promoting it again, because evergreen content does not expire, and audiences change over time. His case study of repurposing a PR piece about Xbox vs. PS4 that gained renewed traction months later illustrates the compounding returns of this approach. The operational insight is that every piece of high-quality content should generate five to ten derivative distribution actions, and distribution should be planned before creation begins, not improvised after publication.

Simmonds connects distribution strategy to audience research through a process that starts with identifying where target audiences spend time (using tools like SparkToro, Reddit analysis, and social listening), then selecting distribution channels based on audience presence rather than marketer convenience. He advocates for diversified outreach beyond email: LinkedIn DMs, Reddit posts, Slack community engagement, Facebook groups, niche forums, and conference networking. His philosophy of relationships over scale in outreach is a direct counter to the AI-scaled mass outreach trend. He argues that genuine engagement, familiarity building, and relationship-first outreach produce better long-term distribution results than automated volume. This connects to the broader broader operator themes of audience-first strategy and the critique of attribution-centric measurement: distribution effectiveness is measured by organic traffic, leads, and revenue impact, not by vanity metrics like social impressions or click-through rates.
