# Annie Duke — Decision Quality, Explicit Thinking, Feedback Loops

## Summary

Annie Duke (World Series of Poker bracelet, $4M+ tournament winnings, Thinking in Bets author) distills decision-quality from poker into corporate contexts. Her core insight: explicit > implicit; pre-mortems work only with kill criteria; there's no such thing as a long feedback loop (you shorten it by finding correlated signals).

## 5 load-bearing insights

1. **Make intuition explicit to find where you're wrong.** Intuition isn't crap, but if you don't make it explicit, you don't get feedback on when it fails. Example: "I think this positioning will resonate" is implicit. Explicit: "We expect 15% CTR on this LP variant, and we'll know we're wrong if it's <8%." Explicitness forces specificity; specificity enables falsification.

2. **Pre-mortems only work with kill criteria.** A pre-mortem asks: "Assume this failed. Why?" But if you don't commit to actions ("If we see >30% churn in month 2, we pivot messaging"), the pre-mortem is a feelings exercise. Kill criteria transform pre-mortems into decision gates. Applicable to campaign launches, product bets, messaging changes.

3. **There is no such thing as a long feedback loop.** Reframe: what correlates with the outcome you desire, measurable in short intervals? "Did this positioning resonate?" Long loop. "Did we get 3+ qualified demos from this week's outreach?" Short loop. Shortening loops accelerates learning velocity. Equivalently: operationalize lagging indicators into leading signals.

4. **Mental time travel (time-shifting) depressurizes high-emotion decisions.** When grounded, a team says "this messaging change will kill us." Mental time travel: "What will we think about this in 1 year? Funny story or genuine failure?" This unlocks perspective without dismissing present concern. In product: "Will this feature matter in 2 years?" often reveals whether you're fighting for something truly structural.

5. **"Nevertheless" leadership: hear + decide.** When team says "This positioning contradicts our messaging," respond "I hear you, nevertheless we ship Friday." This avoids false impression of co-decision-making. Annie uses this with children; it applies to teams. It separates buy-in (from being heard) from veto (which the leader holds).

## Other notable threads

- Kahneman's humility: "I don't know a lot." Always seeking adversarial collaboration (find disagreement, write a paper together). "Nothing is as important as it seems when you're thinking about it."
- Resulting bias in parenting: first child doesn't drink → "I raised them well." Fourth child drinks → "Oh, it's temperament, not my parenting." Same lesson: outcome ≠ decision quality.
- Quitting framework (from her book): Most people quit too late, not too early. If you're thinking "should I quit?" you should have quit already.

## Why this matters for flywheel

The flywheel's decision velocity and taste refinement depend on short feedback loops. Current setup: message tested weekly, CTR/demo conversion checked, insights fed back into next iteration. Annie's framework scales this: make taste assumptions explicit ("We expect SMB buyers to respond to speed-to-lead framing"), pre-mortem weekly ("What if this demo-rate drops 20%?"), and use "nevertheless" when human judgment overrides data ("The data says X, nevertheless we're shipping Y because of positioning coherence across all channels").

This also applies to team leadership: when Reshma says "This vertical positioning is too narrow," you can listen (buy-in) while deciding "Nevertheless, we're shipping this Q2 because the operator research supports it."
