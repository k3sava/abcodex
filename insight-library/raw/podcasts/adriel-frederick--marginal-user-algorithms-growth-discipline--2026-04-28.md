# Adriel Frederick on Marginal Users, Human-in-the-Loop Algorithms, and Growth Discipline

## Why this is in the wiki

Adriel articulates three load-bearing PM principles: (1) focus product design on the *marginal user* (worst case) to see everything wrong; (2) algorithms amplify human intent, they don't replace human judgment; (3) growth portfolios need both cannonballs and lead bullets, not just incremental tinkering. All three unlock flywheel-grade execution rigor.

## 6 load-bearing insights for flywheel

1. **Marginal user: the person on the cusp of converting**
   - Not the average user, not the power user. The person who *wants* to take your desired action but hasn't yet, or the worst-case person in the funnel.
   - Find them by looking at high-traffic, low-conversion geographies or segments. Then go *watch them use the product*. Don't rely on data alone.
   - Example: Facebook registration in edge-network countries on feature phones. Language wrong, country detection wrong, connection slow. Data shows the drop-off; watching the user shows *why*.
   - When you fix marginal user problems, you fix everyone downstream. Focus on making that person's experience "stupid easy."

2. **Finding the real barrier requires leaving the data and talking to people**
   - A funnel can show you *where* people drop off but not *why*. Orthogonal problems hide in the data.
   - Example: Adriel watched someone sign up for Facebook in India. When asked, "What name are you using?" they said their legal name. When asked, "Does anyone in the real world call you that?" — no. Problem: friend requests won't be accepted because nobody recognizes the name.
   - Data alone would have blamed friend request acceptance rate, not registration name choice. Only watching the marginal user revealed the real bottleneck.

3. **Algorithms need humans to make judgment calls; humans need algorithms to amplify their intent**
   - Techno-utopian view: feed all data to algorithm, give it an objective, let it run. Fails because algorithms don't understand long-term effects, user response, or product intent.
   - At Lyft, pricing algorithm optimizing for market share alone would drop prices to zero. At Facebook, algorithm optimizing for engagement alone would amplify anger.
   - Solution: humans decide *constraints* (profit floor, anger exclusion), *strategic choices* (where to win, how to respond to competitive moves). Algorithm amplifies that human intent across millions of individual decisions.
   - Adriel: "Your job is figuring out what the algorithm should be responsible for, what people are responsible for, and the framework for making decisions."

4. **Growth is lead bullets + cannonballs, not just incremental hacks**
   - 60% of experiments succeed, 40% fail. But within the 60%, hidden cost: you're "futzing around" with small things when you could have tackled bigger ones.
   - Cannonballs (massive investment, fundamental product changes): signup with phone numbers, SMS delivery, friend recommendations. Few of these. Maybe 20% of effort.
   - Lead bullets (small optimizations): button copy, placement, funnel friction reductions. Many of these. Maybe 80% of effort.
   - Laziness trap: teams run tons of lead-bullet experiments because they're easier to design, build, and ship. It's seductive but doesn't compound.
   - Adriel: "There's a laziness that can creep in where you're just finding a lot of little things because they're easier to come up with."

5. **Diverse teams solve products faster because they *are* your user research**
   - At Facebook, Adriel's growth team was Black Trinidadian PM + Israeli female engineering manager + Brazilian tech lead + Russian, Chinese, Slavic engineers.
   - When designing for global users: instead of recruiting panels and testing, the team just debated how their friends would use it. "We'd knock out in 15 minutes what might take two weeks to recruit and research."
   - Real example: because of Trinidad background, Adriel knew one person ≠ one phone number (prepaid SIM cards, shared devices, expense). That shaped Facebook's registration design for growth markets.
   - Diversity = speed + quality. Business value when you see it. Retention follows because diverse talent stays when valued.

6. **PM leadership = organization design + empathy, not technical brilliance**
   - The job scales from IC to VP: you can't do everything yourself. You need clear goals, safe people, smooth processes.
   - Adriel: "Taking my own shoes off" — not projecting your objectives onto peers/reports, but genuinely getting into their shoes, understanding what they care about, what they're scared of, what they want.
   - Once you understand *their* objective + *your* objective, you find the common ground and unblock it.
   - "Problems go away" when you show people you have the same mission and you actually care about their success.

## Other notable threads

- R&D teams in large orgs: must feel core to mission, everyone wins (not just R&D), innovation can still happen in core product. Otherwise "organ rejection."
- When market conditions shift, assume you lost product-market fit. Marketsize + PMF must use same definition, or false confidence.
- Marginal user thinking helps prioritize which barriers to tackle first: remove the easiest ones, see what's still wrong, keep grinding.

## Why this matters for flywheel

**Marginal user methodology** = CMO + product team unified. Instead of "what does the data say," ask "who is on the cusp of converting and what blocks them?" Watch them. That discipline scales demand gen from chasing shiny channels to solving real conversion barriers.

**Human-in-the-loop systems** = sales team gets algorithmic personalization but keeps judgment over outreach strategy. Algorithms optimize the *execution* of human intent, not the reverse. Guardrails built in.

**Growth portfolio discipline** = stop running 47 micro-tests and asking "did we move the needle?" Force conversation: what are the 1-2 cannonballs we need? What are the 10-15 lead bullets? Allocate time accordingly. Compounding happens at cannonball scale, not lead-bullet scale.
