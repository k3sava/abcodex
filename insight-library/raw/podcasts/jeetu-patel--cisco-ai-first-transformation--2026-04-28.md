# Jeetu Patel — Turning Cisco AI-first

## Why this is in the wiki
Jeetu turned Cisco — 90K employees, founded 1984 — into an AI-first company. Direct relevance: the same problems Kesava is solving for the JustCall marketing team are the same problems Jeetu solved at 1000x scale. His three principles (be clear what's up for debate, define success as platform not silos, stay in open ecosystems) map cleanly onto the flywheel's frame for the marketing org.

## 11 load-bearing insights for flywheel

1. **"Innovation is a choice."** "I always find it interesting when people say, 'Well, you're a large company, you can innovate. You're a small company, you can't innovate.' No, it's a choice. Every day you come into work and you can choose to be thinking about being creative, or you can choose to not be creative." Direct response to the "we're too small/too constrained" objection from PMM teammates and from Deepan. Choice, not capacity.

2. **Be clear what's up for debate and what isn't.** "What can end up happening is you can have a pocket veto in a large company where if you ask enough number of people, people say no." On AI, Cisco didn't hedge — they said all-in. Flywheel implication: the substrate's existence is not up for debate; the workstreams it touches first are. Frame meetings accordingly.

3. **"Large companies experiment a lot. What they don't do is when an experiment works, they don't go all in and double down. They keep hedging."** Mistake to avoid in flywheel: when a bet wins (positioning loop, copy-review loop, Aashna routine), the next move is to double down, not to add five more parallel experiments. Underfunding the winners is how the substrate dies.

4. **Personal success aligned with company success.** "If they feel like AI is going to take their job or be negative for them, we had to reassure them that was not the case. But the reverse was guaranteed: if you didn't choose AI, your job is probably not going to be relevant." Same exact framing the flywheel rollout needs internally. Substrate isn't optional; not adopting it is the actual career risk.

5. **Platform > holding company of silos.** Cisco's GMs each wanted their own engineering, sales, marketing — "if your goal is to run a bunch of $40M businesses inside a $40B company, that's not good for the company." Marketing analog: the team without a substrate is a holding company of personal-OS workflows. Substrate makes it a platform.

6. **"Loosely coupled, but tightly integrated."** Don't force every customer to buy everything; do force the products to feel like one product when they're together. Direct read for substrate UX: each agent stands alone, but a marketer using two of them feels like one continuous workflow.

7. **Treat AI as a teammate, not a tool.** "There was this light bulb that went off... stop trying to think of this as a tool. Think of this as a teammate that got added to your team." Echoes Claire Vo word-for-word from the same week. Two unrelated operators converging on the same framing — strong signal. Use this verbatim.

8. **Three constraints on AI adoption: infrastructure, trust, data.** Cisco focuses on the infrastructure layer. For flywheel, the constraints are different but parallel: substrate compute (handled), teammate trust (the actual gate), and contextDB completeness (the data gap). Map all three explicitly in the next pitch.

9. **"Stamina trumps intellect."** "It's very important to have smart people, but you can become smart if you have curiosity, hunger, staying power, persistence. You can't teach hunger." Quoted twice in this episode. Hire/retain on stamina, not raw IQ. Direct frame for who to invest in on the JustCall PMM team.

10. **Criticize in public.** "Every management book will tell you praise in public, criticize in private. I fundamentally disagree. What you have to do is establish enough trust among the team so that you are comfortable critiquing and debating in public." This validates the captain-rotation/in-room-critique culture Kesava is already running. External-operator confirmation it scales — at 30K reports.

11. **Permission to play.** "Just by building a product that is amazing in some area, you don't actually get it to mass scale distribution. Is it going to be logical for people that we built it versus another company building it?" Apply to flywheel: does the team have permission to ship a substrate-built artifact externally? The answer is yes for narrow assets (LPs, blog posts, vertical playbooks) and no for broad ones (analyst reports, partnership comms). Map the permission boundary before scaling.

## Other notable threads

- **Right to win + route to market.** The "permission to play" sister concept. Two filters Aaron Levie pushes him on: do customers think this is logical for us, and can our existing distribution carry the new product? Marketing version: every flywheel bet should pass both filters — does the asset feel like Kesava's voice/team's voice, and can the existing channels (LinkedIn, blog, sales decks) carry it.
- **"I will accept critique, you will go a lot farther in life if you don't care who gets the credit."** From Chuck Robbins. Direct read for flywheel attribution norms — when the substrate generates an artifact a teammate ships, the teammate gets credit. Substrate is invisible.
- **Six-week dinner with Aaron Levie + Jeff Kweiser.** Three former Box leaders, dinner every six weeks for six years. Operating discipline for relationship maintenance. Pattern Kesava could adopt with key clearPMM allies.
- **"Capacity is augmented to humans... so you can do things you really care to do, and not do things you don't care to do."** The substrate's job. Same articulation as `feedback_flywheel_human_value_frame.md`.
- **"Think exponentially across multiple dimensions at once."** Ray Kurzweil quote. Most people exponentialize one variable. The right model is many-dimensions-compounding. Useful frame for flywheel projections — bets compound, contextDB compounds, taste compounds, the team compounds; the right unit of forecast is multi-dimensional, not headcount.

## Why this matters for flywheel

Three direct loads. First, the three principles (decide what's not-up-for-debate, become a platform not a holding company of silos, open-ecosystem partnerships) are exactly the structure the flywheel pitch needs for Deepan and the CFO. Lift the language. "Cisco-level transformation playbook applied to the marketing team" is more pitchable than "AI-native marketing team."

Second, "treat it as a teammate, not a tool" — converging language across Claire Vo (same week), Jeetu, and Boris Cherny. Three independent operators at vastly different scales using the same phrase. Use it as the lead frame in any internal flywheel onboarding doc.

Third, "criticize in public + stamina trumps intellect" together describe the operating norms the flywheel team already runs. External validation that these are not eccentric Kesava preferences — they're how high-trust AI-native orgs operate at scale.
