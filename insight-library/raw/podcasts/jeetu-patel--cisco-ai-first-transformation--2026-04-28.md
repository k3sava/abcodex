# Jeetu Patel — Turning Cisco AI-first

## Summary
Jeetu turned Cisco — 90K employees, founded 1984 — into an AI-first company. Three principles: be clear what's up for debate, define success as platform not silos, stay in open ecosystems.

## Full text source
Transcript at `lenny-dropbox/Jeetu Patel.txt`.

## 11 load-bearing insights

1. **"Innovation is a choice."** "I always find it interesting when people say, 'Well, you're a large company, you can innovate. You're a small company, you can't innovate.' No, it's a choice. Every day you come into work and you can choose to be thinking about being creative, or you can choose to not be creative."

2. **Be clear what's up for debate and what isn't.** "What can end up happening is you can have a pocket veto in a large company where if you ask enough number of people, people say no." On AI, Cisco didn't hedge — they said all-in.

3. **"Large companies experiment a lot. What they don't do is when an experiment works, they don't go all in and double down. They keep hedging."** When a bet wins, the next move is to double down, not to add five more parallel experiments.

4. **Personal success aligned with company success.** "If they feel like AI is going to take their job or be negative for them, we had to reassure them that was not the case. But the reverse was guaranteed: if you didn't choose AI, your job is probably not going to be relevant." Not adopting AI is the actual career risk.

5. **Platform > holding company of silos.** Cisco's GMs each wanted their own engineering, sales, marketing — "if your goal is to run a bunch of $40M businesses inside a $40B company, that's not good for the company."

6. **"Loosely coupled, but tightly integrated."** Don't force every customer to buy everything; do force the products to feel like one product when they're together.

7. **Treat AI as a teammate, not a tool.** "There was this light bulb that went off... stop trying to think of this as a tool. Think of this as a teammate that got added to your team." Echoes Claire Vo word-for-word from the same week. Two unrelated operators converging on the same framing — strong signal.

8. **Three constraints on AI adoption: infrastructure, trust, data.** Cisco focuses on the infrastructure layer.

9. **"Stamina trumps intellect."** "It's very important to have smart people, but you can become smart if you have curiosity, hunger, staying power, persistence. You can't teach hunger." Quoted twice in this episode.

10. **Criticize in public.** "Every management book will tell you praise in public, criticize in private. I fundamentally disagree. What you have to do is establish enough trust among the team so that you are comfortable critiquing and debating in public."

11. **Permission to play.** "Just by building a product that is amazing in some area, you don't actually get it to mass scale distribution. Is it going to be logical for people that we built it versus another company building it?"

## Other notable threads

- **Right to win + route to market.** The "permission to play" sister concept. Two filters Aaron Levie pushes him on: do customers think this is logical for us, and can our existing distribution carry the new product?
- **"I will accept critique, you will go a lot farther in life if you don't care who gets the credit."** From Chuck Robbins.
- **Six-week dinner with Aaron Levie + Jeff Kweiser.** Three former Box leaders, dinner every six weeks for six years. Operating discipline for relationship maintenance.
- **"Capacity is augmented to humans... so you can do things you really care to do, and not do things you don't care to do."**
- **"Think exponentially across multiple dimensions at once."** Ray Kurzweil quote. Most people exponentialize one variable. The right model is many-dimensions-compounding.
