# Claire Hughes Johnson — Scaling People

## Why this is in the wiki
CHJ scaled Stripe from 160 to 7,000+ as COO. Her book Scaling People is the closest external precedent for the operating-system layer of the flywheel pitch: how a young company actually wires goals, cadence, decision rights, and culture into something that doesn't fall over at scale. Three direct loads for Kesava: (a) the "force for positive momentum" rule for product managers without authority is the JustCall PMM team's actual operating reality, (b) the operating-cadence framework gives shape to the b1/b2/b3 sweep + AI digest rhythm, (c) the personal operating principles section is a direct calibration for Kesava's clearPMM voice and the team operating norms.

## 11 load-bearing insights

1. **Force for positive momentum.** "If you're not sure who the decision maker is, one, it's probably you... Don't get stuck. Too many people get stuck and it makes your work terrible. What do we all care about? Progress, impact, momentum." Direct mandate for any PMM operating without authority. Translates to the captain-rotation rule already in Kesava's memory — the captain decides, doesn't ask. CHJ's framing makes this a career-maker rule, not just a meeting-room rule.

2. **The house metaphor: foundation, supporting beams, mechanicals.** Foundation = founding documents (mission, long-term goals, operating principles). Supporting beams = goal/QBR/planning structures. Mechanicals = operating cadence (the rhythm of meetings, reviews, demos, customer events). "If you don't start putting those things in early, people will just invent those things, and then you'll have a house that got added on to 17 times." Direct read for the flywheel substrate's design — the operating system itself IS one of the layers being pitched, not just the AI.

3. **Levels and ladders earlier than feels comfortable.** Stripe put them in around 200 people; one peer company waited until 800 and called it a bloodbath. "Putting in levels and ladders felt like ripping the bandaid off, honestly, but I was glad we did it." Direct read for the JustCall PMM team during the Q2 reorg: clearer ownership/leveling early prevents the title inflation drama later. Also a calibration for clearPMM consulting tier structure — clear levels reduce anxiety on both sides.

4. **Title minimalism for optionality + culture.** Stripe's "everyone's a product manager / growth lead" pattern is deliberate. "The minute you start titling a lot, you're signaling hierarchy and authority." Two reasons: (a) optionality as the org grows, (b) culture signal that expertise sits with whoever has knowledge, not whoever has the title. Counter to JustCall's title-rich tendency. For PMM team writing: don't lead with titles in flywheel-internal docs; lead with the work owned. (Note: clashes with Kesava's "kill pod" + team-naming work, but compatible — both push toward de-emphasizing hierarchical signals.)

5. **Build self-awareness to build mutual awareness.** First personal operating principle. "The more that you can seek feedback, seek to understand your motivators, your strengths, your blind spots, your tendencies, and take that on board and expose it to others, you're going to be a much more effective company builder." Direct read for the Kesava-Deepan working relationship and for the AI Growth lane staffing: the lane will work only if Kesava and Deepan can name the friction openly.

6. **Say the thing you think you cannot say.** Second personal operating principle. Tactical pattern: (a) ask a question, not a statement; (b) own the observation as your perception, not a judgment. "Lenny, you know what? I wonder if you missed an opportunity in that interview." Direct read for Kesava's commons + ledger writing — labels and observations beat verdicts. Same shape as Hamel/Shreya's open-coding rule: name what's wrong without categorizing it as Wrong-with-a-capital-W.

7. **Distinguish leader from manager.** Third operating principle. Reid Hoffman example: "I wouldn't hire you to manage McDonald's." Reid was secure enough to hear the feedback because his real operating principle was an environment of open feedback. Direct read for Kesava's clearPMM positioning: Kesava's leverage is leadership work (positioning, voice, taste), not management work; staff the management layer (project ops, deliverable shepherding) explicitly, don't conflate.

8. **Come back to the operating system.** Fourth operating principle. "Picture yourself spinning out of control. Come back to: this is the order I do things, this is how I get decisions made." Stability through ritual and common practices, not through control. Direct read for the b1/b2/b3 cadence and the 2-minute commit rule — the rituals ARE the operating system.

9. **Hypothesis-based coaching, not data-perfect coaching.** "Take some data, form a hypothesis, and then explore it with the person... a scientific hypothesis is a well-informed piece of intuition." Counter to the "wait until I have a million data points" management orthodoxy. Direct alignment with Hamel/Shreya's error-analysis rule: a few traces + a hypothesis beats waiting for statistical significance. Same shape across operating-people work and operating-AI work.

10. **Cadence diagnostics: too fast = no progress between reviews, too slow = stale content.** Stripe sometimes shifted QBRs to every-six-week reviews when product areas demanded it. "If the content you're reviewing seems stale, it's probably too slow." Also: don't let the metrics review become the strategy review by default — that means the strategy review isn't happening often enough. Direct check on Kesava's flywheel rhythm — does the daily AI digest become the weekly synthesis by default? Watch the substitution.

11. **Don't hire the silver-bullet COO. De-risk with a Head of Business Operations.** "I worry that it's become too much of a panacea... a lower risk strategy could be to bring in a head of business operations or a role that's sort of COO-like and try a couple of functions with someone." Same shape applies to clearPMM positioning: don't pitch "I'll be your fractional CMO" as a panacea; pitch a scoped engagement (e.g., positioning audit + earned-channel build) and let it grow into more if both sides want.

## Other notable threads

- **Communication is a strategic function, not extraneous.** Internal communications gets under-invested early; sales teams already do it well because they have to. "It actually is worth talking to smart sales leaders who've scaled and saying, what do you do that keeps everyone on the same page?" Cross-link to Reshma's enablement lane and to the team FAQ pattern.
- **Repeater-in-chief.** Leaders are repeaters. Use different channels — email, video, all-hands, async post — because different teammates absorb differently. "Just like a marketer, use different channels." Direct read for the JustCall flywheel internal narrative — Kesava can't just write the deck once.
- **Make the implicit explicit.** Meeting hygiene rule. Why are we meeting? Who's deciding? What's the criterion? Who's informed? Direct check on every commons + ledger entry.
- **Type 1 vs. Type 2 decisions (Bezos).** High-impact + irreversible vs. reversible. Match the process to the type. Don't drag a Type 2 through committee. For flywheel iteration: most variants are Type 2 — ship and roll back if needed.
- **Offsites yank people out of routine and imprint memory.** Group experience cements belief or plan. Direct read for the periodic Kesava + Deepan + PMM-team working sessions; they're not optional.
- **Touchstones (mission / long-term goals / operating principles) as catnip.** "Examples and details and frameworks are catnip." Direct read for the flywheel pitch deck — the templates and rubrics are what travel, not the prose.

## Why this matters for flywheel

Three direct loads. First, the house metaphor is the cleanest external scaffold for what the flywheel substrate IS at the operating-system layer. Foundation (positioning + ICP + voice + kill-list) → Supporting beams (goals, QBRs, content cadence) → Mechanicals (sweep cadence, AI digest, daily inbox, ledger). The pitch maps cleanly. When Deepan asks "but what is the substrate, structurally?" the answer is CHJ's house, with the AI compute layer wired into the mechanicals.

Second, the "force for positive momentum" rule + decision rights framework gives the flywheel team a single operating norm that survives any reorg. Q1 was paralyzed by ownership ambiguity. Q2 needs the explicit rule: when in doubt, you're the decision-maker, ship the variant, log it, course-correct. CHJ's framing makes it a career-positive rule, not a captain-overreach rule.

Third, the personal operating principles section is the calibration for Kesava's voice across commons, ledger, and outbound. Self-awareness, say-the-thing, leader-vs-manager, come-back-to-the-OS. They map almost 1:1 onto Kesava's existing memory rules (anti-fabrication, kill-list, captain rotation, evaluate before draft). Useful external validation that the operating norms Kesava is enforcing are not idiosyncratic — they are the same norms a high-functioning Stripe-class company runs on.
