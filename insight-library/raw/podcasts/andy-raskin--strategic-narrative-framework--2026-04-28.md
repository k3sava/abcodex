# Andy Raskin — Strategic Narrative Framework (Old Game to New Game)

## Summary

Raskin is the author of "The Greatest Sales Deck I've Ever Seen" (2M views, Zuora post). His framework: instead of "problem-solution-why-we're-better," craft a narrative: "The world shifted from [old game] to [new game], winners already play the new game, here's what it takes to win." A narrative spine for positioning.

## 5 load-bearing insights

1. **The "old game to new game" beats problem-solution-comparison.** Traditional pitch: "CRM is hard to install → we're easier → buy us." Salesforce's pitch: "Software era is over → cloud era is here → join the winners." Same category (CRM) but first pitch commoditizes on ease, second creates a movement. The narrative structure makes the buyer the hero, not positioning your product as the hero.

2. **Name the shift concisely.** Zuora: transactions → subscriptions. Gong: opinions → reality. Salesforce: software → cloud. Airbnb: "belong anywhere." These are 1-3 words. The naming is hard (you lose completeness to gain memorability) but essential. Wilkinson's echo: "fish where the fish are" / undermonetized verticals require naming the shift (e.g., SMB intent + vertical GTM = new game vs. broad enterprise ACV).

3. **Show winners already playing the new game.** Stakes-setting: "Here's the life-and-death split." Luke wants to go home (old game: farm boy). Obiwan kills the aunt and uncle (stakes appear). Now Luke joins the rebellion (new game). In sales: "Airbnb, Box, Stripe — they're all already doing this. Companies stuck in the old game are dying." (Show the longevity decline of Fortune 500 as proof.)

4. **Obstacles = not problems, but gates to winning the new game.** Problem framing ("sales teams lack insights") = commoditizes the solution. Obstacle framing ("how do you get reality into all your sales calls?") = positions the tool as necessary to win the new game. Zuora's list: How do you measure lifetime value? How do you track changing preferences? These are the "monsters" (Star Wars, LOTR) that stand between where they are and the promised land.

5. **The object of the new game is the rallying cry / mission.** Zuora: "Turn every customer into a subscriber." Airbnb: "Belong anywhere" / "Live there." This is asymptotically unachievable (you never literally "belong everywhere") but it frames the buyer's mission, not Zuora's product mission. When the object becomes clear, the obstacles + your gifts (features) fall into narrative coherence.

## Other notable threads

- Strategic narrative vs. hero's journey: Raskin decouples storytelling-as-skill from strategic-narrative-as-structure. He's not teaching screenwriting; he's teaching how to reframe the buyer as the protagonist in a world that's changing.
- Narrative work: Started as coding (dot com, bad pitch), read screenwriting books to fix investor rejection, applied narrative structure to business pitches. One post (2M views) proved CEOs would budget for "narrative consulting" despite disbelief that this was a service.
- 360Learning example: "Collaborative learning" was a category name; shifting to "upskill from within" (Google's internal experts as educators) named the shift from top-down to peer-driven learning, reframing the narrative entirely.

## Why this matters for flywheel

A modern positioning challenge: don't sell "AI-powered sales automation"; sell the shift from "humans do sales ops, then AI helps them" (old game) to "AI does ops, humans decide taste/strategy" (new game). Raskin's framework is the tool to name the shift, show winners, name the obstacles, and define the rallying cry.

This also applies to internal flywheel narrative: "Old game: PMM + demand-gen work separately. New game: PMM owns AI-native ops, demand-gen focuses on positioning + channels."
