# Andrew Wilkinson — Niche Markets, Lazy Leadership, and AI Automation

## Why this is in the wiki

Wilkinson has founded or been involved with 75 businesses (hitting every business model). His core thesis: successful startups find undercompeted niches where customers have real willingness to pay. The "fish where the fish are" metaphor directly parallels the flywheel's positioning-discovery loop. Equally load-bearing: his "lazy leadership" — ruthlessly delegating non-core tasks to avoid the job-versus-business trap.

## 5 load-bearing insights for flywheel

1. **"Fish where the fish are" disciplines the market choice.** Wilkinson's biggest mistake: copying failed business models (he lost $10M on Flow, competing with venture-backed Asana). The heuristic: if many people have repeatedly failed, there's a structural moat against you, not a whitespace. Counter-intuitive advice: starting small and boring (government form-filling software doing $30M/year) beats fighting venture-backed companies in sexy categories. The gym analogy: don't deadlift 300 pounds on day one.

2. **Willingness-to-pay scales with unfair advantage, not passion.** A marketer charging $1K/month to restaurants can charge $5K/month to realtors with bigger budgets, without changing the service. The variable: understanding where the money is (realtors sell $20-50K houses; restaurants operate on thin margins). Requisite: spend time in the industry before you start it. This is anti-idea, pro-research.

3. **Distinguish business from job via delegation, not passion.** A pressure-washing business is a job if you're the only pressure-washer, but a business if you drive leads and hire employees. Wilkinson calls this "lazy leadership" — the moment you can hire people to handle the work you hate, you've pivoted from job to business. Most founders fail because they operate from "Protestant work ethic" (I must do everything) instead of "how do I be Teflon for tasks I hate?"

4. **Narrative matters more than product.** Wilkinson and a friend pitched the same business twice: once in the dot-com era (rejected 1/10), once with a screenwriting-inspired narrative structure (got a term sheet). They changed zero product; only the pitch story changed. This validates Andy Raskin's strategic narrative thesis across non-SaaS categories.

5. **AI-native work: $200/month employee, 24/7, never-tired.** Wilkinson uses Lindy to build 4-5 AI agents per workflow (email, calendar, meeting, CRM, scheduler). Example: inbox agent auto-archives threads where he already chimed in (saves 20% email volume), auto-tags time-sensitive items, offers multiple-choice responses for simple decisions. His assistant cost: eliminated. This is the operational model for a single-founder operating at scale (100+ portfolio companies).

## Other notable threads

- Things (boutique task manager, 20-year bootstrap, <10 people) succeeded vs. Asana's maximalist approach. Both won, but in different games. Wilkinson prefers the Things founder's life (headphones-on, high margin, recurring revenue).
- Bootstrapped-to-$300M-revenue hypothesis: scale doesn't require VC money if you pick non-competitive markets. Network effects (Letterboxd, Dribble) are the primary moat Tiny acquires.
- CEO hiring discipline: "If I think about firing them even once, I should fire immediately." Superstar hires feel impossible to lose. Mediocre hires trigger the question repeatedly before 12 months, almost always failing.

## Why this matters for flywheel

The flywheel's bet: AI substrates (Commons, contextdb) allow a small team to operate at enterprise scale without hiring. Wilkinson's Lindy automation model is the same principle — delegation to AI instead of hiring. "Lazy leadership" here means: how do we make the humans' highest-value judgment tasks (positioning, messaging, taste, strategy) non-delegable, and push routine work (research synthesis, draft-writing, cross-platform distribution) to the agent layer?

This also validates the positioning discipline: avoid crowded categories (B2B SaaS saas, consumer fitness). Look for undermonetized buyer segments (SMB with intent, regional champions, vertical-specific GTM).
