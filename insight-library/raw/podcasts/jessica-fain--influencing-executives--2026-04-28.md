# Jessica Fain — How to influence executives

## Why this is in the wiki
Jessica was Slack's chief of staff to two consecutive CPOs (April Underwood, Tamar Yehoshua), then ran core product. She has the cleanest articulation of what makes upward influence work: treat the executive like a user, do discovery, align with their incentives, kill your own ideas to build trust. Direct relevance for the flywheel CXO pitch and for how teammates pitch ideas to Kesava.

## 12 load-bearing insights for flywheel

1. **"Influence is about increasing the odds that your good ideas survive."** Jessica draws a sharp line: politics is manipulating outcomes for personal gain; influence is making good ideas durable. "If we treat our stakeholder conversations as discovery interviews as a way to strengthen our ideas, then we end up in a much, much better place." Reframe for Kesava in the CXO pitch: not "selling flywheel," but doing discovery on what Deepan and the CFO need flywheel to be.

2. **"It's not my fault, but it is my problem."** From Annie Pearl. The product-leader's stance toward executive disagreement: never "they don't get it" — always "I haven't found the framing yet." Direct rule for any flywheel pitch that doesn't land — the next move is more discovery, not more conviction.

3. **Executive calendars are strobe lights.** "They may not have gone to the bathroom today... they have not had the time, the energy, the wherewithal to center your problems and you have to help them get into that mindset." Take 30 seconds at the top: why are we here, what happened last time, what are we deciding today. Then stop. Same rule applies to pitching Deepan: he hasn't reloaded the flywheel context since the last meeting; reload it for him in 30 seconds.

4. **Stop asking "what's top of mind?"** "It's become this trope... it's become generic, uninteresting, neutralized." Better: "Tell me what the board is pushing you on." "What pressures are you facing?" "What's the most urgent priority you're scared about messing up?" These get to the actual emotional driver. Marketing analog: ask Deepan what the CFO is pushing him on, not what's top of mind.

5. **Execs want to be successful too.** "How is the metric they're trying to move, the OKR they're responsible for, the board pressure they're under, going to be improved by the thing you're proposing?" Ground the flywheel pitch in revenue-per-employee math (Kesava already has) and connect it explicitly to whatever the CFO is anchored on. The CFO's success is the entry point.

6. **Show your work, but in the appendix.** Don't lead with discovery process; lead with the recommendation. But have the alternates ready. "Stuart plus two more" — when Stuart had a strong design opinion, Slack designers shipped his version plus two of their own. This is the right shape for any flywheel proposal back to Kesava: the recommended path plus two real alternates, not one option.

7. **Take the bait when execs drop subtle threads.** "I wonder if..." "Have you considered..." Most people don't act on those; the best people respond within an hour. Webflow's Kev shipped a 30-min loom on design-review tiering after a one-line ask from Rachel. For flywheel teammates: when Kesava drops a thread in a chat or a debrief, the right move is to ship a draft within an hour, not wait for the next standup.

8. **"That's so interesting. What led you to believe that?"** Verbatim phrase for disarming a leader you disagree with. Treats the disagreement as a discovery interview. Useful for Kesava in retrospectives with the team and useful for substrate prompts that get model output that conflicts with VoC data.

9. **Shrink the change.** From the book *Switch*. "How do you make it so much smaller so that it's an experiment? It's a one-week proof of concept... something small that can get people over the hump of 'I'm not going to invest six months for this project if I don't know if it's going to work.'" Direct read for flywheel demo strategy: don't pitch the year-long substrate; pitch a 2-week scoped bet with a defined check-in date.

10. **Kill things to build trust.** "One of the biggest things you can do to build trust is kill things, deprioritize things. That is a very, very senior way of thinking, and it shows that you have the same aligned incentives as the executive." Counter-intuitive but high-leverage. For flywheel: when a substrate-built bet underperforms, kill it loudly in front of Deepan. The credibility for the next bet compounds from that.

11. **The risk question.** "What are the outcomes you're most afraid of? What would a failure state be for you in this experiment?" Get the exec to name their failure mode out loud. Then design the milestone check-ins around defusing those specific risks. For the CFO pitch on flywheel: ask Gaurav what failure looks like to him; design the demo to address that exact picture, not the generic skeptic.

12. **Agents as junior colleagues — onboard them like one.** "How would we onboard new junior team members who don't already understand our product philosophy? How do we codify that for ourselves? What's important to me as a product leader that I want to inculcate into anything that's developed for my team?" This is the contextDB and substrate-soul thesis articulated by an external operator. Validates the architecture flywheel is already building.

## Other notable threads

- **The Minto Pyramid for exec presentations.** Lenny names it; Jessica agrees with a gloss: still calibrate to the exec's preferred format. Some execs want bullets, some want dense docs with silent reading time, some want a Loom. Ask. Same applies to internal flywheel comms — Deepan vs CFO vs PMs need different formats.
- **Themes for discussion at the top of a doc.** When a leader peppers your doc with 100 comments, bubble up the spiciest 3 at the top under "themes for discussion" — that's where the live conversation should live; the rest can be answered async.
- **Trained a custom GPT on past product reviews** to anticipate the CPO's likely pushback. The team expected PMs to run their PRDs through it. Direct flywheel pattern: a "Deepan-simulator" GPT trained on slack history, decision memos, retros — the team can red-team pitches before delivering them.
- **The "first the guests" family motto.** Service mindset; transferable to how the substrate should treat teammates — substrate is the host, teammate is the guest, friction goes to substrate not to teammate.
- **AI doesn't kill PRDs — it makes strategy clarity 10x more important.** "If you have an empowered EPD organization, that clarity of what you believe... is the mechanism for letting people go and run at as fast a velocity as they're capable of." Counter to the "PRDs are dead" Twitter take. Same logic for flywheel: clearer brand voice + ICP + positioning are MORE important when 100 agents can ship in parallel, not less.
- **Distribution + trust as the new bottleneck.** Jessica wants Lenny to do a whole episode on distribution. Echo of Evan Spiegel's distribution thesis from the same week — converging consensus from operators who've shipped at scale.

## Why this matters for flywheel

Three direct loads. First, the discovery-not-conviction frame is the right shape for the CXO demo and any future board-facing flywheel pitch. The pitch is not "here's what flywheel is, please fund it." The pitch is "here are the failure modes you fear most about marketing's AI shift; here's how flywheel addresses each one, with a 2-week scoped bet that proves the substrate works on one of them. Pick the one you want."

Second, the kill-things-to-build-trust principle is the missing move in the current flywheel narrative. Right now the bets are framed as wins-only ("UNIQUE CR went up", "iter-54 SSR shipped"). Adding a "what we killed and why" section to the cadence — bets the substrate generated and a human killed — gives Deepan and the CFO concrete evidence that the substrate doesn't just spam more work, it sharpens taste.

Third, the agents-as-junior-colleagues frame is a clean external articulation of why contextDB + substrate souls matter. When Jessica asks "how would we onboard new junior team members?" she's describing exactly what flywheel is building. Use the phrasing in the next pitch — it lands more cleanly than "agentic substrate."
