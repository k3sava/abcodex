# Jessica Fain — How to influence executives

## Summary
Jessica was Slack's chief of staff to two consecutive CPOs (April Underwood, Tamar Yehoshua), then ran core product. Her articulation of upward influence: treat the executive like a user, do discovery, align with their incentives, kill your own ideas to build trust.

## 12 load-bearing insights

1. **"Influence is about increasing the odds that your good ideas survive."** Jessica draws a sharp line: politics is manipulating outcomes for personal gain; influence is making good ideas durable. "If we treat our stakeholder conversations as discovery interviews as a way to strengthen our ideas, then we end up in a much, much better place."

2. **"It's not my fault, but it is my problem."** From Annie Pearl. The product-leader's stance toward executive disagreement: never "they don't get it" — always "I haven't found the framing yet."

3. **Executive calendars are strobe lights.** "They may not have gone to the bathroom today... they have not had the time, the energy, the wherewithal to center your problems and you have to help them get into that mindset." Take 30 seconds at the top: why are we here, what happened last time, what are we deciding today. Then stop.

4. **Stop asking "what's top of mind?"** "It's become this trope... it's become generic, uninteresting, neutralized." Better: "Tell me what the board is pushing you on." "What pressures are you facing?" "What's the most urgent priority you're scared about messing up?"

5. **Execs want to be successful too.** "How is the metric they're trying to move, the OKR they're responsible for, the board pressure they're under, going to be improved by the thing you're proposing?"

6. **Show your work, but in the appendix.** Don't lead with discovery process; lead with the recommendation. But have the alternates ready. "Stuart plus two more" — when Stuart had a strong design opinion, Slack designers shipped his version plus two of their own.

7. **Take the bait when execs drop subtle threads.** "I wonder if..." "Have you considered..." Most people don't act on those; the best people respond within an hour. Webflow's Kev shipped a 30-min loom on design-review tiering after a one-line ask from Rachel.

8. **"That's so interesting. What led you to believe that?"** Verbatim phrase for disarming a leader you disagree with. Treats the disagreement as a discovery interview.

9. **Shrink the change.** From the book *Switch*. "How do you make it so much smaller so that it's an experiment? It's a one-week proof of concept... something small that can get people over the hump of 'I'm not going to invest six months for this project if I don't know if it's going to work.'"

10. **Kill things to build trust.** "One of the biggest things you can do to build trust is kill things, deprioritize things. That is a very, very senior way of thinking, and it shows that you have the same aligned incentives as the executive."

11. **The risk question.** "What are the outcomes you're most afraid of? What would a failure state be for you in this experiment?" Get the exec to name their failure mode out loud. Then design the milestone check-ins around defusing those specific risks.

12. **Agents as junior colleagues — onboard them like one.** "How would we onboard new junior team members who don't already understand our product philosophy? How do we codify that for ourselves? What's important to me as a product leader that I want to inculcate into anything that's developed for my team?"

## Other notable threads

- **The Minto Pyramid for exec presentations.** Lenny names it; Jessica agrees with a gloss: still calibrate to the exec's preferred format. Some execs want bullets, some want dense docs with silent reading time, some want a Loom. Ask.
- **Themes for discussion at the top of a doc.** When a leader peppers your doc with 100 comments, bubble up the spiciest 3 at the top under "themes for discussion" — that's where the live conversation should live; the rest can be answered async.
- **Trained a custom GPT on past product reviews** to anticipate the CPO's likely pushback. The team expected PMs to run their PRDs through it.
- **The "first the guests" family motto.** Service mindset.
- **AI doesn't kill PRDs — it makes strategy clarity 10x more important.** "If you have an empowered EPD organization, that clarity of what you believe... is the mechanism for letting people go and run at as fast a velocity as they're capable of."
- **Distribution + trust as the new bottleneck.** Jessica wants Lenny to do a whole episode on distribution.
