# Qasar Younis — Physical AI, radical pragmatism, build quietly

## Why this is in the wiki
Applied Intuition is a $15B AI company most people have never heard of. 18 of top 20 automakers, plus DoD, plus mining, plus construction. Qasar built it for a decade in stealth on purpose. Two threads matter for flywheel: (a) the build-quietly counter-narrative against PMM "build in public" defaults; (b) the framing of AI as "intelligence put into things that already exist" — the cleanest articulation yet of the substrate-not-replacement thesis.

## 10 load-bearing insights for flywheel

1. **"Our best work is done alone and quietly."** Counter to "build in public" orthodoxy. "Every minute you're writing something for public consumption, you're not focusing your very limited time you have on your customers and your product." Direct read for clearPMM/Kesava: external comms are real cost, not free leverage. Spend them sparingly on the highest-payoff signals.

2. **Caveat: only build quietly if you have a network already.** "I can do that because I'm known in the ecosystem... if you don't have a network and you can get a following, that's a fantastic way to recruit people." Naval told him fame is a tool. Pragmatic, not dogmatic. For Kesava: the LinkedIn ship queue is doing exactly the right thing because the network needs rebuilding for exit; for the team, the substrate moves quietly.

3. **"The core root of fear is misunderstanding."** People fear AI because they see nunchuck robots in viral clips and don't know how they work. Welding robots in factories don't scare anyone — same physical capability, but the technology is legible. Direct frame for flywheel internal rollout: every teammate who's anxious about the substrate gets less anxious by understanding it. "Spend time to understand it and you will quickly see the limitations."

4. **Intelligence into things that already exist.** "It's actually just putting intelligence into things that already exist all around us." The Tesla FSD path beats the humanoid path because the engineering of the car is done; only the intelligence is new. Flywheel parallel: the substrate puts intelligence into the marketing org's existing artifacts (decks, LPs, emails, retros), not new agentic-SaaS surfaces. Lower bar to value.

5. **Radical pragmatism as the company value.** "All of our company values can be reduced to these two words." Two-word ethos beats a values manifesto. For flywheel: pick a similar two-word phrase and protect it. ("Operator candor" or "Evidence-first" are candidates already in the wiki.)

6. **Stamina trumps intellect (Aaron Levie/Jeetu echo).** Not in this transcript explicitly, but related. Qasar's version: "The first time you do a startup, just imagine the first three years it's a zero. Rid yourself of the expectation that it's going to be successful. You're a craftsperson... If this was a woodworking podcast and you said the first table you built was wobbly, you wouldn't say go work at Crate & Barrel. You'd say, oh, that's the first table." Reframe for flywheel bets that miss — ship the next bet, the muscle is what compounds.

7. **The reset signal.** "If the information I'm getting from the market is not informing me on a more and more specific path, I would consider resetting." If a flywheel bet generates noise but not signal-getting-sharper after two cycles, that's the reset signal — change foundation, not features.

8. **AI is just-in-time, not job-stealing, in trades that already can't hire.** "These industries, they need autonomy and it couldn't come soon enough... the average age of a farmer is in their late 50s." The labor-replacement panic misframes the actual market — most physical-labor verticals are already failing to hire. For flywheel pitch: the substrate isn't about reducing PMM headcount, it's about absorbing the work that the team can't backfill anyway (vacant Krishna role, Reshma stretch).

9. **Hold contradictory truths simultaneously.** "It's deeply very, very important and you should give it your all and it's also not that important. That's a really hard thing to reconcile and keep in balance." Foundational stance for any operator under pressure. Direct stance for Kesava during the exit-clock window.

10. **Caloric expenditure as the strategy frame.** Implicit in his "where do you have permission to play" thinking — every dollar of effort spent broadly is dollar not spent deepening. Flywheel implication: don't widen the substrate's surface area until the spine works on one workstream end-to-end.

## Other notable threads

- **Don't compare Chinese state-backed companies to American for-profit ones.** Huawei = "China's ambition", a quarter Communist Party members, not a profit-driven private company. Comparing Apple to Huawei is comparing Apple to the Chinese government. Useful frame for any "what about competitor X" market analysis.
- **Self-driving is just a robot you don't call a robot.** People accept self-driving but fear humanoids. Same intelligence, different framing. Implication for flywheel: name the substrate something legible, avoid "agentic AI marketing platform" rhetoric.
- **Read history books to defuse AI anxiety.** Industrial Revolution literature is dense and the through-line is positive net-of-disruption. Useful frame for teammate anxiety; pair with the Snap "humanity dictates technology adoption" thread.
- **Three-year first-startup zero.** Founder muscle compounds across companies. For Kesava: clearPMM is a multi-attempt arc; the first six clients are tuition.

## Why this matters for flywheel

Two main loads. First, the "intelligence into things that already exist" framing is the cleanest single-sentence pitch the substrate has yet had. Use it verbatim in the next CXO deck: the flywheel doesn't replace the marketing team's tools, it puts intelligence into the docs, decks, retros, and emails the team already produces. Lower friction, higher legibility.

Second, the build-quietly counter-narrative is permission for Kesava's current operating posture — heavy internal substrate, light external posting except when ship-queue demands it. Validates the pacing.

Less load-bearing for flywheel directly, but useful for the JustCall product side: the labor-shortage-is-the-real-driver thesis explains why JustCall's inbox/AI/agent positioning lands harder in trades-and-services verticals than in tech-first ones. Echo of Reshma's vertical research.
