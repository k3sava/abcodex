# Qasar Younis — Physical AI, radical pragmatism, build quietly

## Summary
Applied Intuition is a $15B AI company most people have never heard of. 18 of top 20 automakers, plus DoD, plus mining, plus construction. Qasar built it for a decade in stealth on purpose. Two threads: (a) the build-quietly counter-narrative; (b) AI as "intelligence put into things that already exist."

## 10 load-bearing insights

1. **"Our best work is done alone and quietly."** Counter to "build in public" orthodoxy. "Every minute you're writing something for public consumption, you're not focusing your very limited time you have on your customers and your product."

2. **Caveat: only build quietly if you have a network already.** "I can do that because I'm known in the ecosystem... if you don't have a network and you can get a following, that's a fantastic way to recruit people." Naval told him fame is a tool. Pragmatic, not dogmatic.

3. **"The core root of fear is misunderstanding."** People fear AI because they see nunchuck robots in viral clips and don't know how they work. Welding robots in factories don't scare anyone — same physical capability, but the technology is legible. "Spend time to understand it and you will quickly see the limitations."

4. **Intelligence into things that already exist.** "It's actually just putting intelligence into things that already exist all around us." The Tesla FSD path beats the humanoid path because the engineering of the car is done; only the intelligence is new.

5. **Radical pragmatism as the company value.** "All of our company values can be reduced to these two words." Two-word ethos beats a values manifesto.

6. **The first three years of a startup are a zero.** "The first time you do a startup, just imagine the first three years it's a zero. Rid yourself of the expectation that it's going to be successful. You're a craftsperson... If this was a woodworking podcast and you said the first table you built was wobbly, you wouldn't say go work at Crate & Barrel. You'd say, oh, that's the first table."

7. **The reset signal.** "If the information I'm getting from the market is not informing me on a more and more specific path, I would consider resetting." If two cycles of work produce noise but not signal-getting-sharper, change foundation, not features.

8. **AI is just-in-time, not job-stealing, in trades that already can't hire.** "These industries, they need autonomy and it couldn't come soon enough... the average age of a farmer is in their late 50s." The labor-replacement panic misframes the actual market — most physical-labor verticals are already failing to hire.

9. **Hold contradictory truths simultaneously.** "It's deeply very, very important and you should give it your all and it's also not that important. That's a really hard thing to reconcile and keep in balance." Foundational stance for any operator under pressure.

10. **Caloric expenditure as the strategy frame.** Implicit in his "where do you have permission to play" thinking — every dollar of effort spent broadly is dollar not spent deepening.

## Other notable threads

- **Don't compare Chinese state-backed companies to American for-profit ones.** Huawei = "China's ambition", a quarter Communist Party members, not a profit-driven private company. Comparing Apple to Huawei is comparing Apple to the Chinese government.
- **Self-driving is just a robot you don't call a robot.** People accept self-driving but fear humanoids. Same intelligence, different framing.
- **Read history books to defuse AI anxiety.** Industrial Revolution literature is dense and the through-line is positive net-of-disruption.
- **Three-year first-startup zero.** Founder muscle compounds across companies.
