# Jenny Wen — The design process is dead

## Summary
Jenny is living proof that the "supporting function" (design) had to abandon its sacred process when engineering's velocity exploded. The same waveform is hitting PMM. Her three hiring archetypes (block-shape generalist, deep T specialist, craft-newgrad) apply broadly.

## 12 load-bearing insights for flywheel

1. **"Trust the design process" is dead.** The diverge-converge-research-mock-iterate loop, treated as gospel, doesn't survive engineers spinning up "seven Claudes." Jenny's keynote on this got the most resonance — and the most backlash from people who invested careers in the old process. PMM analog: the "messaging house → positioning doc → review cycle → enablement deck" cascade is also dead.

2. **Design work has stratified into two modes.** Mode 1: support implementation/execution — engineers ship scrappy versions, designer does last-mile polish + jams with them. Mode 2: vision/direction — but compressed from 2-5-10-year visions to 3-6 months, often a prototype rather than a deck. Both modes coexist; the middle (long mocks) collapsed. PMM equivalent: kill long-form positioning docs, keep short-cycle direction-setting + last-mile polish on the substrate's outputs.

3. **The pie chart shifted.** A few years ago: 60-70% mocking/prototyping, 20% engineer collaboration, 10% coordination. Today: 30-40% mocking, 30-40% jamming/pairing with engineers, ~20% implementation in code. The role expanded into code, not retreated from it.

4. **Build trust through speed.** Anthropic ships features as "research preview" — labels them clearly, then continuously iterates on user feedback. "The way you lose trust around quality is releasing early and then nothing ever happens." The promise is iteration cadence, not initial polish. Direct flywheel rule: every external send is a research preview; never ship without a follow-up cycle promised.

5. **You can't mock non-deterministic AI products.** "You can't make a clickable prototype with these models — you have to use the actual models underneath and see people try it with their use cases." Use cases get *discovered*, not designed. Implication: every flywheel artifact must be tested against real Claude output, not a script.

6. **Slack as gold-mine substrate.** "The best AI news is internal Slack at one of these labs." Jenny treats company Slack as a discovery feed — spotting what's coming, which prototypes have energy, which ideas might land on her plate. Flywheel parallel: Commons rooms + ledger as the discovery substrate teammates should mine, not just push to.

7. **The illegibility framework (from Evan Armacost).** 2x2: founder × idea legibility. The interesting square is when the idea is illegible — frontier, doesn't make sense yet — but there's energy around it. Jenny's job-as-internal-VC: "What are the ideas with energy that I don't yet understand? Dive deeper." The cloud studio prototype → skills framework → co-work form-factor is her example. Flywheel use: maintain a watch-list of internal Claude experiments with energy but no shape yet; convert to outputs.

8. **Three hiring archetypes for the new world.** (a) **Strong generalist / "block shape"** — 80th percentile in many things, not just T-shape (rare). (b) **Deep specialist** — top 10% in one thing (e.g. designer who's 50% software engineer). (c) **Craft new-grad** — early-career, humble, eager, unburdened by old processes; the most overlooked archetype. Most companies over-hire seniors; new grads can be more adaptive. PMM mapping: Sourav/Reshma = generalists; Aditi/Sulagna = deep specialists; under-used hire would be a craft-newgrad PMM.

9. **Managers should go back to IC.** Jenny voluntarily returned to IC at Anthropic from a Figma director role. "I wasn't sure middle management is safe in the future." The IC year gave her hard skills she wouldn't have picked up managing — and the empathy to lead better when she manages again.

10. **"Low-leverage time" is high-leverage when it's the senior leader doing it.** The classic "delegate everything anyone else can do" advice is wrong now. Senior leaders dogfooding the product, repro-ing bugs, filing PRs, hand-making anniversary cards — these things are high-leverage *because of who's doing them*. Mike Krieger files PRs himself.

11. **Roasting as psychological-safety signal.** Jenny encourages teams to feel comfortable poking fun at her ("they make fun of phrases I say at crits"). It signals trust + lack of fear without crossing into friendship. Pair with high standards: "tough parent" model — always there, but always raising the bar. Combines with Radical Candor: care personally + challenge directly.

12. **AI taste WILL get good.** Jenny's heretical concession: "I think we might be holding on to that a little bit too much. AI's sense of taste will get better. At the end of the day, someone has to decide what gets built — but maybe not because their taste is uniquely human, just because someone has to be accountable." This reframes the human-in-the-loop debate: it's about *liability and decision ownership*, not unique creative judgment. Flywheel pitch: humans hold the decision seat, not the taste seat.

## Other notable threads

- **Figma still alive.** For exploring 8-10 different directions (linear coding tools collapse to one direction). For micro-typography/visual-detail iteration. The IDE may shift from engineers to designers/PMs as engineers move to terminals/agents.
- **All-Claude tool stack.** Cloud chat → migrating to co-work for longer tasks. Cloud Code in VS Code IDE for frontend tweaks. Cloud Code via Slack/mobile for tagging-Claude-to-fix-an-icon — "you pick up the PR and it's done."
- **Co-work was NOT 10 days.** The viral "10 days" referred to final assembly. Months of internal prototypes preceded it (todo list form factors, multiple-choice question UIs, use-case onboarding flows). Pattern: long discovery, sudden shipping window, ship everything-all-at-once.
- **Claude is not yet a hireable designer.** "First pass and a bunch of options? Pretty good. Strong generalist, deep specialist, craft-newgrad? Not there yet." But "it's gotten a lot better in the last year."
- **Talking is a beautiful UX for variable intelligence.** Kevin Weil's point: talking scales across IQ ranges in a way no other UI does. Why chat persists.
- **For new designers/marketers breaking in: build stuff.** "Don't feel limited by little experience — they're often unburdened by it." Socratica community-style projects (the Claude robot, the googly-eyes-on-a-Boston-bus project) > theoretical school portfolios.

## Why this matters

Three concrete moves applicable to any PMM team:

1. **Two-mode stratification.** A lot of PMM work today is "long-form positioning + messaging docs + decks" — the dead middle. Kill the middle and split PMM into (a) substrate last-mile polish + jamming with the substrate's outputs, and (b) 3–6 month direction-setting prototypes.

2. **Illegibility framework as a discovery routine.** Scan internal channels for *illegible energy* — projects the team is buzzing about but can't articulate. That energy is the next loop, not the legible asks already on the ledger.

3. **Three hiring archetypes as a roster-gap diagnosis.** Most teams over-index on mid-T generalists or deep specialists; the missing archetype is the craft-newgrad — eager, AI-native-by-default, no baked-in rituals.
