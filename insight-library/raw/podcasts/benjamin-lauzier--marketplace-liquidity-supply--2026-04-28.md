# Benjamin Lauzier on Marketplace Liquidity and Supply Scaling

## Why this is in the wiki
Ben built Lyft's driver supply to reach 1% of US workers. His thesis on marketplace liquidity as the core metric and supply-first strategy applies to any two-sided network. The Lyft mentor program exemplifies low-cost, peer-driven supply scaling.

## Insights
1. **Marketplace liquidity before marketplace dynamics** — Pre-PMF, stop theorizing marketplace ratios. Pick the hardest side (90% of time: supply), nail PMF there first. Use "crutches" like Craigslist to hack the other side temporarily. Fill rate (intentful demand converting to transaction) is the output metric.

2. **Market health metrics > liquidity metrics** — Track fill rate, but drive on the proxies that predict it. For Lyft: ETA. For Thumbtack: available plumbers in neighborhood. Find your threshold (e.g., "if 2min ETA, 90% convert") and optimize that instead.

3. **Supply control backfires if you ignore intent** — Thumbtack tested selling bookings (direct, not leads). Data said +20% driver earnings, but drivers felt control loss (no "thrill of sale," perceived less hustle). Lyft saw same with volatile earnings smoothing. Market forces beat command.

4. **Manage via guardrails, not control** — High-bar quality curation, coaching tools, rating systems for supply below threshold. Selective managed moves (Lyft rental fleet for carless drivers) work when surgical, not when universal.

5. **Peer mentorship beats HR overhead** — Lyft paid top drivers $35/mentor session (not DMV-style offices). Mentors shared routes, tips, contact info. Community advocacy > marketing copy. 10x efficiency vs. Uber's office-based onboarding.

## Why this matters
Supply-first positioning, fill-rate discipline, and peer-mentorship mechanics scale without command overhead — useful for any team building two-sided supply.
