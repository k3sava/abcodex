# April Dunford — Positioning Authority, 200+ Companies

**Why this is in wiki:**
April is the world's foremost positioning expert (200+ B2B tech companies positioned). *Obviously Awesome* is the canonical positioning textbook. This episode distills her core finding: **weak positioning leaks across the entire pipeline** (awareness → consideration → sales → churn) and manifests as "sluggishness" in the middle (reps need 3 calls to make the light come on) or churn post-sale (buyer expected something different). Positioning is not messaging; it's business strategy anchored in how you win against alternatives.

---

## The Positioning Problem (Invisible Until It's Catastrophic)

Weak positioning doesn't show up as one metric. It spreads:

1. **Top of funnel:** Low response to marketing. People don't get what you are.
2. **Middle of funnel:** Sluggishness. Reps spend 3+ calls repeating themselves.
3. **Bottom of funnel:** Churn. Buyer gets the product and says "wait, this isn't what I thought."

**The signal April watches:** Sit in on sales calls. If reps say "could you back up and say that again?" — positioning problem.

All 6 of April's companies that got acquired had strong positioning. The causal chain: Positioning → clarity → faster sales → shorter sales cycles → lower CAC.

---

## Positioning ≠ Messaging

Positioning is the job you're claiming to do *relative to alternatives*. Messaging is how you talk about it.

Help Scout example:
- **Alternative 1:** Shared inbox (easy but grows into feature hell).
- **Alternative 2:** Help desk software (full-featured but impersonal, designed for cost-cutting).
- **Help Scout positioning:** "Customer service as a growth driver for digital businesses, with help desk features that don't feel corporate."

This is not a tagline. It's a *economic claim* — we deliver growth while others deliver cost savings.

## The Pitch Structure (Setup + Follow-Through)

April built a pitch architecture that *encodes* positioning:

### Setup (Not About You)
1. **Insight:** Your POV on the market (the "problem inside the problem").
   - *E.g.*, "Digital businesses see customer service as growth, not cost."
2. **Alternatives + Pros/Cons:** Paint the market. What's good/bad about other approaches?
   - Shared inbox: easy, but doesn't scale.
   - Help desk: scales, but impersonal.
3. **Perfect World:** "Can we agree the ideal solution would be X, Y, Z?"
   - Easy to use. Full-featured. Built for digital businesses.

**Goal:** By end of setup, prospect is either aligned or disqualified. If aligned, you've won 70% of the deal.

### Follow-Through (Prove It)
1. **Intro:** "We're Help Scout. We do X for Y."
2. **Differentiated Value:** Value → How we do it. Repeat 3x.
   - Value 1: Easy inbox. (Show it.)
   - Value 2: Scales. (Show assignments, prioritization.)
   - Value 3: Growth mindset. (Customers stay anonymous, not ticket numbers.)
3. **Proof:** Case study, third-party validation.
4. **Objections:** Address silent objections (hard to adopt, expensive, doesn't meet security needs).
5. **Ask:** Next step (POC, budget approval, stakeholder meeting).

---

## Handling Objections & Disqualification

**Silent objections** — they nod along but harbor doubts:
- "Sounds good, but I think it's hard to adopt."
- "Sounds good, but it's probably expensive."
- "Sounds good, but I don't know if it meets our security needs."

Address these at the end, not throughout (it breaks flow).

**If they disagree with your insight:**
Disqualify. If a prospect says "we see customer service as a cost center," Help Scout can't win. Zendesk will. Better to know now.

**The insight should resonate naturally** — it's why you built the product in the first place. If your target market doesn't agree with your POV, rethink your target, not your positioning.

## Onboarding as Positioning Proof

April's key insight: **Most founder insights are non-controversial for the right target.**

The Help Scout insight ("customer service is growth for digital businesses") resonates with Shopify sellers, SaaS founders, DTC brands. But not with enterprise call centers.

Positioning is not about appealing to everyone. It's about being crystalclear to your bullseye and owning that space.

---

## Operational checklist

1. **Sales team audit:** Have someone sit in on 5 sales calls. Do reps repeat themselves? Do prospects ask clarifying questions late? Positioning problem.
2. **Insight articulation:** What's the POV that justifies your product's existence? Not "we do X" but "because Y is changing, companies like you should think about [category] differently."
3. **Alternative map:** Who are the *real* alternatives? What do they do well/poorly?
4. **Perfect world:** Frame the ideal solution *before* talking about product.
5. **Pitch discipline:** Every rep uses the same structure (insight → alternatives → perfect world → demo → proof → objections → ask).
6. **Disqualification:** If a prospect doesn't buy the insight, disengage. Better to find 10 aligned prospects than to hammer on 50 misaligned ones.

## Other Threads

- Positioning is especially critical post-product-market-fit when you're competing for share in a crowded space (e.g., call center software post-Aircall).
- Good positioning is durable. If it works, you can use the same structure for 2-3 years (not constantly reinventing).
- B2B tech positioning is harder than B2C because B2B buying committees have contradictory needs (CFO wants cost control, COO wants features, CIO wants security).
