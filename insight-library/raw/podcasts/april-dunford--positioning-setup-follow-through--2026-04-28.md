# April Dunford: Positioning, Setup Follow-Through, Differentiation

## Why this is in the wiki

April has positioned 200+ B2B companies and is the author of two positioning books. Her core insight—positioning is not a naming exercise or tagline, but a repeatable structure that ties together insight, alternatives, and a "perfect world" vision—is load-bearing for flywheel's external market positioning. Her emphasis on "setup + follow-through" sales pitch structure (not features-first) and her framework for evaluating whether a claim is meaningful (does it differentiate?) apply directly to how flywheel should position AI-native marketing teams to buyers and to how the team should talk internally about the work.

## Load-bearing insights

### 1. Positioning is a structure, not a name or tagline
April's positioning architecture: (1) Insight (the problem nobody is solving or the frame nobody is using), (2) Alternatives (what customers are doing today instead), (3) Your solution (why you're different), (4) Perfect world (the outcome in the customer's terms, not your terms). This structure forces clarity. Flywheel application: instead of "AI-native marketing team" as a name, position as: Insight = humans can't scale repetitive work without burning out. Alternatives = hire more people, use generic tools, accept slow iteration. Solution = AI agents handle routine; humans do taste/strategy. Perfect world = 10x output per marketer, faster iteration, less burnout.

### 2. 40-60% of buyers say "no decision" (not "no to you")
April cites research: when you survey buyers who didn't buy, 40-60% say they made no purchase decision at all. They were not comparing you to competitors; they weren't convinced there was a problem worth solving. This flips the framing: your pitch must first establish that the problem is real and worth paying for. Flywheel implication: don't lead with "use our agents." Lead with "your marketing team is constrained by time, not talent. You're losing speed and iteration cycles. Here's why that matters for growth."

### 3. Differentiation must be based on what the customer cares about, not what you think is cool
April: "Many companies get excited about their technical differentiation (machine learning, architecture) but that's not what the buyer cares about. The buyer cares: will this solve my problem faster/cheaper/better?" Flywheel: resist the urge to lead with "we use Claude AI" or "we built a multi-agent system." Lead with "here's how we cut your content cycle from 3 weeks to 3 days." The technical substrate is the enabler, not the differentiator.

### 4. Sales pitch structure: Setup (establish the frame) → Follow-through (deliver on that frame)
April teaches a two-part pitch: (1) Setup = establish the problem and why the customer should care. (2) Follow-through = show how your solution uniquely solves that specific problem. Most B2B pitches jump to features (follow-through) without establishing setup. Buyers check out. Flywheel: setup is "your team is stuck optimizing single channels; you're not iterating on narrative, positioning, bundling." Follow-through is "here's how AI lets you run 3x more experiments."

### 5. The "Help Scout" example: one specific customer, not generic personas
April cites her work on Help Scout: instead of positioning as "customer support software," they positioned as "Help Scout is the alternative to expensive enterprise ticketing systems for companies that want to delight customers without hiring a huge support team." Notice: specific alternative (enterprise ticketing), specific customer type (growing companies), specific outcome (delight without headcount). Flywheel: resist "for marketing leaders"; be specific: "for product-led B2B companies that ship fast and need to test positioning/messaging in parallel with product launches."

## Other notable threads

- **Buyer psychology**: Buyers want to reduce risk. They're not comparing you to all alternatives; they're evaluating risk. Reduce the perceived risk of adoption (cheap pilot, fast onboarding) and you win more deals than competitors with "better" features.
- **Naming**: Naming should be evocative, not descriptive. "Help Scout" evokes helpfulness + expertise. "Marketing Cloud" is descriptive and generic. Flywheel's name embeds the thesis (circular motion, compounding, not linear).
- **Category creation is a market maturity game, not a brand decision**: You can't create a new category just by naming it. Categories emerge when market conditions create demand. Trying to force a new category (e.g., "recruiting optimization platform" when buyers still call it ATS) wastes energy.
- **Conviction in differentiation**: The strongest positioning comes from the CEO/founder's actual belief about why they're different. If the founder doesn't believe it, the pitch falls apart.

## Why this matters for flywheel

April's positioning structure gives flywheel a repeatable template for both external (buyer pitch) and internal (team alignment) communication:

1. **External**: Position flywheel not as "AI agents for marketing" but as "your team's speed and iteration are throttled by process, not capability. Here's how AI changes that equation."

2. **Internal**: Use April's setup/follow-through structure in every meeting. Setup: "our goal is to prove that AI can replace routine work without replacing judgment." Follow-through: "here's this week's proof points."

3. **Differentiation clarity**: Resist the urge to talk about multi-agent systems, Claude, or technical architecture in buyer conversations. Those are enablers. The differentiation is speed + human taste, not technology.
