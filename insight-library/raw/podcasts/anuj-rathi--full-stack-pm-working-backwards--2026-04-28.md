# Anuj Rathi: Full-Stack PM, Working Backwards, India Marketplace Dynamics

## Why this is in the wiki

Anuj is India's most-requested product leader (Lenny's 2026 poll). His frameworks for PM craft—working backwards, the three-PR-FAQ method, the four BB framework (Brilliant Basics/Bread-and-Butter/Big Bets/Breaking Bad), full-stack thinking—are directly applicable to flywheel's positioning and execution. His insight on marketplaces (three-way networks break traditional OKRs; need multi-stakeholder empathy) mirrors the complexity of B2B SaaS with multiple user roles. His "lazy, vain, selfish" user framework for onboarding and his emphasis on "show, don't tell" product narrative are load-bearing for how flywheel should think about non-technical users adopting AI-native workflows.

## Load-bearing insights

### 1. Full-stack product managers own outcomes, not features
Anuj: "Product managers have to own outcomes and not only features." A PM is an influencer across users, engineers, marketing, leadership, competition. Owning only one surface (e.g., the UI spec) guarantees failure. Successful PMs in marketplaces, platforms, and complex B2B tools must have partnerships with or mastery of external positioning, go-to-market, comp analysis, and infrastructure. This applies to flywheel: the team members (content strategist, designers, video editor, SEO writer) must each own a full-stack slice—not handoff to a downstream "execution" layer.

### 2. Working backwards is a full-company machine, not just a press release
Amazon's press release is the visible artifact, but the real value is the "GTM machinery" you're building backwards from. Anuj: "You're already thinking about… routes that we will explore that finally we have zeroed in on... money we'll spend, alternatives..." This applies to flywheel's design: every campaign, every piece of content, every measurement should trace back to the outcome you're shipping (not just the feature). Example: 10DLC form help docs → less support tickets → faster time-to-value → higher retention. That full chain is the working-backwards model.

### 3. Three divergent PR FAQs (not one) are a leadership tool for discovery
Anuj: "I really truly believe in the power of three... three press releases, alternative and divergent." When you show one roadmap, stakeholders feel unheard. When you show three well-thought divergent paths, they see their input incorporated but trade-off-analyzed. Applies to flywheel: when proposing content strategy, show (1) performance-marketing approach, (2) narrative-first approach, (3) hybrid. Let Deepan and the CXOs choose—but you've forced the thinking.

### 4. "Lazy, vain, selfish" user model for onboarding is the unlock
Anuj cites Scott Belsky's insight: new users are lazy (blow my mind), vain (don't ask me to change habits), selfish (show me what's in it for me). Most products lose here because PMs are too close to the product. Flywheel: when designing onboarding for non-technical marketers (the lazy, vain, selfish user), the first 10 seconds must reframe the task as "use AI to write better copy faster" not "connect agent to your CDP." The entire first experience is a game of empathy, not feature showcase.

### 5. Show, don't tell: narrative-walk every user journey in concrete detail
Anuj: "We'll talk about personas, but we try to go to… 'Lenny, 30 years old… his relationship with this category is X…' We literally start from there. Each pixel, each copy, each word is in service of that part." Flywheel application: when rolling out a new content workflow or AI feature, the team should map out the full 30-minute journey (what triggered the user? what is their emotional state? what happens in the interim? what's the resolution?). This forces out bad assumptions and catches where the UX / copy / UI fail the lazy/vain/selfish user.

### 6. Four BB framework: Brilliant Basics / Bread-and-Butter / Big Bets / Breaking Bad
Anuj: "Brilliant Basics… you cannot brand as tech debt... These are important, company's built on that... Bread and Butter is your backlog... Big Bets are larger ideas... Breaking Bad is where you redefine your company." Most teams confuse these buckets, so everything feels equally urgent. For flywheel: (1) Brilliant Basics = reliable measurement, data pipelines, canonical content assets; (2) Bread-and-Butter = iteration on existing campaigns, content scaling; (3) Big Bets = new vertical playbook, new demand-gen partnership; (4) Breaking Bad = shift from performance-marketing to narrative-first (only if this is the existential move). Leadership clarity on the bucket mix is the unlock.

### 7. Marketplace complexity breaks traditional OKRs; multi-stakeholder empathy is required
Anuj (on Swiggy's three-way market): "OKRs will not work. The fundamental assumption is solving for one kind of user, divide and conquer. But three different kinds of users means all goals are in conflict." The lever you pull on supply always affects demand and pricing. A/B tests don't work (network effects). Anuj's solution: Big Bets instead of OKRs, with explicit multi-stakeholder FAQs. Flywheel parallel: if the product team, marketing team, and sales team are all pulling different levers (conversion, engagement, retention), you need to stop optimizing each in isolation and use the Big Bet / show-don't-tell frameworks to align on the actual customer journey.

## Other notable threads

- **India product context**: 1.4B people, diverse languages, low per-capita income (~$2.5K vs US $30K), but high engagement + network effects. Products must think "which person pays me?" not "how many people use me?" This mindset applies to B2B SaaS: not all users are revenue-generating; focus on the high-intent segment.
- **Influencer framing of PM**: "We are in the business of influence... full-stack influencers." This reframes product work from "build features" to "move people's behavior across internal (team, exec) and external (user) domains."
- **Interview question for PMs**: "Which products prioritize speed vs. excellence?" Good answers show market understanding, competitive assessment, and risk modeling. Bad answers: "just ship and learn" (misses viability). This is a screen for full-stack thinking.
- **Contrarian on speed vs. excellence**: "Most experiments should be thought experiments... If you have smart people who do metathinking, many experiments wouldn't even be tried." This is the opposite of "move fast and break things"—it's "think well and avoid waste."
- **Book recommendations**: Working Backwards, How Brands Grow (Byron Sharp), The Luxury Strategy. The third teaches about aspirational psychology and social hierarchies shaping product perception.

## Why this matters for flywheel

Anuj's frameworks answer three core flywheel questions:

1. **How to design for non-technical users**: The lazy/vain/selfish framework + show-don't-tell narrative walk ensures that when Flywheel onboards non-technical marketers, every pixel, copy choice, and workflow is in service of their real need (faster, better copy), not the technical substrate.

2. **How to avoid feature-bleed and align teams**: The four BB framework forces clarity on what's debt (measurement, data), what's routine (campaign iteration), what's a big bet (new vertical playbook), and what's existential (narrative-first vs. performance-first). This prevents team sprawl and keeps the core mission visible.

3. **How to work with multi-stakeholder complexity**: Anuj's marketplace insight (three-way networks break OKRs) maps directly to flywheel's three-way tension: content quality (human taste), campaign performance (metrics), and AI efficiency (agent capability). Instead of optimizing each independently, use Big Bets + three divergent strategies to align all three around a clear outcome.
