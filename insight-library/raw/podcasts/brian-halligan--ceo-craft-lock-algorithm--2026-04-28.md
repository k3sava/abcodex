# Brian Halligan — CEO craft

## Why this is in the wiki
Brian ran HubSpot ~20 years, now coaches Sequoia portfolio CEOs. The conversation is mostly hiring/team-building craft, with a strong distribution-as-moat throughline. Useful for the JustCall PMM team's hiring conversations and for the flywheel pitch frame around distribution.

Transcript at `lenny-dropbox/Brian Halligan.txt`.

## 10 load-bearing insights for flywheel

1. **Hire the spiky, not the un-weakness.** HubSpot used to hire candidates where everyone scored 3/4 (no major weakness). They switched to candidates with 4s and 2s (real strengths, real weaknesses). "Hit rate improved." Direct read for PMM team: when scoring teammate or hire candidates, weight peaks not averages.

2. **Hire slow, fire fast — most do the opposite.** "At least 50% of the time, 18 months after you hire a C-level exec, they're gone." High mortality. Don't compound delay. For Kesava in PMM team rebuilds: when a hire isn't working at 6 months, don't drag to 12.

3. **Big-company-fancy-resume hires fail almost 100% of the time at startup scale.** "Avoid the big company hire. Salesforce, Google, Microsoft, like 100% attrition rate on all those folks." Impedance mismatch — they expect infrastructure that doesn't exist. JustCall analog: hiring senior PMMs from JPM-scale orgs into an evolving team hits this exact wall.

4. **Blind references with sharp questions.** "Top 1% of your employees? Top 10? Would you enthusiastically rehire? On a 1-10, how likely are you to try to rehire them down the road?" Generic strengths-and-weaknesses asks signal "I've already decided." For Kesava when reference-checking exit candidates: use these phrasings verbatim.

5. **The LOCK + S algorithm.** Brian's CEO evaluation rubric:
   - **L** — Lovable (would a 28-year-old version of you crawl across glass to follow this person?)
   - **O** — Obsessed (deep founder-market fit, evidence of going down rabbit holes)
   - **C** — Chip on the shoulder
   - **K** — Knowledge of the domain
   - **S** — Student (learn-it-all, not just know-it-all; goes far back in history)
   The S maps directly onto Kesava's profile and is the differentiator under-rated in most PMM rubrics.

6. **The five-tool CEO is new.** "It used to be very rare to have a CEO who could code, have taste, have vision, sell the product, convince employees. Brett Taylor is one." AI lowers the gap on coding and one or two other skills; the rare profile becomes more common. Implication for the next decade of operators Kesava will pitch to: more five-tool founders, fewer specialist CEOs.

7. **"Starting a company has never been easier; scaling one has never been harder."** Quote from Brian's MIT syllabus. Easy starts compound noise. The new moat is distribution. Echoes Evan Spiegel and Jessica Fain from the same week — three independent operators converging on the same thesis. For flywheel: distribution-as-moat is the default external pitch frame; every bet maps to "what new distribution does this unlock?"

8. **Peer rooms ("kids' table" / "adults' table").** Brian splits his Sequoia CEO cohort by company size (under-100 vs over-100). "Misery loves company. They learn from each other; my job is to weigh in occasionally." Direct parallel to commons-rooms architecture — the value isn't broadcast, it's peer-room. Validates the rooms feature.

9. **Sales as the last job AI replaces.** Brian's tweet thesis. Sales is judgment, trust, presence — least automatable. Marketing analog: positioning, taste, narrative judgment are the last marketing tasks to automate. Tactical execution (LP variants, copy review, vertical playbook compile) goes first. This shapes the flywheel's automation order.

10. **CEO-coach-table is misery-loves-company.** When asked what helps CEOs build hard skills (giving feedback, BS detection, layering people), Brian's answer is peer rooms. Not coaching, not books — peers under the same pressure. For Kesava's exit network: rebuild a peer room of 5-7 PMM leaders going through similar ramps.

## Other notable threads

- **Build like the 2004 Red Sox.** Mostly homegrown talent + a few big-name free agents. People underrate homegrown. Marketing version: Aditi/Aashna/Akhil are the homegrown spine; bring in 1-2 senior IC marketers as "Pedro Martinez/Curt Schilling" rather than 5.
- **Parker Conrad's NDA-then-board-deck interview hack.** Sign NDA, send last board memo, half-hour chat. If they're complimentary, red flag — wanted-yes-people detector. Marketing analog: send a bad LP and ask for honest critique; if they polish your ego, fail.
- **People dramatically overrate their interview gut.** Most CEOs after 150 years still do. Calibration humility.
- **Brian Chesky's "founder mode" pushes against homegrown delegation orthodoxy.** Brian thinks Chesky is mostly right, but most CEOs aren't following it. Worth tracking — divergence between elite practice and rank-and-file practice.
- **Five new traits CEOs have to learn that don't come naturally:** giving feedback (positive and negative) constantly; BS detector; inspiration as a skill; layering people without breaking them; running peer cohorts. All four directly transferable to Kesava's leadership of the JustCall PMM team and to the post-exit clearPMM consulting work.

## Why this matters for flywheel

Three loads. First, the LOCK+S rubric is a clean evaluation frame for clearPMM client-fit and for Kesava's own self-positioning in exit pitches. Use it as a filter on the consulting-pipeline candidates.

Second, the distribution-as-moat thesis converges across three episodes this week (Spiegel, Fain, Halligan). That convergence is itself the signal — when three independent operators land on the same frame, the next CXO-facing flywheel deck should lead with "distribution is the new moat in marketing too" instead of "AI-native team."

Third, the peer-rooms model validates commons rooms architecture. Use Halligan's framing in the next external write-up of commons rooms: "kids' table and adults' table" is more legible than "scoped real-time channels."
