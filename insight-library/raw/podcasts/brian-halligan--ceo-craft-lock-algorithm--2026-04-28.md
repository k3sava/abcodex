# Brian Halligan — CEO craft

## Summary
Brian ran HubSpot ~20 years, now coaches Sequoia portfolio CEOs. Hiring/team-building craft, with a strong distribution-as-moat throughline.

## Full text source
Transcript at `lenny-dropbox/Brian Halligan.txt`.

## 10 load-bearing insights

1. **Hire the spiky, not the un-weakness.** HubSpot used to hire candidates where everyone scored 3/4 (no major weakness). They switched to candidates with 4s and 2s (real strengths, real weaknesses). "Hit rate improved." Weight peaks not averages.

2. **Hire slow, fire fast — most do the opposite.** "At least 50% of the time, 18 months after you hire a C-level exec, they're gone." High mortality. Don't compound delay.

3. **Big-company-fancy-resume hires fail almost 100% of the time at startup scale.** "Avoid the big company hire. Salesforce, Google, Microsoft, like 100% attrition rate on all those folks." Impedance mismatch — they expect infrastructure that doesn't exist.

4. **Blind references with sharp questions.** "Top 1% of your employees? Top 10? Would you enthusiastically rehire? On a 1-10, how likely are you to try to rehire them down the road?" Generic strengths-and-weaknesses asks signal "I've already decided."

5. **The LOCK + S algorithm.** Brian's CEO evaluation rubric:
   - **L** — Lovable (would a 28-year-old version of you crawl across glass to follow this person?)
   - **O** — Obsessed (deep founder-market fit, evidence of going down rabbit holes)
   - **C** — Chip on the shoulder
   - **K** — Knowledge of the domain
   - **S** — Student (learn-it-all, not just know-it-all; goes far back in history)

6. **The five-tool CEO is new.** "It used to be very rare to have a CEO who could code, have taste, have vision, sell the product, convince employees. Brett Taylor is one." AI lowers the gap on coding and one or two other skills; the rare profile becomes more common.

7. **"Starting a company has never been easier; scaling one has never been harder."** Quote from Brian's MIT syllabus. Easy starts compound noise. The new moat is distribution. Echoes Evan Spiegel and Jessica Fain from the same week — three independent operators converging on the same thesis.

8. **Peer rooms ("kids' table" / "adults' table").** Brian splits his Sequoia CEO cohort by company size (under-100 vs over-100). "Misery loves company. They learn from each other; my job is to weigh in occasionally."

9. **Sales as the last job AI replaces.** Brian's tweet thesis. Sales is judgment, trust, presence — least automatable. Marketing analog: positioning, taste, narrative judgment are the last marketing tasks to automate. Tactical execution (LP variants, copy review, vertical playbook compile) goes first.

10. **CEO-coach-table is misery-loves-company.** When asked what helps CEOs build hard skills (giving feedback, BS detection, layering people), Brian's answer is peer rooms. Not coaching, not books — peers under the same pressure.

## Other notable threads

- **Build like the 2004 Red Sox.** Mostly homegrown talent + a few big-name free agents. People underrate homegrown.
- **Parker Conrad's NDA-then-board-deck interview hack.** Sign NDA, send last board memo, half-hour chat. If they're complimentary, red flag — wanted-yes-people detector.
- **People dramatically overrate their interview gut.** Most CEOs after 150 years still do. Calibration humility.
- **Brian Chesky's "founder mode" pushes against homegrown delegation orthodoxy.** Brian thinks Chesky is mostly right, but most CEOs aren't following it. Worth tracking — divergence between elite practice and rank-and-file practice.
- **Five new traits CEOs have to learn that don't come naturally:** giving feedback (positive and negative) constantly; BS detector; inspiration as a skill; layering people without breaking them; running peer cohorts.
