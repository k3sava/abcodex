# Buyers navigating rapid market change rely on a vendor's market POV to validate their purchase; without one, reps lose the sale before the demo ends

## Claim
In markets disrupted by AI, buyers draw on an average of seven information sources but rely on vendor reps as their most important validation anchor. Vendors who articulate a clear point of view on the market's future give buyers the narrative they need to justify the decision internally and reduce the fear of backing the wrong future.

## Mechanism
Buyers face two distinct problems when markets change quickly: information overload from many competing sources, and anxiety about committing to a product that may be obsolete in two years. Product demos answer the first problem but not the second. A market POV answers the second problem by telling buyers what the vendor believes the market will look like in three to five years, and explaining why the vendor's product choices follow directly from that belief. Buyers can then evaluate whether the vendor's view of the future matches their own instinct, rather than worrying that the product they buy today will be wrong. Gartner data confirms that reps remain the most important information source at every stage of the buying journey, so the POV must be deliverable through a rep conversation, not just on a marketing page.

## Conditions
Holds when: the market is in genuine transition with multiple possible futures; buyers face real uncertainty about which direction will win.
Fails when: the market has stabilized and buyers already have their own clear view; in settled markets, product differentiation matters more than market narrative. Also fails when the POV is indistinguishable from a competitor's, which makes differentiation worse rather than better.

## Evidence
Dunford cites Gartner Group research directly:

> "Reps remain the most important information source when buyers are researching a business problem or need, identifying a preferred supplier and securing internal support, and finalizing the purchase."

Buyers use an average of seven information sources during a purchase process; fewer than half list AI as one of them. Dunford points to OpenAI, Anthropic, Microsoft, and ServiceNow as examples of companies whose market POVs connect to their distinct competitive positions.

## Signals
- Prospects ask reps what the vendor thinks about the market's future, not just what the product does.
- Win rates improve when reps lead with market perspective; deals stall when they lead with features.
- Buyers say things like "I agree with your view on where this is going" in win interviews.
- Deal velocity increases because buyers have an internal justification story ready before procurement asks.

## Counter-evidence
A market POV is not a product vision. Companies that conflate the two create roadmap anxiety: buyers hear "we believe the future is X" and immediately wonder whether the product will still fit if the future turns out differently. Dunford distinguishes the two explicitly: product vision describes future capabilities; market POV describes current beliefs about where the market is heading. Some buyers in highly technical markets, particularly those who build their own internal evaluations, prefer capability demonstrations over market narratives and discount POV-first conversations as sales talk.
