# Novelty-driven AI revenue mimics genuine PMF in the first 60 days and collapses at months 3 to 6

## Claim
Many AI startups generate "vibe revenue," purchases driven by novelty and curiosity rather than by solved problems. Vibe revenue is statistically indistinguishable from product-market fit in the first 60 days and diverges sharply between months 3 and 6 as the novelty effect fades.

## Mechanism
Novelty triggers the same behavioral signals as genuine PMF: high initial conversion, strong first-month retention, and compounding referrals from people sharing the new experience. The signals are indistinguishable in the first two months because the underlying behavioral mechanism is similar. Customers who sign up for a novel experience repeat the experience while it still feels new, generating retention and referral data that looks exactly like a retained cohort.

The divergence occurs because the underlying reason for staying is different. PMF customers stay because the product is embedded in a repeating workflow; they would need to change how they work to leave. Vibe customers stay until the novelty is consumed, then churn because no workflow dependency formed. The divergence point is months 3-6, precisely when most AI startups report their first significant churn spike. Isenberg argues the coming shakeout will be defined by which companies had PMF versus vibe revenue, not by who had the best demo or the fastest initial growth.

## Conditions
Holds when: the product's primary value proposition in the first session is the experience of the AI itself rather than a workflow outcome; the user's first use case is exploration rather than a recurring task; there is no meaningful switching cost once the user stops using the product.

Fails when: the product integrates into a repeating operational workflow from day one; value compounds over time through personalization, data accumulation, or team-level coordination; or the product is purchased by teams rather than individuals, since organizational switching costs create retention that individual novelty does not.

## Evidence
Isenberg developed this framework by observing first-cohort churn patterns across AI startups and noting that identical early growth metrics predicted sharply different 6-month retention outcomes. The diagnostic signal he identifies: usage intensity at months 3-6. Vibe revenue cohorts show strong month-1 engagement that decays to near-zero by month 5. PMF cohorts show moderate month-1 engagement that stabilizes or grows by month 5 as the product becomes embedded in workflow.

His framing on referrals: vibe revenue generates enthusiasm-driven referrals ("you have to try this") while PMF generates efficiency-driven referrals ("this saves me two hours a week"). The first creates spikes; the second creates compounding.

## Signals
- Month-3 cohort retention below 20% despite strong month-1 retention above 50%
- Power users who engage daily are still not integrating the product into repeating workflows
- Referrals are enthusiasm-driven rather than efficiency-driven
- Churn reason is "I just don't use it anymore" with no triggering event or alternative product

## Counter-evidence
Some products convert vibe-revenue customers into genuine PMF over time, especially when exploratory first use reveals a workflow fit the user did not anticipate. The 60-day versus months-3-6 timing is a general pattern, not a hard rule; products in slower-paced workflows (quarterly planning, annual reviews) may show longer timelines before the novelty-vs-PMF divergence appears. Several early-stage AI companies argue that vibe revenue is a legitimate first stage on the adoption S-curve, transitioning to genuine stickiness as product maturity increases and more workflow integrations become available. The framework may also overindex on individual consumer behavior; team and enterprise products have different novelty decay curves.
