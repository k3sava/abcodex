# Wistia raised $17.3M in debt to buy out their investors and stay independent

## Claim
Chris Savage and Brendan Schwartz turned down an acquisition offer in 2017 and instead raised $17.3M in debt from Accel-KKR in November of that year, paying out early investors and employees so the founders could regain full control of Wistia. The buyout converted preferred shares to common, replaced stock options with profit-sharing, and the company returned to profitability with revenue up 40% year-over-year in the first quarter post-buyout.

## Mechanism
Selling the company would have ended the founders' ability to set direction. Taking debt instead bought out the investor incentive structure (preferred shares, exit timeline) without surrendering operating control. The trade is high-risk in the short term (debt service against cashflow) and high-control in the long term (no board pushing for sale). The discipline that follows: rein in advertising, slow hiring, return to profitability, fund growth from operating cashflow.

## Conditions
Holds when the company is profitable enough to service debt without compromising operations, and when the founders are willing to accept personal-financial concentration risk in exchange for control.
Fails when the company is pre-profit or in a category where venture-subsidized competitors can outspend on distribution.

## Signals
- Cap table simplified to founder-and-employee common stock.
- Profit-sharing replaces equity-based compensation.
- Company publicly states its independence is durable, not transitional.

## Counter-evidence
Debt financing concentrates personal-financial risk on the founder. If the company's category collapses (network-effect competitor, technology shift), debt service obligations stay while revenue evaporates. Wistia's choice is reproducible only for companies that are already profitable and operating in stable categories.
