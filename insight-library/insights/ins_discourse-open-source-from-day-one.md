# Discourse shipped open-source from day one in 2013 and now powers 22,000+ communities including OpenAI and Mozilla

## Claim
Jeff Atwood, Robin Ward, and Sam Saffron founded Discourse in 2013 as open-source forum software released under GPL v2 from inception ("there is only one version of Discourse, the awesome open source version"). The platform has grown to power 22,000+ communities across 23 countries and is used by OpenAI, Mozilla, NVIDIA, Asana, Zoom, GitLab, and Atlassian, with 950+ contributors and 36,000+ pull requests on GitHub.

## Mechanism
Open-source from day one is a credibility commitment that proprietary forks cannot replicate later. The single-version posture (no enterprise edition, no premium-feature gate) means every customer gets the same software the community contributes to, which compounds trust. Atwood's prior credibility from Stack Overflow gave Discourse distribution velocity that pure-bootstrap projects often lack; the lesson generalizes only for founders with relevant public reputation.

## Conditions
Holds when the founders accept that their product will be cloneable and self-hostable, and that revenue will come from hosting and services rather than license control.
Fails when the category requires patent or copyright moat (consumer apps, regulated software).

## Signals
- Single open-source version, no enterprise fork.
- Hosting and services as the revenue model.
- Founders still operating 12 years in.

## Counter-evidence
Open-source from day one requires a category where self-hosting is operationally viable for a non-trivial fraction of buyers. In categories with heavy regulatory or compliance overhead, hosted-only models work better. Atwood's Stack Overflow reputation also reduced distribution friction in ways that aren't reproducible for first-time founders.
