# Harvest grew from agency revenue to 70,000+ customers and $64B+ invoiced, no outside capital across 18+ years

## Claim
Danny Wen and Shawn Liu launched Harvest in 2006 in New York City to solve the time-tracking and invoicing problem in their own consulting firm Iridesco. The agency revenue funded the SaaS build, and Harvest has grown 100% bootstrapped since: 70,000+ customers, 4.5+ billion hours tracked, $64+ billion invoiced through the platform.

## Mechanism
A consulting firm that builds SaaS for its own pain point gets a verified problem statement before the first paid customer and a runway that doesn't depend on outside funding. The agency cashflow funds the engineering for the product; the product, once shipped, eventually outgrows the agency. The transition is gradual rather than binary, which preserves the team's calm-company posture across the shift.

## Conditions
Holds when the agency's pain point is general enough that other businesses share it (so the SaaS has TAM beyond the founders).
Fails when the agency revenue is too thin to fund both client work and the SaaS build, or when the SaaS competes with the agency's own clients.

## Signals
- Founders still recognized as operators 18 years in.
- No outside capital on the cap table.
- Consistent pricing model (subscription) since launch.

## Counter-evidence
Agency-funded SaaS bootstraps slow the company's velocity in the first 2-3 years compared to venture-funded peers. Harvest's category (time tracking) consolidated with venture-backed competitors (Toggl notwithstanding). The pattern works less well in categories where speed-to-market dominates.
