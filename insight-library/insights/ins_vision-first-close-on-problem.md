# When AI changes product, competition, and market simultaneously, position with a future vision first and present value second

## Claim
AI is forcing simultaneous repositioning across three dimensions at once: what the product can do, who the competitors are, and what the market expects. Positioning that leads with a clear hypothesis about how the industry will look in two to three years outperforms positioning built on current features, because confused and wary buyers need directional leadership from vendors, not a feature comparison.

## Mechanism
Normal repositioning triggers are isolated: a product update, a new competitor, a market shift. Repositioning against one variable is addressable with targeted messaging. AI is triggering all three simultaneously, and the rate of change is high enough that any positioning anchored to current features will be obsolete before the sales cycle closes. Dunford's mechanism: buyers in this environment are not evaluating features; they are evaluating confidence. A vendor with a clear, specific hypothesis about the future signals that they understand the disruption and are building toward it. That confidence reduces buyer confusion, which is the primary loss driver in an ambiguous market. Closing on current problem-solution value then gives the buyer the near-term justification to sign.

## Conditions
Holds when: the market is actively confused about where AI is taking the category, and at least some competitors are vague or silent on the future.
Fails when: the category is stable and buyers are comparing features on a shared rubric.

## Evidence
Dunford observed positioning failures accelerating as AI made traditional competitive ladders unreliable. Her framing:

> "Positioning based on a strong hypothesis of how the future will look wins in this market."

> "Customers are confused and wary. What they want from vendors is a vision of the future."

The structural advice she gives:

> "Lead with the vision, but close on the problem."

Her detection mechanism: watch win/loss patterns. If you're losing to "no decision" or to "we're not sure what to evaluate," that's a vision gap, not a features gap.

## Signals
- Sales is describing lost deals as "they weren't sure which direction to go" rather than "a competitor beat us on feature X."
- Marketing is updating the homepage every quarter to keep pace with competitor AI announcements.
- Customer advisory board feedback keeps returning to "what's your roadmap" before product questions.

## Counter-evidence
Vision-led positioning can fail when the vision is generic ("AI will transform everything"). Dunford is specific that the vision must be a hypothesis, grounded in a real point of view, not a commitment to a particular timeline. Premature vision can also mislead buyers if the product doesn't currently solve their immediate problem.
