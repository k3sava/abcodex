# Ghost is structured as a non-profit foundation that legally cannot be bought or sold

## Claim
John O'Nolan and Hannah Wolfe founded Ghost in April 2013 as a non-profit foundation, with a legal constitution that ensures the company can never be bought or sold and that 100% of revenue is reinvested into product and community. The structural choice predates the modern calm-company conversation and treats permanence as a legal artifact, not a personal commitment. Ghost has since reached 100M+ installs, $10.47M ARR, and a 33-person team across 5 continents.

## Mechanism
Founders who say "we'll never sell" can change their minds. A legal constitution that prohibits sale removes the option entirely. The structure also changes hiring incentives (no equity-based wealth event waiting at the end), which self-selects for contributors who value mission over exit. Open-source core plus paid hosting (Ghost(Pro)) creates a virtuous cycle: more usage drives more demand for hosting, which funds more product investment, which drives more usage.

## Conditions
Holds when the product can sustain itself on operating revenue (open-source core + paid hosting model) without venture funding.
Fails when capital-intensive distribution is required to compete; the non-profit structure can't absorb a deficit-funded growth phase.

## Counter-evidence
Non-profit structures restrict the founders' ability to take on outside investment if the category demands it. Ghost's choice locks in the calm-company posture but also forecloses the option of accepting capital to compete in a venture-subsidized fight. The structure is reproducible only for products with self-sustaining unit economics from early days.
