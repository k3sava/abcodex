# The bottleneck for AI adoption has moved from model capability to organizational capability

## Claim
Companies fail to capture AI value not because models are inadequate, but because organizational structures and leadership fluency have not kept pace with what the models can now do.

## Mechanism
Shipper observes that model capability is broadly accessible. The gap is not access; it is organizational capacity to act on that access. Leaders who have not personally used AI tools cannot identify real opportunities or challenges in their own organization because they are guessing rather than diagnosing. Without hands-on fluency at the top, AI strategy gets delegated to people who are also guessing. The five-step sequence he describes forces leadership to get hands-dirty first, then designate champions with protected time, then pick one narrow painful workflow before attempting scale. Each step is designed to build organizational capability before expanding scope.

## Conditions
Holds when: the organization has above-average AI access but below-average AI financial impact. McKinsey data cited by Shipper suggests this describes the vast majority of large enterprises.

Fails when: the bottleneck is genuinely tooling or data access. In some industries (healthcare, defense, finance), regulatory constraints and data access barriers outweigh organizational readiness as the binding constraint.

## Evidence
Shipper draws on observations from working with hedge funds, private equity firms, and Fortune 500 executives:

> "The bottleneck for AI adoption has moved from model capability to organizational capability."

He cites McKinsey data showing only 6% of surveyed organizations achieve AI "high performer" status with meaningful financial impact. He notes that "leaders who haven't gotten their hands dirty don't clearly understand the practical opportunities and challenges of AI." The five steps: get fluent, assign AI champions with protected time and mandate, pick one painful workflow, build automation to 95% reliability with evaluation gates, then scale what works.

## Signals
- A company has multiple AI tool subscriptions but no workflow that has measurably changed output metrics.
- Leaders discuss AI strategy primarily through vendor comparisons or budget debates, not through demonstrated personal use.
- AI champions exist but lack protected time and a clear mandate.

## Counter-evidence
Organizations with significant data access constraints report that tooling and structural integration precede adoption readiness. For those teams, organizational fluency is not the bottleneck; data pipelines and regulatory clearance are. Shipper's five-step model assumes data and access problems are already resolved.
