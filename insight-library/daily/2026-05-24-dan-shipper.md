Every grew from 4 to 30 humans since GPT-3 while automating everything they could automate. Dan Shipper's May 21 report maps the structural reason: AI makes expert competence cheap, which drives adoption up, which floods the market with generic output, which makes the expert who can turn drafts into production-ready work more valuable, not less. The counterintuitive loop is: more automation, more demand for expert judgment. For consulting, this is a sharp response to the "AI will replace consultants" fear. The argument isn't that AI doesn't change things. It's that commoditized output raises the floor for what "good" means, and someone still has to know the difference. For ab-codex, Shipper's framing reinforces why operator-attributed insights hold. The insight that traces back to a named human who shipped something is what distinguishes signal from AI slop.
