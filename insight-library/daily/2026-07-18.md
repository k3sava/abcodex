# What landed today

Three new cards from two operators. Boris Cherny published a five-step AI adoption maturity framework on July 16 that was not captured in the previous daily. Jarred Sumner's July 8 Bun-in-Rust essay produced two cards, both grounded in techniques developed during an 11-day migration of 535,000 lines of Zig to Rust using 64 concurrent Claude agents.

## Theme 1, AI adoption stalls at guardrails and process, not model limits

Boris Cherny's July 16 LinkedIn post (`ins_cherny-adoption-maturity-bottlenecks`) names five adoption maturity levels by agent count: Gated at 0, Assisted at roughly 1, Parallel at roughly 10, Supervised autonomy at roughly 100, AI-native at 1,000 or more. The claim is that each transition is blocked by a different organizational constraint, not by model capability. At Gated, the constraint is a security policy with no approval path. At Assisted, the engineer's own attention. At Parallel, managing concurrent output streams. At Supervised autonomy, trust and decision throughput. At AI-native, designing per-task guardrails at scale.

Cherny observes that he talks to engineers daily and sees the same pattern across companies: one person achieves 10x output while the rest of the organization has not caught up. The framework is a diagnostic for that gap. Anthropic itself operates at Step 3 as of July 2026, and Cherny reports reaching Step 4 personally. The implication is direct: buying more tokens or waiting for a more capable model does not move an organization forward when the constraint is an approval gate or a manual review queue.

- Boris Cherny, `ins_cherny-adoption-maturity-bottlenecks`. A five-step adoption maturity framework showing that the binding constraint at each level is guardrail design and approval process, not model capability.

## Theme 2, Preparation and adversarial review as the two-step quality framework for agent-driven migrations

Jarred Sumner's July 8 essay on rewriting Bun in Rust documented two distinct techniques used during a migration that would otherwise be ungovernable at scale. The first: create pattern-mapping documents before agents begin (`ins_sumner-pre-agent-pattern-documents`). Sumner's team wrote PORTING.md and LIFETIMES.tsv before code generation started. PORTING.md mapped Zig-specific constructs to Rust equivalents. LIFETIMES.tsv cross-referenced Zig ownership patterns against Rust lifetime annotations. Every one of the 64 concurrent agent instances received these documents as context. Pattern inference happened once, by humans, in advance. Agents applied the mapping rather than re-deriving it, which reduced divergence across 535,000 lines.

The second technique addressed what pattern documents could not prevent (`ins_sumner-adversarial-reviewer-pairing`). Separate reviewer agent instances were instantiated specifically to find failures, not to confirm correctness. The framing shift mattered. A reviewer with an explicit adversarial mandate approaches the code without the implementer's priors and looks for reasons to reject rather than reasons to accept. Three bug categories were caught by reviewer agents that the implementer pass had not surfaced. Together, the two techniques form a two-step quality framework: reduce inference divergence before the run, then catch residual failures adversarially during review.

- Jarred Sumner, `ins_sumner-pre-agent-pattern-documents`. Creating pattern-mapping documents before a large agentic migration gives parallel agents a shared reference, reducing per-file inference errors and producing consistent outputs.
- Jarred Sumner, `ins_sumner-adversarial-reviewer-pairing`. Pairing implementer agents with separate adversarial reviewer agents, instructed to find failures, catches bug categories that single-context review misses.
