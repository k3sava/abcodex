Cutler's May 21 post takes on a problem that sounds administrative but runs deeper: why organizations consistently fail to describe how their teams actually work. His argument is that the formal definition of a team (its charter, its scope, its boundaries) almost never matches the informal reality of how work flows, decisions get made, and exceptions get handled. The gap between the org chart and the actual operating model is where friction lives. This follows his May 16 post (TBM 422) on exception, presence, and delegation patterns in leadership under pressure. Together, the two posts build a frame: formal structure is a story organizations tell themselves, while actual coordination happens through norms, relationships, and workarounds that rarely get written down. For consulting, this is directly relevant to any engagement involving product org design or PMM function setup. Clients often want a clean team definition before they have a stable operating model. Cutler's framing gives a useful way to reframe that request: map how work actually moves first, then define the team structure to match it, not the other way around.
