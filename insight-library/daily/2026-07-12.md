# What landed today

Three new cards from three operators across two publication days. The scan surfaced a quiet convergence: each operator, writing independently about a different layer of the AI stack, arrived at the same structural observation. The model layer is not where lasting value concentrates. Deployment, institutional relationships, and specification discipline are.

## Theme 1, The deployment layer, not the model layer, is where AI moats form

Tom Tunguz's July 7 essay, captured as `ins_tunguz-fde-deployment-moat`, documents the largest single-year AI-adjacent labor commitment on record: $9.75 billion deployed by five companies in twelve months to build forward-deployed engineering (FDE) teams. The investment thesis is structural. GPT-4, Claude, and Gemini are already capable enough. The bottleneck is no longer model intelligence but customer deployment. Enterprises cannot install, configure, and operate frontier AI without embedded engineering support, and once embedded engineers train a team on one vendor's patterns, the switching cost becomes institutional rather than technical.

Three structural FDE models have formed. The Balance Sheet model (Microsoft, Amazon, Salesforce) funds FDE from existing headcount. The Standalone model (OpenAI, Anthropic) creates separate PE-backed entities, with OpenAI's Deployment Company raising $4 billion at a $14 billion post-money valuation. The Partner Ecosystem model (Google Cloud) deploys $750 million to system integrators rather than building direct. The value delivered across all three models is the same: education. Embedded engineers teach customer teams how to configure and operate AI. The lock-in is behavioral, not contractual.

> "The bottleneck shifted from model capability to deployment. GPT-4, Claude, & Gemini are powerful enough."

> "The switching cost is institutional, not technical. And there's $10b behind it."

- Tomasz Tunguz, `ins_tunguz-fde-deployment-moat`. The AI deployment gap has triggered a $9.75 billion forward-deployed engineering investment wave; the moat built is institutional and behavioral, not technical.

## Theme 2, The model layer itself will commoditize

Benedict Evans' July 9 essay, captured as `ins_evans-token-pricing-commoditization`, maps the structural dynamics behind model pricing. The current supply crunch looks like pricing power. Evans argues it is not. Multiple frontier models share similar training regimes and data sources. Trillion-dollar capacity additions are in progress. Per-token efficiency improves each generation. Inference carries 40-50% gross margins today, but those numbers exclude training costs that still exceed revenue.

Evans draws two historical analogies. TSMC generates $53 billion in net income, less than Apple alone, despite being the indispensable manufacturing layer beneath every iPhone. Cellular carriers built a trillion dollars of infrastructure over two decades while application-layer companies captured the majority of consumer and enterprise value. Both cases followed the same pattern: necessary infrastructure, compressed margins, value concentrated in what ran on top. Evans sees the same logic at work in AI.

> "We're in a supply crunch, and this is unstable. All of the variables are in play."

> "Every path to foundation models having market dominance...requires something to change."

> "All the value was captured by other people further up the stack."

This is the first insight card from Benedict Evans, a new operator to the library.

- Benedict Evans, `ins_evans-token-pricing-commoditization`. AI foundation model pricing faces structural commoditization pressure; every observable dynamic points away from winner-takes-all moats at the model layer, with value expected to concentrate further up the stack.

## Theme 3, Specification discipline, not prompting tricks, is the skill that transfers

Ethan Mollick's July 7 thread, captured as `ins_mollick-specs-not-tricks`, supplies the practitioner view on what actually works once you accept that the model layer is not where the advantage sits. Four Wharton Prompting Science reports found no significant gains from widely promoted tricks: politeness formulas, threats, expert personas, and chain-of-thought instructions for reasoning-optimized models all failed to produce reliable improvements on GPQA and MMLU-Pro benchmarks. What correlates with better outputs is specification discipline: clear goal statements, defined output formats, explicit quality criteria, and tests for verification. Mollick describes this as identical to what makes any strong contractor productive.

This finding connects to the prior day's `ins_shipper-tend-your-loop`: once reliable AI execution exists, the human job reorganizes around loop design and quality specification. Mollick's Wharton research identifies the specific input discipline that makes that quality specification effective. Tricks decay as models improve. Specifications compound.

- Ethan Mollick, `ins_mollick-specs-not-tricks`. Four Wharton research reports found no reliable gain from prompting tricks on frontier models; specification discipline, setting clear goals, output formats, quality criteria, and verification tests, is what actually transfers.
