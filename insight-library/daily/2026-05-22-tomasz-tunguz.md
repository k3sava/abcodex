Tunguz analyzed the fastest-growing companies in AI and software and found one shared property: they are either selling AI directly or reselling inference. The inference market, he argues, is the largest and fastest-growing in technology today, projected to reach $250 billion within seven years. The framing borrows from calculus: the first derivative of inference is any business built on top of it. That includes tools, workflows, vertical applications, and services that consume model calls as their raw material. The implication for pricing and packaging is direct. If inference is the new distribution layer, then the companies that will win are those that either own inference economics (model providers, cloud hyperscalers) or that add enough domain-specific value on top to justify a margin. Pure resellers with no differentiation will get compressed. For substrate, this is a useful framing for how to think about the architecture's pricing model. For consulting, it is a clean story to use with clients who are asking where AI creates defensible business value rather than temporary cost savings.
