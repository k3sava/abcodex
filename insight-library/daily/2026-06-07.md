# What landed today, 2026-06-07

Four new cards, one new operator. No new synthesis patterns: the convergences below involve two operators at most, which does not clear the three-operator threshold. Three themes emerged from this week's scan: the architecture of local AI routing, the problem of open-model churn making single-provider commitments risky, and the organizational cost of AI adoption when teams have no shared feedback channel.

## Theme 1, Local routing and model-agnostic architecture are now practical defaults

Two posts from Tomasz Tunguz this week describe the same underlying shift from two directions. In `ins_local-ai-router-minimill` (June 5), he documents a seven-day experiment routing AI tasks on a local Mac model. The numbers are specific: 78% of AI work handled locally, task duration cut from 47 to 19 seconds, queue wait from 73 seconds to 4 seconds, a 94% reduction. The mechanism is task classification: easy tasks go to the local model and complete without touching the cloud queue; complex tasks route to a cloud frontier model. Tunguz calls this the minimill pattern. A purpose-specific local processor absorbs the commodity workload, leaving the large model free for demanding runs.

The companion card `ins_open-models-lead-rotates` (June 2) provides the market-level argument for keeping that routing layer model-agnostic. OpenRouter token data shows open-weight models at 69.1% of named token volume as of June 2026, and open-model leadership has already rotated once: DeepSeek's early lead gave way to MiniMax and Kimi models. Tunguz frames the dynamic with Hayek: competition is a discovery procedure. Developers routing freely discover the best current option continuously. An architecture locked to one provider arrives late to each discovery.

Together the two cards describe a cost stack: route easy tasks to local to cut latency and spend; keep the routing layer model-agnostic so the best available model can be swapped in as the market moves.

- Tomasz Tunguz, `ins_local-ai-router-minimill`. Routing ~80% of AI tasks to a local model cuts average task duration from 47 to 19 seconds and queue wait by 94%, measured over seven days on a Mac.
- Tomasz Tunguz, `ins_open-models-lead-rotates`. Open-weight models now hold 69.1% of OpenRouter token volume and leadership rotates fast enough to punish single-provider commitments.

## Theme 2, AI adoption fractures teams when wins and costs flow to different people

Charity Majors, CTO of Honeycomb.io, published `ins_ai-adoption-feedback-loop-gap` on June 2. The argument is structural. Wins from AI-assisted development are public: all-hands meetings, blog posts, conference talks. Costs surface in private channels: on-call rotations, SRE post-mortems, and quiet complaints that do not travel upward. The two groups operate in separate realities with no natural correction mechanism.

> "The wins and costs are happening to two different groups of people. There is no natural feedback loop."

This connects directly to the June 4 card from Ethan Mollick (`ins_cognitive-surrender-agentic-defaults`), which described how frictionless agentic systems reduce critical evaluation of AI outputs at the individual level. Majors describes what happens when that pattern propagates at team scale: the people generating the wins and the people absorbing the debt are different, and no organizational mechanism links them. Her proposed fix is practical: tell complete stories that acknowledge both wins and downstream costs, and convert philosophical disagreements about AI into tractable engineering questions.

- Charity Majors, `ins_ai-adoption-feedback-loop-gap`. AI adoption splits engineering organizations when wins are publicly celebrated and costs accumulate silently in on-call queues with no channel back to the people who caused them.

## Theme 3, Agent execution design, state persistence as the missing capability

Simon Willison published the full technical write-up of his MicroPython-in-WASM sandbox on June 6, adding a mechanism not covered in the June 2 release note. The June 2 card (`ins_wasm-sandbox-agent-code-execution`) addressed security: WASM isolation prevents sandboxed code from reaching the host system. The June 6 card `ins_agent-sandbox-stateful-execution` addresses iterative computation: a queue-based blocking loop keeps the MicroPython interpreter alive between agent tool calls, so variables and functions defined in one call remain in scope for the next.

The design enables multi-step agent analysis workflows where each call builds on the prior state, without restarting the runtime or relaxing WASM isolation. Willison pairs this with wasmtime's fuel mechanism, a configurable per-operation budget that bounds CPU consumption without killing interpreter state. The result is a sandbox that is both safe (no host access) and stateful (persistent Python session across calls). Both properties are required for useful agent code execution; the June 2 card documented one; the June 6 card documents the other.

- Simon Willison, `ins_agent-sandbox-stateful-execution`. A queue-based MicroPython loop maintains Python state across sequential agent tool calls, enabling iterative data analysis without restarting the interpreter or relaxing sandbox isolation.
