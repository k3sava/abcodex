Dan Shipper ran the experiment: automate as much as possible inside Every using Codex and Claude Code, then observe what happens to headcount and total work. Result: more automation meant more work, not less, because every automated loop requires a human to watch, steer, and fix it when it drifts. His framing is sharp. We are moving from a Knowledge Economy -- get paid for knowing things -- to an Allocation Economy, where the productive unit is deciding what gets done, not doing it. The framer, not the frame, is where the value lives. For consulting positioning: this is the reframe that replaces "AI will take my job" with "my job is now the layer AI cannot replace." For ab-codex: it explains why human-attributed insights compound when AI-generated content does not. The operator name and the track record are the allocation signal. For career-ops: Kesava is already practicing allocation -- the question is whether the positioning says so out loud.
