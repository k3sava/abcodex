# What landed today, 2026-06-08

Four new cards from this week's scan. Three come from Tomasz Tunguz's Theory Ventures blog, published June 1 through June 5, and one from Kevin Indig's Growth Memo, also June 1. Both operators are already in the library. No new synthesis patterns: the three Tunguz cards are from a single operator, and no third operator converged on the same claim from the AEO lane.

## Theme 1, AI infrastructure is decentralizing at the compute and model layers

Tomasz Tunguz published three pieces within five days that describe the same structural shift from different angles. `ins_local-ai-minimill-router` (June 5) documents the architecture shift in concrete terms. A two-lane router on a single Mac now handles 78% of Tunguz's AI workload, cutting average task time from 47 seconds to 19 and queue age from 73 seconds to four. The mechanism is direct: the router classifies tasks as easy or hard and runs easy tasks locally. Most knowledge-work tasks are not frontier-hard, so the majority route locally. Cloud spend and queue latency drop sharply without changing the work itself. Tunguz names the pattern after Nucor's steel minimills, which disrupted integrated mills by decentralizing production to smaller, faster units close to demand.

> "A laptop on my desk now handles 78% of my AI work, with the rest sent to the cloud. Throughput jumped about 25%, average task duration fell from 47 seconds to 19, & queue age dropped from 73 seconds to four."

The compute-layer shift mirrors one at the model layer. `ins_open-weight-majority-inference` (June 2) documents that open-weight models now generate 69.1% of named token volume on OpenRouter versus 30.9% for closed models. The enabling mechanism is market competition: each wave of open releases (DeepSeek, then MiniMax and Kimi, then Qwen and Alibaba) sets a new performance floor at lower cost, displacing the prior leader before it can build switching costs. Developers route production traffic to the best available model. No provider holds the position long enough to extract rent.

`ins_ai-shorts-business-model-risk` (June 1) adds the equity market's read on this landscape. Short interest in AI stocks concentrates in companies whose revenue depends on unresolved future assumptions: neoclouds carry 16.8% short interest, developer tools 9.5%, enterprise SaaS 8.9%. Infrastructure with current revenue sits at 1.1% for hyperscalers and 1.2% for NVIDIA. The market is not skeptical about AI technology. It is skeptical about business models that need adoption to outrun burn rate.

> "The skepticism is concentrated in companies whose AI exposure still depends on future capital access, future demand, or future operating leverage."

- Tomasz Tunguz, `ins_local-ai-minimill-router`. A local router routes 78% of AI workloads to on-device execution, cutting latency and cloud cost without changing output quality.
- Tomasz Tunguz, `ins_open-weight-majority-inference`. Open-weight models crossed 69% of developer inference traffic on OpenRouter as competition keeps the performance floor rising and the price floor falling.
- Tomasz Tunguz, `ins_ai-shorts-business-model-risk`. Short interest in AI concentrates in business models dependent on unproven future demand, not in infrastructure with current revenue.

## Theme 2, AI Overviews abolished search intent as a segmentation tool

Kevin Indig's Growth Memo on June 1 documented a behavioral shift that most content teams have not priced into their strategy. `ins_aio-intent-compression` shows that AI Overviews collapse all five search intent types into a single reading behavior. The analysis covers 846,000 anonymized U.S.-based Google search sessions. Without an AIO, SERP retention at 21 seconds ranged from 12% for navigational queries to 32% for local queries, a 20-point spread that content strategists could segment against. With an AIO present, all five intent types cluster at 41.9% to 48.5%. Session length is approximately 4x longer across the board.

The mechanism is that AIOs inject a detailed answer at the top of the SERP. That answer satisfies enough of the initial query that even navigational and transactional searchers begin reading rather than clicking immediately. Two decades of content strategy assumed navigational users would click fast and informational users would linger. AIOs eliminated that behavioral difference in a single feature release.

> "Intent compression breaks the segmentation SEO ran on for 20 years."

- Kevin Indig, `ins_aio-intent-compression`. AIOs flatten SERP retention across all five search intent types, collapsing the behavioral variance that underpinned intent-based content segmentation.
