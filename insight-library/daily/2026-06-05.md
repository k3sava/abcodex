# What landed today, 2026-06-05

Five new cards, two new operators, one new synthesis pattern. Three operators publishing independently within two weeks of each other converged on the same structural argument: AI does not level organizations, it amplifies them. A Tunguz card on open-model market share adds a sharp data point to the infrastructure layer. An April Dunford card updates the positioning playbook for the specific conditions AI has created.

## Theme 1, AI amplifies what was already there

Ethan Mollick published "Co-Existence and the End of Co-Intelligence" on June 4 (`ins_coexistence-era-three-questions`), arguing that the interactive human-and-chatbot era is over. Late 2025 coding agents produced seventeen times more code in study conditions; Anthropic reported 80% AI-written code and eight times more shipped work per developer. When AI is sometimes genuinely better than the person using it, the operative question stops being "how do I use this" and becomes three separate judgment calls: when to refuse AI's help, when to hand over full control, and how to navigate AI as the gatekeeper between your work and its audience.

Charity Majors (Honeycomb CTO, new operator) published the organizational health version of the same argument on June 2 (`ins_ai-amplifies-existing-org-dynamics`). Her framing: AI is an amplifier. High-performing teams with tight feedback loops get faster. Teams without those loops compound errors invisibly. The diagnostic she offers is pointed:

> "What would it take for you to feel comfortable shipping code without reading it?"

The honest answer to that question reveals the current state of the organization's feedback infrastructure before AI makes it consequential.

Dan Shipper (Every CEO, new operator) published the economics version on May 21 (`ins_framer-premium-after-automation`). Shipper runs a 30-person company that automated everything it could automate, and found that automation expanded human work rather than contracting it.

> "AI commoditizes the residue of human expertise...creating demand for what's different."

His framing: models execute within frames humans define. The premium moves to framers, the people in contact with current reality who decide what problems matter this week. These three cards form a new synthesis pattern, `pat_ai-amplifies-existing-dynamics`, which updates `pat_execution-cheap-judgement-scarce` with a seventh operator.

- Charity Majors, `ins_ai-amplifies-existing-org-dynamics`. AI magnifies strengths and dysfunctions alike; asymmetric feedback loops between enthusiasts and skeptics are the organizational failure mode.
- Ethan Mollick, `ins_coexistence-era-three-questions`. The co-existence era makes the question of when to use, refuse, or cede to AI more consequential, not less.
- Dan Shipper, `ins_framer-premium-after-automation`. Full automation at Every revealed that framers who define what problems matter now are in higher demand, not lower, after AI handles execution.

## Theme 2, Open-weight models now hold the developer API majority

Tom Tunguz published data from OpenRouter on June 2 (`ins_open-models-developer-frontier-majority`): open-weight models generated 69.1% of named open-versus-closed token volume on the platform. The leadership rotates continuously among non-US providers including DeepSeek, MiniMax, Kimi, and Qwen, with no single provider holding the lead for long. Tunguz applies Hayek's discovery procedure framing: the competitive routing market on OpenRouter functions as a daily price-performance comparison engine, which drives faster innovation than closed-model dynamics.

The card extends the intelligence-per-dollar argument from `ins_intelligence-per-dollar-model-metric` (June 3): cost-optimized production AI is routing toward open models, not because they're best on every benchmark, but because the competitive market discovers cost-performance frontiers faster than incumbency can.

- Tomasz Tunguz, `ins_open-models-developer-frontier-majority`. Open-weight models now hold 69.1% of developer API token volume; continuous competition on OpenRouter rotates quality leadership and compresses the advantage window of any single provider.

## Theme 3, Vision-first positioning is the answer to AI's triple disruption

April Dunford published "Positioning in the Age of AI" on May 21 (`ins_vision-first-close-on-problem`), identifying an unusual structural condition: AI is changing products, competitors, and market expectations simultaneously. In a normal market, a company repositions against one variable at a time. When all three variables shift together, feature-based positioning is obsolete before the sales cycle closes.

Her prescription is specific:

> "Lead with the vision, but close on the problem."

Buyers in this environment are selecting on confidence, not features. A vendor with a grounded hypothesis about how the industry will look in two to three years reduces buyer confusion, which Dunford identifies as the primary loss driver in the current B2B AI market. Closing on present value gives the buyer near-term justification to sign. The win/loss signal that exposes a vision gap: deals lost to "we're not sure what to evaluate" rather than "a competitor beat us on feature X."

- April Dunford, `ins_vision-first-close-on-problem`. When AI changes product, competition, and market simultaneously, positioning that leads with a future hypothesis outperforms feature positioning because confused buyers need directional confidence from vendors.
