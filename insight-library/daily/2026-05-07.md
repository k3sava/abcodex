# Speed is the wrong variable: launch triage, pricing iteration, and the verification gap

Today's batch clusters around a single uncomfortable finding: speed is the wrong variable to track in AI-native work. Aatir Abdul Rauf found it in launch discipline. The teams winning at AI-native GTM didn't remove launch gates; they made triage faster. Lovable tiered every release by revenue generation potential, user breadth, and value unlocked, then used AI to run that scoring in minutes instead of days. Elena Verna found the same pattern in pricing: Lovable's 40% paid conversion from freemium came from treating pricing as a hypothesis and iterating monthly, not from getting the initial model right. Aleyda Solis found it in AEO: 74% of cited sources rotate weekly, so adding optimization work without a measurement layer underneath produces no actionable signal.

The second thread is friction and its emotional surface. Emily Pick's positioning work at Docebo named something most launch playbooks overlook: when a product changes a workflow users have memorized, the target emotion is relief, not excitement. User anxiety and IT admin anxiety are two separate surfaces. Dissolve each distinctly, and adoption follows. That is a different craft than feature launch.

The third thread is verification. Eugene Yan's production principle and Hamel Husain's eval methodology both say the same thing from different angles: the gap in most AI systems is not generation quality but the absence of a feedback loop that tells you what quality means. Husain's specific finding is the sharpest: error analysis is the step almost every team skips, and it is the one that gives the most leverage per hour invested.
