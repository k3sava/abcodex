Tomasz Tunguz opens with a single prediction: nobody will open Gmail five times a day in five years. His argument is that AI agents will handle email triage, routing, and drafting in the background, surfacing only the handful of messages that genuinely require a human decision. He's not describing a better email client. He's describing email as an interface that dissolves into agent-mediated task flows, with people re-entering the loop only at decision points. The title — six messages that actually matter — puts a number on what remains after agents filter everything else.

For consulting: this is a useful reframe for clients who ask where AI fits in their ops stack. Not "use AI to write emails faster" but "stop designing workflows around email as the interface." For ab-codex: a macro-level signal on how software interfaces change as agents intermediate between people and tools — relevant to how Kesava positions any product that currently relies on email as a communication or conversion layer.
