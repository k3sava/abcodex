Fishkin's May 25 post closes a 25-year loop. Google spent that time telling websites to "just make great content." His argument: that advice is now incomplete and reductive. AI generates content at quality parity. Quality alone no longer signals value to search engines or to AI citation systems. The replacement thesis is "inimitable product" -- something that cannot be duplicated by a model, a competitor, or a template. The piece doesn't fully define what inimitable looks like in practice, but the framing is clean: distinctiveness beats quality, and distinctiveness has to be structural, not stylistic.

For ab-codex: this maps directly to how the library should describe itself in AEO-facing copy. The edge is not content quality. It is operator attribution. Named humans who shipped something, verified, with source traces. An AI cannot generate that. No template produces it. A citation system that rewards verifiable provenance rewards exactly what ab-codex is built around. For consulting: clients building content programs in 2026 need to hear this argument. "Publish more" is not a strategy. "Publish what no one else can publish" is.
