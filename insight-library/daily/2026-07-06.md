# What landed today

Four new cards, no new operators, no synthesis patterns. Two threads across the week. The first is about where AI model reliability is silently degrading even as general capability improves. The second is about how the adoption of agent-grade tools is reshaping both daily knowledge work and SaaS market structure at a pace that most organizations are not tracking.

## Theme 1, Better models can be worse tools in specific harness contexts

Armin Ronacher published "Better Models: Worse Tools" on July 4, 2026, documenting a specific and repeatable regression. `ins_ronacher-harness-overfit-tool-schema` captures the mechanism. Newer Claude models (Opus 4.8 and Sonnet 5) fail against custom strict tool schemas at roughly 20% of invocations, appending fabricated keys to nested parameters the schema forbids. Older Opus 4.5 handled the same schema without adjustment. Codex models showed no regression at all. Ronacher's hypothesis: Claude Code's client silently repairs malformed tool calls during production use, so RLHF reward flows even when the model produces malformed output. Newer models learn to rely on that silent repair layer. When deployed against a stricter external harness without that layer, the malformed patterns cause failures.

> "slightly malformed tool calls can still complete the task and receive reward"

> "alternative tool schemas might not just be unfamiliar. They might be implicitly punished by post-training."

The practical implication is pointed. Developers who adopt newer frontier models without testing against their specific tool schemas may see regressions that benchmark scores do not predict.

Simon Willison published his write-up of the sqlite-utils 4.0rc2 release on July 5. `ins_willison-cross-model-review` captures the complementary practice he has now made standard. After having Claude Fable write most of the release (37 prompts, 34 commits, 30 files modified, roughly $149.25 in API spend), he asked Fable to perform a final review. Fable found five release-blocker bugs the development session had not surfaced, including a transaction flaw in `delete_where()` that caused silent data loss. The second insight is the method itself: Willison now habitually has Anthropic's best model review OpenAI's output and vice versa, because different training distributions catch blind spots that same-provider review reinforces.

> "I've started habitually having Anthropic's best model review OpenAI's work and vice versa, because I've had that turn up interesting results often enough to be valuable."

- Armin Ronacher, `ins_ronacher-harness-overfit-tool-schema`. Newer frontier models trained inside forgiving harnesses develop tool-calling drift that produces 20% failure rates against strict external schemas.
- Simon Willison, `ins_willison-cross-model-review`. Competing AI models reviewing each other's work find category-level bugs that same-provider review would reinforce rather than catch.

## Theme 2, Agent-native work patterns are normalizing and restructuring markets simultaneously

Ethan Mollick's June 30 essay "The Twilight of the Chatbots" contained a figure that has not yet been extracted as a standalone card. `ins_mollick-four-agents-concurrent-norm` captures it. At OpenAI, 25% of workers run four or more AI agents simultaneously every week. This is not a power-user metric. It is an adoption floor at the company that builds the tools. The mechanism is asynchronous delegation: agents accept an upfront brief, work autonomously, and return results as a batch. Supervision is front-loaded (task specification) and back-loaded (output review), not spread across the run. Four agents in parallel costs roughly the same cognitive effort as managing one interactive chatbot conversation.

> "A quarter of OpenAI workers have at least four agents running at one time every week."

While Mollick's other three cards from the same source cover why experts benefit more than novices, why the 14-hour autonomous run is now a practical unit of work, and why institutions cannot adapt at model-capability speeds, this figure is the ground-level usage datum none of the earlier cards contained.

Tomasz Tunguz published "The CIO's Choices are Clear in 2026" on June 30, analyzing 87 public SaaS and platform companies. `ins_tunguz-category-beats-growth` captures the structural finding. Growth rate does not separate winners from losers in the current market. Category does. Infrastructure and Dev Tools are up 68.5% over one year. Security is up 17.6%. Business Applications are down 36.2%. All three groups posted similar revenue growth rates. The mechanism is seat substitution: AI agents handling tasks that formerly required human workers erode the headcount on which seat-licensed SaaS renews. Salesforce's own Agentforce handles approximately 50% of its customer interactions, and Benioff publicly reduced support headcount from 9,000 to 5,000 as a result.

> "I've reduced it from 9,000 heads to about 5,000, because I need less heads."

> "The market buys the AI stack and sells the seat-priced application layer."

- Ethan Mollick, `ins_mollick-four-agents-concurrent-norm`. A quarter of OpenAI workers run four or more simultaneous agents weekly, establishing concurrent multi-agent delegation as a normalized work pattern at the frontier.
- Tomasz Tunguz, `ins_tunguz-category-beats-growth`. Across 87 public SaaS companies, category (infrastructure vs. seat-based) predicts market performance far better than growth rate, because AI agent substitution is eroding seat-count renewal for horizontal business applications.
