# What landed today

Three new cards, one new operator profile. Two themes from separate lanes that converged on a shared question: what does AI actually change about how work gets done, and for whom?

## Theme 1, Adversarial agents running at scale expose what single-instance workflows miss

Jarred Sumner published a detailed account of the Bun Zig-to-Rust rewrite on July 8. `ins_sumner-parallel-adversarial-agent-rewrite` documents the result: 960,000 lines migrated in 11 days at roughly $165,000 in API costs, using 64 simultaneous Claude Code instances across four worktrees with a separate adversarial reviewer agent alongside each implementer. Sumner estimated the same work would have required a team of engineers a year.

The mechanism Sumner describes is precise. Adversarial reviewer agents carry no generation-phase priors. They read the implementer's output with fresh context and a prompt pointing them toward bugs. That decoupling is what caught three classes of errors the implementers missed: a use-after-free in async pipe closure handling, incorrect negative timestamp handling producing invalid timespec values, and eager evaluation in `unwrap_or` for color-mix percentages. The 1.386 million assertion conformance test suite provided a deterministic ground truth at each step, so neither the implementer nor the reviewer could hallucinate a passing result. Peak output: 58 commits in one minute.

> "This Rust rewrite would've taken a team of engineers...a year of work. With 1 engineer using Fable & closely monitoring Claude Code, we went from start to 100% of the test suite passing on all platforms in 11 days."

This extends `pat_verification-as-human-job` (now at 15 convergence operators). The pattern's core claim, that execution is solved and verification is not, shows up here structurally: the adversarial reviewer is the verification layer, and its independence from the implementer is what makes it effective. The conditions Sumner names are the same conditions that make any agentic verification loop hold: a language-agnostic test suite already exists, tasks decompose by file or crate boundary, one human can monitor and redirect without reviewing every commit.

- Jarred Sumner, `ins_sumner-parallel-adversarial-agent-rewrite`. Parallel adversarial reviewer agents running against 64 simultaneous implementer instances compressed a 960K-line language migration into 11 days by decoupling review context from generation context.

## Theme 2, AI is reshaping how work is assessed, not just how work is done

Tom Tunguz published a model worldview benchmark on July 6 that inverts the usual geography-as-values framing. `ins_tunguz-ai-worldview-alignment-divergence` captures the finding: GPT-4o and DeepSeek R1 cluster as near-twins on the value map despite originating in San Francisco and Hangzhou respectively. Gemini 3.1 Flash Lite and Qwen 3.6 Flash sit as neighbors. The alignment choices made during post-training predict model values more reliably than the lab's country of origin, because pretraining on the global English-language web produces a roughly similar prior across labs, and the divergence happens during RLHF and fine-tuning.

> "GPT-4o & DeepSeek R1 are near-twins, one trained in San Francisco, one in Hangzhou"

That finding has a practical implication for anyone selecting models on perceived geopolitical alignment: they may be optimizing for the wrong variable. Post-training alignment choices, not national origin, are where values get encoded.

The companion finding from Lenny Rachitsky and Noam Segal's July 7 survey adds a different register to the same question. `ins_rachitsky-segal-career-recommendation-paradox` documents a split that the headline productivity numbers miss. Fifty-three percent of tech workers would discourage someone from entering the field, producing a net promoter score of negative 39, even as personal career optimism stays relatively stable. One in three personal optimists still would not recommend the field to a newcomer. The NPS by seniority is the sharpest signal: senior and staff ICs score negative 49, VPs score negative 23, founders score negative 5. The more deeply someone has experienced industry conditions rather than its narrative, the less willing they are to send others in.

This is distinct from the companion card `ins_rachitsky-segal-ai-baseline-creep-burnout` captured yesterday. That card documents the mechanism connecting AI productivity to higher baseline expectations. This card captures the decoupling: personal optimism and institutional trust are no longer the same question, and experienced workers have separated them.

- Tom Tunguz, `ins_tunguz-ai-worldview-alignment-divergence`. AI model worldview maps to post-training alignment choices more than lab geography, making GPT-4o and DeepSeek near-twins and Gemini Flash and Qwen neighbors on a value benchmark.
- Lenny Rachitsky and Noam Segal, `ins_rachitsky-segal-career-recommendation-paradox`. Personal career optimism has decoupled from institutional trust in tech, with 53% of workers discouraging newcomers even as they assess their own trajectories positively.
